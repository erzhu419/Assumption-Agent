# Summary

The paper builds a retrieval-level “wisdom library” self-improvement loop for a scaffolded Chinese open-ended LLM solver, then audits the loop’s three accepted wisdom additions using a multi-layer re-judgment stack. Its main methodological claim is that same-family, \(n=50\) LLM-as-judge gates are fragile: the original KEEP decisions often fail to reproduce under cross-family judges, larger samples, fresh domains, cross-solver runs, and faithfulness probes. The paper is unusually explicit about negative results, selection bias, and post-hoc audit construction, but the final claims are weakened by internal inconsistencies, small and non-independent samples, lack of human/external ground truth, and a late preregistered fresh-split result that partially overturns the headline “0/3 survive” story.

# Strengths

- **Clear and important target problem: auditing self-improvement claims rather than demonstrating self-improvement.** The abstract and Introduction explicitly frame the paper as audit methodology, not as evidence that wisdom-library loops work. This is valuable because LLM self-improvement papers often conflate self-generated improvements with independently validated improvements.

- **Honest reporting of negative and contradictory evidence.** The paper reports that all three original KEEPs fail multiple audit measurements, but also includes Exp. 45 showing L1 drops are consistent with regression-to-the-mean, and Exp. 47 showing \(2/3\) original KEEPs replicate under a preregistered fresh split with a laxer L1 threshold. This willingness to narrow the claim substantially strengthens the submission relative to a one-sided “judge fragility” narrative.

- **Operationally concrete audit layers.** Sections “The six-layer audit stack,” Exp. 1, Exp. 8, Exp. 35, Exp. 40, Exp. 42, and Exp. 47 give enough detail to understand how cross-family re-judgment, sample extension, expensive-tier judging, cross-solver replication, GSM8K probing, and fresh-split replication were run. Even if the layers are not novel individually, the paper makes them actionable.

- **Useful selection-bias analysis.** The “Winner’s curse / selection-adjusted analysis” paragraph in Sec. “Binomial confidence intervals” is one of the strongest parts of the paper: it directly addresses the fact that the three KEEPs are selected maxima among 12 noisy measurements. The conclusion that L1 alone cannot distinguish judge fragility from regression-to-the-mean is exactly the kind of statistical self-critique this area needs.

- **Substantial artifact ambition.** The claimed release of code, logs, 21 candidates, answer pairs, prompts, cost breakdowns, and reproducibility details is a real strength for a methodology/case-study paper. The paper also preserves provenance through registry versions, cached answer pairs, and per-experiment logs.

- **Attempts to separate pairwise preference from faithfulness.** Experiments 9–11 and 14–17 attempt to ask whether a wisdom actually changes reasoning in its advertised direction, rather than only whether a judge prefers the answer. The operationalizations are weak, but the distinction is important and often missing in retrieval/self-improvement work.

- **Explicit discussion of post-hoc audit construction.** Sec. “A note on staged stress testing” admits that L1–L6 were not preregistered and were added in response to reviewer-simulation objections. This transparency does not remove the post-hoc concern, but it makes the evidentiary status much clearer.

# Weaknesses

- **The central headline is internally inconsistent with the paper’s own strongest replication result.** The title, abstract, Fig. 1 caption, Fig. 3 caption, Introduction, and Conclusion repeatedly state variants of “0/3 survive” and “final validated library delta \(+0\).” However Exp. 47 reports that on a preregistered fresh 30-pid split, \(3/12\) candidates pass both gates, including \(W_{077}\) and \(W_{078}\), i.e. \(2/3\) original KEEPs replicate under the preregistered protocol. The abstract partially acknowledges this, but the Introduction and Conclusion still present the stricter cached-data null as the main result. For a methodology paper about audit validity, this claim-vs-evidence mismatch is severe. A fix would be to make Exp. 47 the primary endpoint or clearly separate “cached-data strict-threshold non-reproduction” from “fresh-split partial replication,” and rewrite the title, figures, abstract, and conclusion accordingly.

- **The audit stack is post-hoc and the paper does not provide a clean primary estimand.** Sec. “A note on staged stress testing” states that L1–L6 were added after the inner loop in response to reviewer-style objections, with thresholds fixed only before each layer’s data was observed. This bounds threshold hindsight but not layer-selection hindsight or narrative selection. For a top-venue methodology paper, the core audit procedure should be specified before application, with a primary outcome, secondary outcomes, and decision rule. Exp. 47 is a step in this direction, but it only uses inner + L1 thresholds on 30 pids, not the full six-layer stack. A fix would be a genuinely preregistered fresh-loop replication applying the full stack to all candidates with a declared primary endpoint.

- **Sample sizes are too small for the strength and granularity of the claims.** Most key measurements use \(n=50\), \(n=30\), or even \(n=20\), often with many ties and effective \(n\) below the nominal count. Table “Binomial confidence intervals” shows overlapping CIs throughout; several audit CIs include both parity and the gate threshold. Yet the narrative frequently uses strong language such as “falsified,” “collapse,” “failure to reproduce,” and “judge-fragility replicates across every conditionally-distinct axis.” For this class of paper, the audit conclusions should be based on adequately powered comparisons or explicitly framed as exploratory point-estimate non-replication. A fix would be a power analysis, \(n\geq 100\)–200 per primary cell, and hierarchical modeling over candidates, judges, solvers, and problem domains.

- **The paper repeatedly over-interprets point estimates despite acknowledging overlapping confidence intervals.** Table “Binomial confidence intervals” correctly says the narrative “flips” occur within overlapping CIs and are not sharp 95% rejections. But other sections continue to state that all three KEEPs are “falsified,” “actively harm,” or “fail under every axis.” The GSM8K harm claims are also based on 1–2 problems out of 30 with solver drift between runs. The fix is to consistently distinguish point-estimate non-reproduction, statistical rejection of the gate threshold, and evidence of actual harm.

- **No human ground truth for the main open-ended task.** The paper’s central domain is Chinese open-ended multi-domain reasoning, but all quality judgments are by LLMs. gpt-5.5 structured Likert ratings in Exp. 38/41/48 are useful, but they are still LLM judgments and are post-hoc. For an audit-methodology paper, especially one criticizing LLM-as-judge gates, the absence of even a modest human evaluation is a major gap. A fix would be human pairwise or rubric ratings on a stratified subset of cached answer pairs, ideally blind to condition and judge verdicts.

- **The selection-adjusted analysis is important but not sufficient for the cross-family claim.** Exp. 45 simulates regression-to-the-mean for top-3-of-12 noisy \(n=50\) measurements, then compares expected remeasurement drops to observed L1 cross-family drops. But L1 changes the judge family, so it changes the estimand, not just remeasures the same noisy quantity. Thus “pure selection bias is statistically sufficient to explain the observed L1 drops” depends on assumptions that are not modeled. A fix would be a hierarchical model with candidate true effects, judge-family random effects, temporal drift, side-order effects, and selection on the original judge.

- **Claims that audit layers are “conditionally distinct” are still too close to independence language.** The abstract and Introduction sometimes say the layers are conditionally distinct and not independent, which is good. But Fig. 3 caption calls them “independent filters,” Exp. 9 says orthogonal tests have verdicts “statistically independent” from Experiments 1–8, and several arguments count layers as if they were independent confirmations. They reuse candidates, problem pools, cached answer pairs, model families, and candidate-selection events. A fix would be to remove independence language and treat the stack as correlated stress tests, perhaps quantified by a single multilevel model rather than a vote-count narrative.

- **The theoretical decomposition is not identifiable and sometimes contradicts its own caveats.** Sec. “What the A/B gate actually measures” correctly says the \(Z^{\mathrm{specific}}, Z^{\mathrm{generic}}, Z^{\mathrm{style}}\) decomposition is informal and not identifiable. But the later bullet list says L1 “cancels” judge-specific style and persistent lift “localises” to \(Z^{\mathrm{specific}}\), and the text argues “by elimination” that \(Z^{\mathrm{specific}}\) is near zero. This is too strong: judge swaps confound style, competence, tie propensity, calibration, and task interpretation, and faithfulness probes do not identify causal utility. A fix would be to keep the decomposition purely motivational or formalize assumptions under which any component is estimable.

- **The audit stack’s sensitivity remains weakly demonstrated.** Exp. 44 and Exp. 46 construct controls, but all six controls fail, including intended positive controls and a duplicate of an existing library wisdom. The paper later says Exp. 47 demonstrates sensitivity because some fresh-split candidates pass inner+L1, but these are not known positives and did not pass the full six-layer stack. For a methodology paper, showing that the audit can accept genuinely useful interventions is as important as showing it rejects dubious ones. A fix would be synthetic tasks with known helpful wisdoms, or remove-one/reinsert-one experiments where a previously validated library component is withheld and restored.

- **The “fresh split” replication uses a laxer threshold and smaller \(n\), making the reversal hard to interpret.** Exp. 47 uses \(n=30\) and L1 threshold \(0.55\), while much of the paper’s strict null uses \(n=50/100\) and threshold \(0.60\). The paper is transparent about this, but then continues to summarize \(+0\) as the final validated delta. At \(n=30\), Wilson intervals around 0.57 include both 0.50 and 0.60. A fix would be to rerun Exp. 47 at \(n\geq100\) with both 0.55 and 0.60 thresholds reported and declared in advance.

- **The main loop and scaffold are only weakly validated.** The v20 scaffold gains in Sec. “Architecture ratchet” and “Held-out generalization” are same-family LLM-judged, and the Limitations section reveals dynamic same-domain exemplars selected from the evaluation pool. The authors note these issues, but the scaffold is still used as the substrate for candidate generation and audit claims. For a case study this may be acceptable, but the scaffold improvement claims should be de-emphasized or independently audited. A fix would be cross-family/human validation of v20 vs. baseline, or make clear that scaffold quality is not an empirical contribution.

- **Evaluation-pool exposure undermines held-out/generalization language.** The Limitations section states that at solve time each evaluation pid receives a same-domain exemplar dynamically selected from other pids in the evaluation pool. Even if this is symmetric between base and ext, it weakens claims about held-out generalization, fresh-domain independence, and external validity. Symmetry helps relative comparisons but does not eliminate distribution exposure. A fix is a clean rerun with strict pool separation and no in-pool exemplar retrieval.

- **LLM identity and reproducibility are fragile.** The paper uses model names such as gemini-3-flash, GPT-5.4, GPT-5.5, and Claude Opus 4.6 via a third-party proxy. The Limitations section notes exact-token reproduction depends on proxy routing state. For an audit paper whose results hinge on judge-family differences and temporal drift, this is a serious reproducibility issue. A fix would be official endpoint replications, archived model IDs where possible, cached prompts/outputs for all analyses, and separating conclusions that require live model reproduction from those supported by cached artifacts.

- **Several analyses are hard to trust because the paper is sprawling and internally disorganized.** Experiment numbering is nonsequential, Exp. 31 refers to “Exp. 11” as the majority-of-3 result though that appears to be Exp. 7, Exp. 34/35/36/37 are interleaved with later experiments, and the Conclusion appears outdated relative to Exp. 47. The text repeatedly says objections are “closed” and uses “strict reviewer closure” framing, which reads more like an evolving rebuttal log than a polished scientific paper. A fix would be a major rewrite: one preregistered primary study in the main paper, a compact exploratory-study summary, and all chronological debugging/rebuttal material moved to appendices.

- **Novelty relative to existing evaluation practice is overstated.** The Discussion says L1 cross-family re-judgment is “non-trivial” and not routinely reported in adjacent self-improvement papers. That may be true for the selected table, but cross-model judging, judge bias, side bias, and LLM-as-judge reliability are already active evaluation topics. The paper’s novelty is best framed as applying these checks to one self-improving retrieval loop, not as inventing a new audit methodology. A fix would be a fuller related-work treatment of LLM judge calibration/bias and a more modest novelty claim.

- **Autonomy claims are stronger than the evidence supports.** The loop is described as “no human curation,” “recursive self-validation,” and “the agent runs its own audit-update-reaudit loop,” but humans designed the library ontology, modules, thresholds, audit layers, and many follow-up experiments. Exp. 18–19, as summarized, has the agent implement only part of a replacement gate, with humans filling missing bodies. For this class of paper, autonomy boundaries matter. A fix would be a provenance table marking each decision as human-authored, agent-authored, or algorithmic, and removing “first”/autonomy claims not directly supported.

# Questions to the authors

1. If you had to declare one primary empirical claim after seeing Exp. 47, what is it: “0/3 original KEEPs fail the strict cached-data audit,” “2/3 original KEEPs replicate under preregistered fresh inner+L1,” or something else? How would the title and conclusion change under that primary claim?

2. Can you provide a preregistered full-stack replication on a fresh loop or fresh candidate batch, with \(n\geq100\), applying all six layers and a single declared decision rule?

3. Do the L1 drops remain larger than expected under a hierarchical model that includes selection, judge-family random effects, temporal drift, and side-order effects, rather than comparing cross-family drops to same-estimand regression-to-the-mean?

4. What happens if you run a clean no-evaluation-pool-exemplar version of the inner gate and Exp. 47? Are the same candidates selected, and do \(W_{077}\) and \(W_{078}\) still replicate?

5. Can you include human ratings for a stratified subset of the cached Chinese open-ended answer pairs, especially cases where gemini prefers ext and expensive judges/gpt-5.5 prefer base?

6. Can you construct a genuine positive control that the audit stack should accept, e.g. a synthetic task family with a known helpful intervention or a remove/reinsert test of a validated library component?

7. Why should a real retrieval-level improvement be required to survive cross-solver and fresh-domain tests if the original claim is about one solver and one deployment distribution? What is the formal estimand of “self-improvement” that justifies all six layers as necessary?

# Rating

Reject

The paper is interesting and unusually honest, but it is not yet a top-venue methodology contribution. The most important issue is the unresolved claim-vs-evidence mismatch: the paper’s dominant “0/3 survive” framing is partially overturned by the preregistered fresh-split result, yet the title, figures, and conclusion still emphasize the stricter cached-data null. The audit stack is largely post-hoc, underpowered, lacks human ground truth, and has unresolved sensitivity/positive-control problems. I would be much more positive on a rewritten version centered around a preregistered full-stack replication with a clear primary endpoint and a compact, consistent narrative.
