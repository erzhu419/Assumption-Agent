# Summary

The paper presents a detailed case study of a closed-loop LLM “wisdom library” system that proposes methodological aphorisms, accepts them via a same-family LLM-judged A/B gate at \(n=50\), and then audits the accepted candidates. The central empirical finding is negative: the original gate accepts 3/12 candidates, but these do not survive cross-family re-judgment or preregistered fresh-data replications at \(n=100\) and \(n=200\). The paper argues from this null that same-family LLM-as-judge A/B gating is an unreliable validator for retrieval/prompt-level self-improvement loops, and proposes a broader six-stage architecture for genuine self-hypothesizing/self-validating agents.

# Strengths

- **Honest and useful negative result, clearly foregrounded.** The abstract, Introduction “What we found,” and Sections 3.2–3.5 repeatedly state that the loop does *not* yield validated library growth. This strengthens the paper because negative results about self-improvement loops are currently underreported, and the authors avoid claiming “self-improvement works.”

- **Strong audit mindset with multiple concrete failure modes.** The six audit layers in the Introduction and Section 2.6 target judge family, side position, sample size, solver family, domain shift, and faithfulness. Even if some layers are post-hoc, the decomposition into nuisance variables is useful for this class of methodology paper.

- **Preregistered fresh-data replications are the most persuasive empirical component.** Section 3.5 reports three fresh evaluations: \(n=100\) without exemplars, \(n=100\) with exemplars, and \(n=200\) with exemplars, all yielding \(0/12\) under the joint rule. This substantially strengthens the central null beyond the post-hoc cached audit.

- **The paper explicitly acknowledges post-hoc audit construction.** Section 3 states that the audit layers were not preregistered and were added in response to objections. This transparency is important because otherwise the audit stack could easily look like hindsight-driven garden-of-forking-paths evidence.

- **Selection-bias analysis addresses an important alternative explanation.** Section 3.3 models top-3-of-12 winner’s curse and concludes that cached drops are consistent with regression to the mean. This is a useful corrective to a simpler but less justified “cross-family judge proves the gate was wrong” interpretation.

- **Reproducibility intent appears strong.** The main body claims release of code, logs, judgment files, registry states, prompts, compute/cost, model identities, temperatures, seeds, and proxy details. For a case study / audit-methods paper, such artifacts are central to credibility.

- **The paper is unusually clear about non-claims and unresolved objections.** The “Scope and non-claims,” Discussion, and Limitations sections explicitly list single-loop scope, lack of human ground truth, positive-control failure, post-hoc audit stack, and one-cycle limitation. This improves trust in the authors’ interpretation.

- **Stage-roadmap diagnostic table reports failures as well as successes.** Section 4.1 states that the naive cheap world model fails with AUROC \(0.40\), and naive new-prior generation fails on \(0/5\) hold-outs. Reporting these failures helps prevent the roadmap section from becoming purely speculative.

# Weaknesses

- **The main contribution is diluted by an overextended architectural roadmap.** Sections 4 and 4.1 move from a concrete negative case study about an LLM-judged wisdom-library gate to a six-stage theory of self-hypothesizing agents, including world models, schedulers, formal alignment, and new-prior generation. For a top-venue methodology paper, this leap is insufficiently supported by the evidence: the core experiments establish unreliability of one gate in one loop, not the necessity or sufficiency of the proposed six-stage architecture. A fix would be to make the paper primarily an audit-methods case study and demote the roadmap to discussion, or provide substantially stronger empirical validation of the roadmap on realistic tasks.

- **Single-loop, single-primary-solver scope is too narrow for the broad methodological recommendation.** The central loop uses gemini-3-flash as solver, same-family judging for acceptance, a Chinese open-ended benchmark, one library, and one evolution cycle (Sections 2.1, 3.1, Limitations). Yet the conclusion recommends cross-family re-judgment, selection-bias modeling, preregistered \(n\ge100\) fresh evaluation, hierarchical Bayes, and pool-separation sensitivity for future retrieval-level self-improvement papers. The recommendation is plausible, but the evidence base is one highly idiosyncratic loop. This would be fixed by applying the audit stack to at least one or two external/self-improvement baselines or rerunning the full loop with a different solver/judge family.

- **The audit stack is post-hoc, and the paper’s strongest methodological object was not itself validated prospectively.** Section 3 explicitly says L1–L6 were added after the inner loop, and the Discussion repeats that a truly preregistered application to a freshly run candidate-generation loop remains future work. The preregistered \(n=100/n=200\) replications validate the null for the original candidates, but they do not validate the *audit protocol* as a prespecified tool applied to a new loop. The fix is a prospective study: freeze the audit stack, run a new loop end-to-end, then audit all accepted candidates under the frozen protocol.

- **The positive-control failure seriously limits interpretation of gate sensitivity.** In Discussion “Positive controls remain unresolved,” all 6/6 controls fail the gate, and the authors infer that the gate may be structurally anti-additive. This is a major weakness for an audit paper: if no known-helpful intervention passes, then the system cannot distinguish “candidate wisdoms are bad” from “adding context generally hurts” or “the evaluation setup is hostile to any addition.” The fix is to include a validated positive control, ideally a synthetic task family where a specific inserted hint deterministically improves objective accuracy, and show that the gate/audit stack accepts it.

- **No human ground truth on the main open-ended evaluation leaves judge disagreement hard to interpret.** The paper uses cross-family LLM judging, expensive judges, GSM8K, and structured LLM ratings as substitutes, but the main Chinese open-ended pool lacks human labels or human pairwise preferences (Limitations; Discussion “No human ground truth”). Since the paper’s target is judge fragility, replacing one LLM judge with other LLM judges cannot fully establish which verdict is correct. A fix would be a human evaluation on a stratified subset of cached and fresh pairs, especially around the threshold cases \(0.55\)–\(0.60\).

- **Claim-vs-evidence mismatch around “clean null” and “removing either collapses the loop’s apparent gains.”** The abstract and Introduction call the result a “clean null” and say removing either exemplar boost or selection bias collapses the gains. The data do support \(0/12\) passing the prespecified threshold at larger \(n\), but some \(n=200\) candidates are close to the inner threshold, e.g. \(W_{078}\) inner \(0.590\), L1 \(0.573\), and \(\textsc{wcand03}\) inner \(0.580\), L1 \(0.574\) in Section 3.5. The thresholded decision is null, but the effect-size interpretation is more nuanced: there may be sub-threshold positive signal. The fix is to consistently phrase this as “no candidate meets the preregistered acceptance rule,” not as evidence of zero utility.

- **Statistical treatment of the empirical-Bayes selection model is questionable and underexplained.** Section 3.3 reports a fitted Beta prior with concentration \(\hat\alpha+\hat\beta \approx 6\times10^7\), implying almost no between-candidate heterogeneity. This extreme concentration is likely a boundary/identifiability artifact from only 12 noisy binomial observations, not robust evidence that all candidates have identical true rates. The paper should not lean heavily on percentile claims from this fit without sensitivity analyses over more reasonable hyperpriors/concentrations. A fix would be to report posterior predictive checks under several priors, including weakly informative hierarchical priors and nonparametric/bootstrap simulations.

- **The hierarchical Bayes subsection is confusing and partially self-undermining.** Section 3.4 says the \(n=30\) hierarchical fit gave \(W_{078}\) posterior probability \(0.763\) for the decision rule, but was later falsified because it omitted the exemplar covariate and had effectively flat between-candidate prior. This is presented as a cautionary checkpoint, but it occupies substantial space and may distract from the stronger preregistered \(n\ge100\) evidence. The fix is to move most of this to the appendix and keep only a concise lesson in the main text, or present it as a failed analysis with clearer diagnostics.

- **The paper relies heavily on thresholded pass/fail decisions where continuous uncertainty would be more informative.** Many claims are phrased as \(0/12\), \(3/12\), \(0/3\), or “clears \(0.60\),” while several estimates are close to the threshold (Sections 3.5 and Contribution 4). For an audit methodology paper, the decision rule matters, but so do posterior intervals, effect sizes, and expected utility under long-horizon library accumulation. A fix would be to present a unified table of all candidates’ inner/L1 estimates with confidence/credible intervals and posterior probabilities of exceeding thresholds.

- **The same-domain in-pool exemplar mechanism is a major design confound, not just a limitation.** The gate’s first step mines exemplars from held-out 50 problems, and the v20 scaffold retrieves same-domain examples from the evaluation pool at solve time (Sections 2.7 and Limitations). The authors argue symmetry between base and ext, but Section 3.5 shows exemplar use can shift win rates by \(0.06\)–\(0.18\), so symmetry does not eliminate interaction effects between candidate wisdom and exemplars. The fix is to make strict pool separation the default primary protocol, with exemplar mechanisms evaluated only as an ablation.

- **The scaffold’s claimed baseline improvement is not independently audited, yet it anchors the motivation.** Figure 1 and Figure 2 claim the v20 scaffold reaches \(86\%\) or \(88\%\) win rate versus baselines, while Contribution 3 admits these gains were measured by same-family pairwise LLM judging and should not be treated as independently audited. Since the whole study builds on the scaffold as an “instrument,” unaudited scaffold quality weakens confidence that failures are about wisdom validation rather than scaffold/judge quirks. A fix would be to audit the scaffold-vs-baseline claim with cross-family and/or human/objective evaluation, at least on a subset.

- **The “formal alignment layer” discussion is speculative relative to the empirical proxy.** Stage 3 in Section 4 invokes Markov-category isomorphism and Fisher-distance between statistical experiments, but Section 4.1 only tests cross-language scheduler pick consistency. The paper correctly says this is necessary but not sufficient, yet later text says “Three stages work (priors library, experience feedback, alignment),” which overstates the result. The fix is to label Stage 3 as “not tested; only language-invariance proxy passed” throughout.

- **The synthetic roadmap-validation environment is too small and artificial for the claims made from it.** Section 4.1 uses 60 problems across 4 task families, known-optimal priors, 5 languages, and leave-one-family-out new-prior generation. This is a useful diagnostic toy environment, but not enough to say which stages “work” in general. The fix is to reduce claims to “toy diagnostic probes” or expand to realistic benchmarks with objective outcomes and more task families.

- **Some internal count/reporting inconsistencies make the experimental narrative hard to audit from the main body.** The paper alternates between 11 candidates + 1 base in Section 2.7, 12 candidates in Sections 3.1 and elsewhere, 21 candidates in Contribution 5, and multiple experiment numbers whose details are in appendices. This may be resolvable from supplementary material, but the main text should provide one canonical candidate-flow diagram/table showing all candidates, source generator, gate score, audit status, and inclusion in each experiment. This would fix reader confusion and improve reproducibility.

- **Literature comparison is too categorical.** Table 1 says adjacent papers “almost never” report cross-family re-judgment and marks several with dashes. This may be directionally true, but the comparison mixes weight-level self-improvement, prompt optimization, environment-reward agents, and LLM-judged loops, and the paper’s own single-loop evidence does not justify strong implications for all of them. The fix is to narrow the claim to LLM-preference-gated retrieval/prompt updates and avoid suggesting that weight-level methods face the same failure mode unless directly tested.

# Questions to the authors

1. Did you run a fully prospective experiment where the audit stack and thresholds were frozen *before* candidate generation, not just before fresh evaluation of the original 12 candidates? If so, what was the pass rate?

2. Can you provide a validated positive control where adding a known-useful wisdom/hint improves objective task performance and is accepted by the same gate/audit procedure?

3. On the \(n=200\) v20+exemplar run, what are the Wilson intervals or posterior probabilities for the top candidates exceeding \(0.60\)? In particular, how should readers interpret \(W_{078}=0.590\) and \(\textsc{wcand03}=0.580\) relative to the hard threshold?

4. How sensitive is the empirical-Bayes selection-bias conclusion to the extreme fitted prior concentration \(\approx 6\times10^7\)? What happens under weakly informative hierarchical priors or bootstrap winner’s-curse simulations?

5. Are there human pairwise judgments for any subset of the Chinese open-ended pairs, especially the original 3 KEEPs and the \(n=200\) near-threshold candidates? If not, why was no small human study included?

6. What exactly are the 12 original candidates, their generator sources, original gate scores, fresh \(n=100/n=200\) scores, and audit inclusion status? A compact main-text table would change readability and confidence.

7. Did the same-domain exemplar retrieval ever select examples whose content was unusually aligned with a candidate wisdom, creating candidate-specific interaction despite base/ext symmetry?

8. If the gate may be structurally anti-additive, as suggested by the failed positive controls, why should the conclusion be “same-family gate is unreliable” rather than “this scaffold/gate design is not capable of detecting beneficial additions”?

# Rating

**Weak Reject**

The paper contains a valuable and unusually transparent negative case study, and the preregistered \(n=100/n=200\) replications make the central null credible for this specific loop. However, the submission overreaches from one idiosyncratic Chinese wisdom-library system to broad recommendations about self-improving LLM loops and an ambitious six-stage architecture that is only weakly supported by toy diagnostics. The lack of human ground truth, failed positive controls, post-hoc audit-stack construction, and single-loop/single-primary-solver scope are serious weaknesses for a top-venue methodology paper. I would be much more positive on a tighter version focused on the audit case study, with prospective audit validation and a functioning positive control.
