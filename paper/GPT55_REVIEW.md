# Summary

The paper presents a case study of an autonomous LLM “wisdom library” improvement loop: the loop proposes 12 candidate methodological entries, keeps 3 under a same-family \(+10\)pp LLM-judge A/B gate, and then subjects those KEEP decisions to a multi-layer audit. The main methodological claim is that cross-family re-judgment, selection-bias modeling, and preregistered fresh-data re-evaluation together can distinguish cached-data regressions-to-the-mean from genuine non-replication. Empirically, the cached audit makes the original 3 KEEPs look non-robust, but a later preregistered fresh-data run at a laxer L1 threshold recovers 2/3 original KEEPs plus one originally rejected candidate. The paper argues for routine reporting of cross-family re-judgment and non-pairwise faithfulness checks for retrieval-level self-improving LLM claims.

# Strengths

- **Unusually transparent negative-result reporting** — Throughout the abstract, Experiments 1, 8, 35, 45, 47, and the Conclusion, the authors repeatedly report failures, reversals, weak posteriors, and threshold sensitivity rather than hiding them. This strengthens the submission because auditing self-improving loops is exactly a setting where selective positive reporting is a major risk.

- **Concrete, operational audit stack** — Section “The six-layer audit stack,” Section 3, and Experiments 1–17 define specific interventions: cross-family re-judgment, side reseeding, \(n\)-extension, cross-solver evaluation, fresh-domain/GSM8K tests, and faithfulness probes. Even if not all layers are novel, the operational detail makes the work more useful than a purely conceptual warning about LLM-as-judge fragility.

- **Identical-answer-pair cross-family re-judgment is a clean diagnostic** — Experiment 1 re-scores the same cached base/ext answer strings with Claude Opus rather than regenerating answers. This is a strong design choice because it isolates judge-family sensitivity from solver stochasticity and directly probes the reliability of the original KEEP decisions.

- **Explicit selection-bias / winner’s-curse analysis** — Experiment 45 is one of the better parts of the paper: it recognizes that the top 3 of 12 noisy \(n=50\) measurements should regress on remeasurement. This substantially improves the paper over a simpler “L1 dropped, therefore judge bias” story.

- **Preregistered fresh-data re-evaluation is a meaningful addition** — Experiment 47, despite limitations, is stronger than the cached-data audit alone because it evaluates all original candidates on a disjoint fresh split with thresholds committed before that run. The authors also report the important sensitivity that using \(0.60\) instead of the preregistered \(0.55\) changes the headline from 3 passers to 1.

- **Good attention to inferential uncertainty in several places** — The paper reports Wilson intervals, tie handling, effective \(n\), posterior probabilities, McNemar non-significance on GSM8K, and model drift caveats in Sections “Binomial confidence intervals,” Exp. 42, Exp. 47, and Exp. 49. This is valuable because the raw point estimates are often near decision thresholds.

- **Artifact and provenance emphasis** — The method sections, registry description, and reproducibility paragraph describe versioned registries, logs, prompts, seeds, model identities, costs, and commit hashes for the preregistration. For a case study, this substantially strengthens credibility and potential re-use.

- **The paper scopes many claims more carefully than typical self-improvement submissions** — The abstract, “Scope and non-claims,” Discussion, and Conclusion repeatedly say this is one loop, one solver family, small \(n\), no human ground truth, and suggestive rather than decisive evidence. This honesty is a real strength, even though the paper still sometimes oversteps.

# Weaknesses

- **The central “distinguishing real non-replication from selection bias” claim is not demonstrated strongly enough** — The abstract and Conclusion claim the audit stack plus Exp. 45/47 “suggests a separation” between selection-driven cached drops for W077/W078 and real non-replication for W076. For this class of methodology paper, the core diagnostic claim needs substantially stronger evidence than three KEEPs and one fresh \(n \approx 30\) split. Exp. 47 itself reports weak joint posteriors: W078 \(0.539\), W077 \(0.370\), WCAND03 \(0.424\) for the exact preregistered decision rule; W076’s fresh inner \(11/27=0.41\) is a low point estimate but still from a small sample. A fix would be to preregister the diagnostic classification rule and run it on multiple independent fresh splits or a fresh full loop with \(n \ge 100\) per candidate, then report calibrated error rates for “selection-driven drop” vs “true non-replication.”

- **Most of the audit stack is post-hoc, and Exp. 47 does not remove that concern** — Section “A note on staged stress testing” explicitly states the full stack was not preregistered and grew through reviewer simulation. Exp. 47 is preregistered only after a large number of cached audits, gate variants, and threshold discussions had already occurred, and its \(0.55\) L1 threshold was chosen after extensive prior knowledge of the system. For a methodology paper about audit practice, post-hoc layer selection and threshold selection are central threats. The fix is a clean prospective study: freeze the entire audit stack, thresholds, candidate-generation process, and statistical decision rule before running a new loop.

- **No human or otherwise validated ground truth for the main open-ended task** — The paper repeatedly interprets cross-family disagreement, gpt-5.5 structured ratings, and GSM8K side probes as evidence about wisdom utility, but the primary Chinese open-ended task has no human evaluation. Cross-family LLM disagreement shows non-robust judging; it does not establish which judge is closer to truth. Exp. 38/41/48 use stronger LLMs as human substitutes, but that remains LLM self-evaluation. A fix would be a blinded human panel on a stratified subset of cached and fresh base/ext pairs, with inter-annotator agreement and comparison to each judge family.

- **The statistical modeling remains too weak for the claims made** — Exp. 45’s empirical-Bayes selection model assumes exchangeability among the 12 candidates even though Exp. 44 later shows the 12 are strongly prefiltered and not exchangeable with controls; it also models regression from noisy measurements but compares to drops that include a judge-family change. Exp. 49 calls the DerSimonian–Laird random-effects pool a “proper hierarchical model,” but DL with \(k=1\)–2 fresh judge cells cannot estimate between-judge variance and does not model shared pids, paired base/ext structure, candidate-level effects, or correlated judge errors. The joint posterior in Exp. 47 multiplies inner and L1 probabilities under an independence assumption despite same answer pairs and same pids. A fix would be a single preregistered hierarchical logistic/Beta-binomial model over pid × candidate × judge × solver, with candidate and pid random effects and explicit judge-correlation sensitivity.

- **The main empirical headline is highly threshold-sensitive** — Exp. 47 reports that at the preregistered \(0.55\) L1 threshold, W077, W078, and WCAND03 pass; at the original \(0.60\) L1 threshold, only W078 passes. The abstract and Contribution 2 foreground “2/3 original KEEPs replicate,” but W077’s L1 is only \(17/30=0.57\), with wide uncertainty and a joint posterior below 0.5 for the exact rule. For an audit methodology, a binary replicate/non-replicate conclusion should not hinge on two pairwise verdicts. A fix would be to report continuous posterior decision curves over thresholds and define replication by posterior probability or expected utility rather than point-estimate threshold crossing.

- **Small \(n\), one loop, and imbalanced coverage limit generality** — The core case is 12 original candidates, 3 original KEEPs, one solver family, and one loop cycle. Several audit layers are partial or were originally partial: Exp. 26 only W076, Exp. 31 only W078, Exp. 32 initially only W078, Exp. 33 only new candidates under gemini, and Exp. 40 reveals non-null cross-solver cells. This is too small and uneven to support broad methodology conclusions beyond “this case was fragile.” A fix would be a balanced factorial design over all candidates × solvers × judges × domains, ideally across multiple independently run loops.

- **Cross-family re-judgment is over-interpreted relative to low agreement** — The paper correctly says L1 cannot identify \(Z^{\mathrm{specific}}\), but later language still frequently treats L1 failures as evidence of judge-fragility or lack of methodological substance. Exp. 37 reports \(\kappa \approx 0.18\)–\(0.26\), i.e. weak agreement among judges, which means a majority/cross-family panel is not automatically a more valid measurement. For LLM-as-judge audit work, reliability and validity must both be established. A fix would be to calibrate judge families against human labels or objective known-answer tasks before using them as an audit authority.

- **Positive controls do not establish sensitivity** — Exp. 44 and Exp. 46 are useful, but all six controls fail, including “useful” and base-library-duplicate controls. The paper then treats WCAND03 in Exp. 47 as an empirical positive control, but WCAND03 is not independently known to be useful; it is another LLM-judged candidate passing a noisy fresh gate. For an audit stack, specificity without a true positive control leaves open that the audit rejects or accepts for artifact reasons. A fix would be a synthetic or semi-synthetic task where a known intervention deterministically improves outcomes, or a human-validated wisdom whose utility is established before insertion.

- **Evaluation-pool exemplar exposure is a real confound, not fully neutralized by symmetry** — The Limitations section says the solver prompt includes a same-domain exemplar dynamically selected from the evaluation pool’s other pids, and argues this is symmetric between base and ext. Symmetry does not guarantee neutrality: an added wisdom can interact with the in-pool exemplar, retrieval ordering, or answer style differently in base and ext. For a held-out gate, any use of evaluation-pool examples weakens the interpretation. The fix is a clean re-run with strict pool separation and no in-pool exemplars, or an ablation showing the base/ext delta is invariant to removing this mechanism.

- **Internal inconsistencies and narrative instability make the paper hard to trust** — Exp. 39’s abstract/summary says “0/6 English wisdoms clear \(0.60\) under any family across two independent runs,” while the Exp. 39 text says an earlier run had EW06 gemini \(0.63\). Exp. 27 refers to “Exp~11 majority-of-3” even though the three-family triangulation is Exp. 7. The paper alternates among final deltas of \(+0\), \(+3\), \(+2/3\), and \(+1\) depending on cached/fresh and \(0.55/0.60\) thresholds. A fix would be to restructure around one primary claims table with data split, threshold, judge set, and decision rule, and remove contradictory obsolete statements.

- **The paper is far too sprawling for its evidential base** — The main body contains dozens of experiments, many of which are exploratory, retrospective, or only loosely connected to the main claim. Phrases like “Strict-reviewer closure,” “strongest negative finding,” “first time,” and repeated “closed” objections are self-promotional relative to the evidence. For a top-venue methodology paper, the contribution would be stronger if the paper were shorter, with a preregistered protocol, a small number of primary analyses, and exploratory material clearly separated into appendices. A fix is to make Exp. 45/47 plus a cleaned audit-stack protocol the core, and demote speculative architectural/self-rebuttal material.

- **The random-30% baseline and related p-values are not meaningful utility baselines** — Sections on Exp. 15, Exp. 27, Exp. 33, and the CI section compare pass rates to an “arbitrary random-inclusion” \(30\%\) reference, while also admitting it is not principled. This does not tell us whether a gate is useful, only whether its pass count is lower than an arbitrary number. A fix would be to define a decision-theoretic utility function or compare against concrete policies: always accept, always reject, same-family gate, majority-of-3, human-screened gate, and known-positive/known-negative controls.

- **Reproducibility is limited by proprietary models, proxy routing, and observed drift** — The paper is commendably transparent about ruoli.dev proxy access and temporal drift, but that does not solve the problem: Exp. 37 and Exp. 42 show substantial drift even with nominally fixed settings. For an audit methodology paper, reproducibility of verdicts is central. A fix would be to include official-endpoint replications for primary cells, release all cached answer texts and judge prompts, and treat model-time as a random factor with repeated temporal samples.

- **Theoretical decomposition is explicitly non-identifiable but still drives interpretation** — Section “What the A/B gate actually measures” carefully says the \(Z^{\mathrm{specific}}, Z^{\mathrm{generic}}, Z^{\mathrm{style}}\) decomposition is informal and not identifiable. Later sections nevertheless use it to interpret judge-tier differences and faithfulness failures as evidence about generic/style components. For a methodology paper, an informal taxonomy is acceptable, but conclusions should not depend on it. A fix would be to add actual placebo/length-matched/random-context interventions and causal diagrams specifying what each audit layer can and cannot identify.

# Questions to the authors

1. If Exp. 47 were repeated with \(n \ge 100\) fresh pids and the stricter \(0.60\) L1 threshold, would the claimed “2/3 original KEEPs replicate” still hold? This is the single most important empirical question for the paper’s revised headline.

2. What was the substantive rationale for the preregistered \(0.55\) L1 threshold in Exp. 47, beyond being “laxer”? Was this threshold chosen before or after observing the cached L1 values and threshold sensitivity of W077/W078?

3. Can you provide a blinded human evaluation on a stratified subset of the cached and Exp. 47 fresh base/ext pairs, especially W077 and W078? If human raters agree with the fresh L1 recovery, my confidence in the main claim would increase substantially.

4. Can you re-run the primary gate/audit cells with strict pool separation, i.e. no same-domain exemplar selected from the evaluation pool? The current “symmetric exposure” argument is plausible but not sufficient.

5. Can you fit and report a full hierarchical model over per-pid verdicts rather than pooled proportions/DL meta-analysis, including judge-family random effects, pid random effects, and correlation between inner and L1 verdicts on the same answer pair?

6. Were all 12 original candidates evaluated against exactly the same base library version, retrieval procedure, exemplar availability, and prompt format? The text suggests W078 was validated after pruning while the success-distilled candidates were validated earlier; this matters for comparing candidate scores.

7. Can you construct a true positive control that is independently known to improve task outcomes before insertion into the library? The current controls establish specificity but not sensitivity.

8. Would a fresh full loop — candidate generation, pruning, validation, and audit all rerun under a frozen protocol — produce the same diagnostic pattern? Exp. 47 is useful but does not answer this.

# Rating

Weak Reject

The paper is unusually transparent and contains several useful audit ideas, especially identical-pair cross-family re-judgment and explicit selection-bias analysis. However, the central diagnostic claim is not yet supported at top-venue strength: it rests on one post-hoc-heavy case study, three original KEEPs, one small fresh split, threshold-sensitive decisions, and no human ground truth. The statistical modeling and LLM-as-judge validity are also not strong enough to justify the “distinguishing real replication from selection bias” framing. I would be much more positive on a shorter, cleaner, preregistered multi-loop version with human or objective calibration and a full hierarchical analysis.
