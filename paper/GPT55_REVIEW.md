# Summary

The submission builds a retrieval-library “self-improving” LLM loop that proposes, validates, and prunes methodological “wisdoms,” then audits the loop’s KEEP decisions using cross-family judging, side/randomness checks, sample extension, cross-solver tests, fresh-domain tests, and faithfulness probes. The central empirical story is deliberately mixed: the original three KEEPs fail to reproduce on cached answer pairs at the original \(0.60\) threshold, but a preregistered fresh-data re-evaluation of the same 12 candidates finds that two of the three original KEEPs, plus one originally rejected candidate, pass the inner gate and a laxer L1 threshold. The paper argues that the audit stack plus selection-bias modelling can distinguish cached-data regression-to-the-mean from genuine non-replication.

# Strengths

- **Clear and important target problem: auditing self-improving LLM loops rather than merely reporting loop gains.** The abstract and Introduction explicitly frame the paper as “audit methodology, not about whether self-improving LLM loops work,” and ask whether a reported \(k\)-KEEP self-improvement claim should be believed. This is a timely and useful reframing for LLM-as-judge self-improvement work.

- **Unusually candid reporting of negative and mixed results.** The paper repeatedly reports failures of its own loop: \(0/3\) cached KEEPs clearing \(0.60\) under L1/L3/L5/expensive-judge audits, Exp~33’s \(0/9\) new candidates, Exp~44/46 controls failing, and the non-generalization of the trigger-conditioned gate. This honesty strengthens the submission relative to many self-improvement papers that only report positive deltas.

- **Operational audit layers are concrete enough to be reused.** The six audit layers in the Introduction and §3 are specified in actionable terms: rejudge identical answer pairs with another family, reseed side presentation, extend \(n\), regenerate with other solvers, test fresh domains/GSM8K, and probe non-pairwise faithfulness. This is a practical contribution even if the particular empirical case is limited.

- **The paper explicitly acknowledges non-independence among audit layers.** The abstract and Introduction state that L1/L2 reuse cached pairs, L3/L4 overlap in solver/problem pools, and the layers are “conditionally distinct” rather than independent. This improves the credibility of the analysis and avoids a common overclaim.

- **Selection-bias / winner’s-curse analysis is a valuable addition.** Exp~45 directly addresses the possibility that cached-data drops are expected after selecting the top 3 of 12 noisy \(n=50\) measurements. Even though I have concerns about the modelling, including this analysis materially improves the paper by preventing an over-simple “cross-judge drop = spurious” interpretation.

- **Preregistered fresh-data evaluation is the strongest part of the empirical story.** Exp~47 clearly distinguishes itself from the post-hoc audit stack and freezes thresholds before evaluating a disjoint 30-pid split. This is exactly the kind of evidence needed after many exploratory audit layers.

- **Reproducibility effort appears substantial.** The main text claims release of code, logs, cached answer pairs, wisdom registry states, judgement files, prompt templates, compute/cost breakdowns, seeds, temperatures, and model/proxy details. For a methodology/case-study paper, this level of artefact logging is a major strength.

- **The paper is careful in several places to retract or narrow earlier claims.** Examples include §Exp~27 retracting the circular F1 framing, §Exp~35 distinguishing point-estimate non-reproduction from formal 95% rejection, and §Exp~47 clarifying that the experiment is not a fresh full loop. These corrections make the paper more scientifically trustworthy.

# Weaknesses

- **The main “fresh-loop replication” framing is internally inconsistent and materially overstated.** Exp~47 explicitly says it is “not a re-execution of the entire self-improving loop” and does not rerun candidate generation, distillation, or pruning; however, the title, abstract, multiple earlier mentions, and especially the Conclusion say “fresh-loop replication” and even “re-executing the entire loop from scratch on 30 preregistered fresh pids.” For this class of paper, the distinction is central: evaluating fixed candidates on new data is much weaker than rerunning the autonomous loop. The fix is to remove “fresh-loop” language everywhere, correct the Conclusion, and title the result as “preregistered fresh-data re-evaluation of the original candidate set.”

- **The strongest positive replication result is underpowered and threshold-sensitive, yet drives the paper’s final differentiated claim.** Exp~47 uses only 30 fresh pids, and the paper itself notes that Wilson intervals near \(0.55\) include both \(0.50\) and \(0.60\). Exp~49 reports W077 fresh L1 as \(P(\theta>0.55)=0.546\), which is barely above a coin-flip posterior probability, yet the paper labels W077 as “REPLICATE.” For an audit methodology paper, binary replicate/collapse language should not rest on such weak evidence. The fix is to run Exp~47 at \(n\ge 100\) with all L1 values reported for all candidates, or downgrade W077 to “inconclusive/moderate fresh support” and make the claim probabilistic.

- **The paper lacks an external ground truth or validated positive controls, so it cannot demonstrate that the audit stack improves decision quality.** The Limitations and Exp~44/46 admit that all constructed positive controls fail and that human ground truth is absent. Exp~47 is then described as establishing sensitivity because the audit accepts WCAND03/W077/W078, but those are not independently known positives; they are just candidates that pass another LLM-judged gate. For a methodology paper, the key question is not only whether the stack rejects things, but whether it accepts truly useful interventions. A fix would be synthetic or semi-synthetic tasks with known useful/harmful wisdoms, human labels, or objective task outcomes where specific interventions are independently validated before running the audit.

- **Much of the audit stack is post-hoc, and the paper does not sufficiently separate exploratory from confirmatory evidence.** §Experiments states that L1–L6 were added through staged reviewer simulation, not preregistered, and §Discussion acknowledges gate-design freedom and layer-selection hindsight. This is acceptable for exploration, but the paper repeatedly uses the full stack as if it establishes a robust methodology. The fix is to restructure the paper into: exploratory audit development on the original loop; one genuinely confirmatory preregistered protocol on a fresh full loop or fresh candidate-generation cycle; and clearly separate claims for each.

- **The paper’s narrative contains many stale or contradictory claims due to chronological accumulation.** Examples: §Theory says “the null verdict survives … because no audit layer at any threshold and no non-pair-wr measurement assigns lift to the three KEEPs,” but Exp~40 finds 7/18 cross-solver cells above \(0.60\), Exp~47 finds W077/W078 replicate under fresh thresholds, and Exp~15/14 find architectural/citation signals. §Exp~7 says W078 is validated by majority-of-3 then “subsequently overturned,” while later Exp~47 partially restores it. This makes the final claim hard to audit. The fix is a major rewrite around the final evidence state, moving chronological dead ends to an appendix and removing obsolete “final” verdicts.

- **The selection-bias analysis is useful but overinterpreted.** Exp~45 fits an empirical-Bayes Beta prior to the 12 observed inner-loop wrs and simulates drops under remeasurement, concluding that pure selection bias is “statistically sufficient” to explain the L1 drops. But L1 changes the judge family, not just remeasures the same Bernoulli parameter; the 12 candidates are pre-filtered and not exchangeable with arbitrary candidates, as Exp~44 itself notes; prior uncertainty and judge/problem correlations are not propagated. The fix is a hierarchical model with separate candidate, judge-family, problem, and evaluation-set effects, and posterior predictive checks for cross-family drops rather than same-estimand remeasurement.

- **Exp~49 is called “hierarchical Bayesian” but is not actually hierarchical in the main description.** The model described is independent Beta-binomial updating per cell, with some pooling across judge families under a simplifying independence assumption. This matters because the same pids and answer pairs are judged by multiple models, so independence is false and posterior probabilities will be overconfident. The fix is either to rename this as a simple Beta-binomial sensitivity analysis, or fit an actual hierarchical/multilevel model with random effects for candidate, judge, pid, and split.

- **The GSM8K “harm” claims are statistically much stronger than the evidence warrants.** Exp~32 reports 29/30 vs 30/30 for W078 and Exp~42 reports 26/30 vs 28/30 for W076/W077, with different base runs drifting from 30/30 to 28/30. The paper repeatedly says all three KEEPs “harm accuracy,” but differences of 1–2 questions out of 30 are not significant without paired item-level tests and confidence intervals. The fix is to report paired correctness tables and exact McNemar/binomial tests, or phrase these as “small negative point estimates on GSM8K” rather than “harm.”

- **The scaffold and loop substrate are themselves evaluated with the same kind of same-family LLM judging the paper criticizes.** §Contribution 3 and §Ratchet report v20’s \(0.64\) vs v16 and \(0.88\) vs baseline but explicitly state these were same-family judged and not re-audited. Because the audit methodology is attached to this scaffold, the credibility of candidate generation and the claimed testbed strength depend on an unaudited measurement. The fix is to apply at least L1 and sample extension to the scaffold-vs-baseline comparisons, or remove scaffold performance as an evidential pillar.

- **The paper uses arbitrary or weakly justified thresholds and baselines while still drawing rhetorical conclusions from them.** The \(0.60\) inner gate, \(0.55\) fresh L1 replication threshold, \(n_{\text{fire}}\ge 8\), and “random 30%” reference are sometimes described as defaults or calibration points, but they materially determine conclusions. §Stats even reports p-values against the arbitrary 30% reference. For an audit methodology paper, threshold policy is part of the method and needs a principled decision-theoretic or preregistered justification. The fix is to define utility/loss for false accepts/rejects, use continuous posterior decision rules, and avoid p-values against arbitrary baselines.

- **Tie handling and effective sample sizes are not consistently transparent in the most important tables.** §Setup defines ties-excluded wr and §Stats gives tie sensitivity for headline cached cells, but Exp~47’s fresh replication table omits wins/losses/ties and \(n_{\mathrm{eff}}\), despite those cells driving the final claim. In LLM-as-judge audits, tie rates are themselves judge- and condition-dependent and can change conclusions. The fix is to report ext/base/tie counts, effective \(n\), Wilson intervals, and ties-as-half sensitivity for every Exp~47 candidate, including candidates not sent to L1.

- **The “all candidates should survive all six layers if real” criterion is too strong and not tied to an estimand.** The Introduction says “A self-improvement claim that is real should survive all six,” but a wisdom could be genuinely useful for one solver family, domain, or judge-defined quality criterion and not another. Conversely, cross-family disagreement need not imply lack of utility; it may imply different but valid preferences. The fix is to define the target population and estimand for each claim—e.g., “robust across these three judge families on this problem distribution”—rather than equating “real” with passing every stress test.

- **L6 faithfulness proxies are weak and sometimes over-described as direct estimates of content-specific causal effect.** §Theory says L6 “directly estimates \(Z_w^{\mathrm{specific}}\),” but embedding cosine between Turn-0 deltas and wisdom text, and LLM-judged citation strictness, are at best correlational proxies. Exp~17 also shows citation can be inflated to 95% by requiring citations in the output, without showing utility. The fix is to validate L6 on controlled examples with known inserted reasoning directions, placebo wisdoms, and preferably human faithfulness labels.

- **The dynamic same-domain exemplar exposure is a real contamination issue for generalization claims, even if symmetric for base/ext comparisons.** The Limitations state that at solve time the prompt includes a most-similar in-pool pid and cached v15-class answer from the same evaluation pool. Symmetry helps relative wr comparisons, but it weakens claims about held-out generalization, scaffold strength, and fresh evaluation independence. The fix is a clean rerun with strict pool separation and no evaluation-pool exemplars, at least for the key fresh-data replication.

- **The paper is far too long and self-referential for a main-track methodology paper.** Large portions read as a chronological lab notebook: “Strict-reviewer closure,” repeated “strongest result” updates, “first time” claims, intermediate verdicts later overturned, and many small experiments with weak \(n\). This obscures the actual contribution and makes it difficult to verify the final evidential chain. The fix is a substantial condensation: one method section, one exploratory case study, one preregistered confirmatory evaluation, one statistical model, and appendices for the rest.

# Questions to the authors

1. In Exp~47, what are the exact ext/base/tie counts and effective \(n\) for all 12 fresh inner evaluations and all L1 evaluations? Were L1 judgements run for candidates that failed the inner gate, and if not, why not?

2. Can you provide the exact preregistration text, timestamp/hash, and rationale for the laxer \(0.55\) L1 threshold in Exp~47? Was this threshold chosen before any pilot on the 30-pid fresh split or related splits?

3. Why does the Conclusion say the loop was “re-executed from scratch” in Exp~47 when §Exp~47 explicitly says candidate generation, distillation, and pruning were not rerun?

4. If W077 fresh L1 has only \(P(\theta>0.55)=0.546\) under your own Exp~49 analysis, what posterior probability or decision rule justifies labelling it a replication rather than inconclusive?

5. Can you rerun the most important result—Exp~47 or an equivalent preregistered fresh evaluation—at \(n\ge 100\), with L1 judging for all 12 candidates and a predeclared continuous posterior decision rule?

6. Do you have any externally validated positive control where a specific wisdom is known, independently of LLM preference judgements, to improve task outcomes? If not, how should readers estimate the audit stack’s false-negative rate?

7. How much do the final claims change if ties are treated as half-wins rather than excluded for Exp~47 and the high-tie GSM8K subjective-judgement experiments?

8. Can you provide a true hierarchical model with candidate, pid, judge-family, and split effects, rather than independent Beta-binomial cells, and report whether W077/W078 still separate from W076?

# Rating

Weak Reject

The paper addresses an important problem and contains unusually honest, useful exploratory evidence, but it is not yet a clean top-venue methodology contribution. The main rating drivers are the mismatch between “fresh-loop replication” language and the actual fixed-candidate fresh-data evaluation, the underpowered and threshold-sensitive nature of the positive Exp~47 result, the absence of external ground truth or validated positive controls, and the post-hoc/chronological accumulation of audit layers and gate designs. I would be much more positive on a shorter version with a preregistered full fresh loop or \(n\ge100\) fixed-candidate replication, full counts/CIs for Exp~47, and a proper hierarchical model.
