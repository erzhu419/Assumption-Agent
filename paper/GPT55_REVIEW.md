# Summary

The paper presents a single closed-loop LLM “wisdom library” system that proposes, gates, prunes, and audits retrieval-level methodological entries. Its main methodological claim is that a six-layer audit stack—cross-family re-judgment, side reseeding, sample extension, cross-solver tests, fresh-domain/benchmark tests, and faithfulness probes—can diagnose when same-family self-improvement claims are unstable, and that selection-bias modelling plus a preregistered fresh-data re-evaluation separates regression-to-the-mean from true non-replication. Empirically, the original loop keeps 3/12 candidates; cached-data audits mostly push those below the original 0.60 threshold, while the later preregistered Exp. 47 re-evaluation on 30 fresh problems at a laxer L1 threshold recovers 2/3 original KEEPs and one originally rejected candidate.

# Strengths

- **Clear and valuable problem framing: auditing self-improving LLM loops rather than claiming self-improvement works.** The abstract and Introduction repeatedly state that the paper is about audit methodology, not a field-level claim about autonomous improvement. This improves the submission because it targets an important weakness in current LLM-as-judge self-improvement work: same-family gate decisions are often treated as validated improvements.

- **Operational audit stack is concrete and reusable.** The six layers are specified in the abstract, Introduction, and \S3–\S4 with explicit interventions: judge family, side-order seed, sample size, solver family, domain, and non-pairwise faithfulness. Even if individual layers are standard, packaging them as a practical audit checklist for retrieval-level self-improvement claims is a useful methodological contribution.

- **The paper is unusually transparent about negative and mixed results.** Examples include the 0/3 cached-data survival under the original 0.60 threshold, the failure of the trigger-conditioned gate to generalize in Exp. 33, the failed positive controls in Exp. 44/46, the weak W077 fresh L1 evidence in Exp. 49, and the repeated caveats about small \(n\). This honesty strengthens the work relative to many self-improvement papers that only report successful inner-loop gains.

- **Selection-bias analysis is a valuable interpretive correction.** Exp. 45 explicitly shows that the L1 drops are compatible with regression-to-the-mean after selecting the top 3 of 12 noisy \(n=50\) scores. This is important because it prevents the authors’ earlier cross-family-drop story from being overinterpreted as pure “judge fragility.”

- **Preregistered fresh-data re-evaluation is the strongest empirical component.** Exp. 47 holds the original 12 candidates fixed, uses a disjoint 30-pid split, and applies thresholds claimed to be frozen in advance. Although small and threshold-sensitive, it is a meaningful attempt to separate cached-data artifacts from fresh-data replication.

- **Reproducibility effort is substantial.** The main text and stated appendices include code, logs, prompt templates, model identities, temperatures, seeds, proxy details, cost tables, wisdom-record schemas, and registry states. For an LLM-systems methodology paper, this level of provenance is a real strength.

- **The paper identifies several concrete failure modes that are broadly relevant.** Same-family judge instability, side-order sensitivity, hosted-model temporal drift, weak inter-judge \(\kappa\), gate anti-additivity, and faithfulness/utility decoupling are all phenomena that other LLM-loop papers should be forced to confront.

# Weaknesses

- **The central “distinguishes selection-driven drops from genuine non-replication” claim is stronger than the evidence supports.** The abstract, overview figure, and Conclusion state that the audit stack distinguishes selection-driven cached-data drops for W077/W078 from real non-replication for W076. But this conclusion rests on only three original KEEPs, fresh \(n=30\), a laxer L1 threshold of 0.55, and very wide uncertainty intervals; Exp. 49 reports W077 fresh L1 single-cell \(P(\theta>0.55)=0.546\), which is barely above chance. For this class of methodology paper, the distinction between “diagnostic evidence” and “demonstrated classification” matters. Fix: weaken the claim to “suggests” or “is consistent with,” or run a preregistered \(n\geq100\) fresh evaluation under the same 0.60 threshold and all audit layers.

- **The paper remains highly exploratory despite later preregistration.** \S3 states that the full audit stack was not preregistered and grew through staged reviewer simulation; later sections report many experiments, gate variants, thresholds, and analyses through Exp. 49. Exp. 47 is preregistered, but it is performed after extensive exploration of the original candidate set and does not rerun candidate generation. For a top-venue methodology paper, this creates a serious garden-of-forking-paths problem: the audit stack’s apparent success may partly reflect post-hoc layer and threshold choices. Fix: preregister the entire audit protocol before running a new full loop, including candidate generation, pruning, gates, thresholds, and primary estimands.

- **The paper uses threshold-crossing rhetoric despite very weak statistical resolution.** Many labels—“FLIP,” “COLLAPSE,” “REPLICATE,” “HARM,” “NULL”—are assigned at \(n=30\) or \(n=50\), often where Wilson intervals include both parity and the gate threshold. The authors acknowledge this in \S “Binomial confidence intervals” and Exp. 49, but the main narrative still treats point-estimate threshold crossings as substantive categories. This is especially problematic for W077 in Exp. 47: fresh L1 wr \(=0.57\) is called replication, but its interval necessarily includes 0.50 and 0.60. Fix: make posterior/CI-based uncertainty the primary reporting object, predefine stronger evidence categories, and avoid binary labels unless intervals exclude relevant thresholds.

- **The fresh-data replication changes the threshold and therefore changes the estimand.** The cached audit’s headline uses the original 0.60 gate, while Exp. 47 uses an L1 threshold of 0.55. The authors disclose this, but the abstract and Conclusion still combine these results into a single differentiated story. For an audit methodology paper, changing thresholds between cached and fresh evaluations makes “replication” ambiguous. Fix: report parallel results at both 0.55 and 0.60, justify the preregistered 0.55 threshold statistically before any results, and make clear which estimand each threshold targets.

- **Exp. 47 is not a full-loop replication and cannot validate the self-improving loop.** \S Exp. 47 correctly clarifies that candidate generation, success/failure distillation, cross-LLM distillation, and pruning are not rerun. Thus the experiment tests re-evaluation of a fixed candidate set, not whether the autonomous loop would produce reliable KEEPs on fresh data. For this class of paper, the distinction is central because the claimed system is a closed-loop agent. Fix: run a fully preregistered second loop on fresh data and audit its newly generated candidates.

- **L1 in Exp. 47 is only run on inner-gate passers, creating an avoidable selection asymmetry.** Exp. 47 states that L1 was run only on candidates passing the fresh inner gate. This means the paper cannot tell whether fresh inner failures would have passed cross-family L1, nor can it fully characterize false negatives or judge disagreement on the fresh split. This weakens the claim that the audit stack recovers WCAND03 and distinguishes candidate types. Fix: run L1 on all 12 fresh candidates, or predefine and justify the sequential design as the estimand.

- **No human ground truth is provided for the main open-ended Chinese task.** The paper uses cross-family LLM judges and gpt-5.5 structured ratings as substitutes, while explicitly admitting in \S Discussion and Limitations that these are not human annotations. For a methodology paper about auditing LLM-as-judge loops, validating the audit itself against humans or objective outcomes is important. GSM8K is useful but small and not representative of the main Chinese open-ended domain. Fix: include a human evaluation subset, ideally with blinded pairwise and structured ratings, and compare judge-family decisions against human preferences.

- **The selection-bias model in Exp. 45 is too simple to support the paper’s stronger interpretation.** The empirical-Bayes Beta prior is fit to only 12 observed wr values, assumes exchangeability, and abstracts away candidate prefiltering, ties, judge-family changes, prompt interactions, and within-problem correlations. The fact that observed L1 drops fall within a broad simulated 95% range means selection bias is a sufficient possible explanation, not that W077/W078’s cached drops are selection-driven. Fix: present Exp. 45 as a sensitivity/null-model analysis only, or build a hierarchical model over candidates, pids, judges, and candidate-generation stages.

- **The Bayesian/meta-analytic reanalysis in Exp. 49 does not solve the dependence structure.** The DerSimonian–Laird pooling treats judge cells as studies on logit proportions, but the same answer pairs and pids are re-judged across families, creating within-pid and within-candidate dependence. The authors acknowledge that a full hierarchical model over the \(50\times5\times3\) verdict array is future work, but still use the DL posterior to justify replicate/collapse labels. For an audit methodology paper, dependence is not a minor detail; it directly affects uncertainty. Fix: use a per-pid hierarchical logistic model or a cluster bootstrap over pids, with judge and candidate random effects.

- **The theoretical decomposition is explicitly non-identifiable but is sometimes written as if layers cancel specific confounds.** \S “What the A/B gate actually measures” properly says the \(Z^{\mathrm{specific}}, Z^{\mathrm{generic}}, Z^{\mathrm{style}}\) decomposition is informal and not identifiable. However, the subsequent bullet list says L1 “cancels \(J\)-specific stylistic preferences” and that persistent lift “localises to \(Z^{\mathrm{specific}}\),” which contradicts the earlier caveats. For a methodology paper, the estimands should be precise. Fix: remove causal/cancellation language, define each audit layer as testing robustness to a nuisance intervention, and use placebo/known-effect interventions before claiming component attribution.

- **L6 faithfulness is undervalidated and may not measure causal faithfulness.** Exp. 9–14 use embedding deltas, Claude YES/PARTIAL/NO judgments, and retrospective/prospective citation. These can show that outputs differ or cite a wisdom, but not that the wisdom causally improved reasoning. Exp. 17 even shows citation can be forced by prompt format, which weakens citation as a faithfulness proxy. Fix: validate L6 on synthetic tasks with known inserted reasoning effects, include length-matched placebo wisdoms, and obtain human faithfulness labels.

- **The positive-control story is unresolved.** Exp. 44 and Exp. 46 construct six controls, including supposedly useful controls and a base-library duplicate, but all fail badly. The authors then say Exp. 47 demonstrates sensitivity because some original candidates pass on fresh data. This does not replace a known-positive control: those candidates are not independently known to be useful. For an audit-stack methodology paper, demonstrating both specificity and sensitivity is important. Fix: design a synthetic or leave-one-out task where a known intervention should improve performance, then verify the audit accepts it.

- **Held-out exposure and exemplar use are concerning despite the “symmetric” argument.** The Limitations section says the solver dynamically selects same-domain exemplars from the evaluation pool’s other pids, with cached v15-class answer text, and that this applies symmetrically to base and ext. Symmetry does not guarantee neutrality because the new wisdom can interact with exposed exemplars, retrieval, and prompt structure. The gate also initially describes mining exemplars from the held-out 50, while the Limitations later says the relevant candidate exemplar file contains zero WCAND/WCROSS entries; this is confusing. Fix: rerun the key gate and audit experiments with strict pool separation and document the exact exemplar path used for each candidate.

- **Reproducibility is limited by unverifiable hosted model/proxy behavior.** \S Limitations states that model access is through the ruoli.dev proxy and exact-token reproduction depends on proxy routing state. The paper also documents large temporal drift even at temperature 0.0. This is a major issue for a paper whose claims depend on small differences around 0.55–0.60. Fix: use official vendor endpoints or open models for a confirmatory run, cache all prompts/outputs/verdicts, and define which claims are reproducible from cached artifacts versus by rerunning models.

- **The manuscript is overgrown and internally hard to follow.** The main body contains dozens of experiments, chronological reversals, intermediate verdicts later overturned, “strict-reviewer closure” language, repeated “preregistered preregistered” typos, and several boxed library deltas under different thresholds. This makes it difficult to identify the primary confirmatory result and the final estimand. For a top-venue paper, clarity and parsimony are part of scientific rigor. Fix: move most chronological experiments to appendices, foreground 2–3 primary hypotheses and analyses, and clearly separate exploratory, preregistered, and post-hoc sections.

# Questions to the authors

1. What is the exact timestamp/hash of `PREREGISTRATION_v2.md`, and what experiments/results were already known when the 0.55 L1 replication threshold was chosen?

2. For Exp. 47, what are the Wilson intervals and posterior probabilities for all three passers under both the 0.55 and 0.60 L1 thresholds? Would the headline “2/3 original KEEPs replicate” survive if the original 0.60 threshold were used for L1?

3. Why was fresh L1 in Exp. 47 run only on inner-gate passers? Can you report L1 results for all 12 candidates on the fresh split?

4. Can you provide any blinded human evaluation on a subset of the open-ended Chinese answer pairs, especially for W077/W078 fresh pass cases and W076 collapse cases?

5. How can readers verify the exact model identities behind `gemini-3-flash`, `claude-haiku-4.5/opus-4.6`, `gpt-5.4/5.5` through the ruoli.dev proxy? Are official-endpoint reruns available for any headline cells?

6. What is the primary estimand of the paper: survival at the original 0.60 gate, survival at the preregistered 0.55 L1 replication threshold, cross-family average utility, or fresh-loop library delta?

7. Can you construct a known-positive control where the audit stack should accept the intervention independently of the original candidate set, e.g. a synthetic task family or a leave-one-out removal/reinsertion of an actually useful base-library wisdom?

8. How much do the key results change under a cluster bootstrap over pids rather than binomial/DL analyses that treat many verdicts as effectively independent?

# Rating

Weak Reject

The work is interesting and unusually transparent, and I would like to see this line of auditing developed further. However, the main evidential claim is still too strong for the data: the decisive “distinguishes selection-driven drops from genuine non-replication” story rests on only three original KEEPs, a small \(n=30\) fresh split, a laxer threshold, and uncertain LLM-judge labels. The audit stack and many analyses are largely exploratory/post-hoc, and the paper lacks human or known-positive validation of the audit methodology. I also found the manuscript internally overextended, with many threshold-dependent intermediate conclusions that make the final estimand hard to pin down.
