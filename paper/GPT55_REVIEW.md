# Summary

The paper presents a case study of an autonomous retrieval-level “wisdom library” loop: an LLM proposes compact methodological priors, validates them using a same-family LLM A/B gate at \(n=50\) with a \(+10\)pp threshold, and commits accepted entries. The core empirical claim is negative: the gate accepted 3/12 candidates, but these accepts did not survive cross-family re-judgment, larger fresh-data replications at \(n=100\) and \(n=200\), or selection-bias analysis. The authors use this calibrated failure to argue that same-family LLM-as-judge gates are inadequate self-validation mechanisms, and to motivate a broader six-stage architecture for self-hypothesizing/self-validating agents.

# Strengths

- **Honest negative result with scoped claims** (§Introduction “What we found”; §Experiments; §Conclusion). The paper repeatedly distinguishes “no candidate meets the preregistered acceptance rule” from “no candidate has any utility,” e.g. noting that \(W_{078}\) reaches inner \(0.590\) at \(n=200\) but remains below threshold. This restraint materially strengthens the submission because the main empirical result is a null, and the authors avoid overclaiming zero effect.

- **Strong attention to selection effects and winner’s curse** (§3.3 “Selection bias”). The empirical-Bayes top-3-of-12 simulation directly addresses the most obvious confound: the original accepts were selected from noisy \(n=50\) measurements. The reported drops falling at the 48th–72nd percentiles of the selection-only distribution are an important calibration and prevent a simplistic “cross-judge drop = judge bias” interpretation.

- **Preregistered fresh-data follow-ups are a major strength** (§3.5). The paper does not rely only on post-hoc cached audits; it reports three fresh-data replications: \(n=100\) without exemplars, \(n=100\) with exemplars, and \(n=200\) with exemplars under a fresh seed. This is the strongest evidence in the paper and substantially improves credibility relative to many LLM-as-judge studies.

- **The paper explicitly documents post-hoc aspects rather than hiding them** (§Experiments caveat; §Discussion “Objections we have not closed”). The authors acknowledge that the audit stack was not preregistered, that gate-design freedom exists, and that some thresholds/designs were informed by prior runs. This transparency is valuable for a methodology/case-study paper.

- **Useful decomposition of failure modes for same-family A/B gates** (§2.5 “What the A/B gate actually measures”). The Cause A/B/C taxonomy—specific content, generic extra context, and judge stylistic preference—is not identifiable, as the authors state, but it provides a clear threat model for interpreting LLM-judged self-improvement claims.

- **Positive and negative controls partly address “the gate always rejects” concerns** (§3.6, Exp 67–69). The Bayesian-template and reasoning-suppression controls show that the same judge machinery can strongly prefer a substantive intervention and reject a harmful one, with cross-family agreement. This strengthens the interpretation that the original 0/12 is content failure rather than an entirely broken audit apparatus.

- **Reproducibility posture appears unusually strong** (§Method registry; §Conclusion reproducibility; appendices described by prompt). The versioned registry, log provenance, prompt templates, model identities, seeds, proxy details, compute/cost table, and released code/logs are appropriate for this kind of case study and make the work more auditable than typical LLM agent papers.

- **The authors separate audit-stack novelty from standard statistical hygiene** (§Discussion “What is actually novel”). The paper does not pretend that sample extension, side-randomization, or cross-solver tests are new methods; it positions the contribution as bundling and advocacy backed by a concrete null result. This is a mature framing.

# Weaknesses

- **Single-loop, single-domain case study does not support the breadth of the normative conclusion** (§Introduction; §Conclusion recommendations). The evidence comes primarily from one Chinese open-ended wisdom-library loop using one main solver family and a specific retrieval/scaffold design. Yet the conclusion recommends a fairly broad reporting standard for “any future paper proposing retrieval-level self-improvement,” including cross-family re-judgment, selection-bias modeling, preregistered \(n\ge100\) fresh evaluation, hierarchical Bayes, and pool-separation sensitivity. For a top-venue methodology paper, a single instantiation is too narrow to justify such broad prescriptions. This would be fixed by applying the audit stack to multiple independently designed loops, at least one non-Chinese/English loop, and at least one non-wisdom retrieval/prompt optimization system.

- **The main architectural roadmap is much more speculative than the empirical case study** (§4 “Six-Stage Roadmap”; §4.1 stage validation). The paper’s strongest contribution is the audit of a failed gate; the six-stage self-hypothesizing architecture is largely conceptual and only weakly supported by small synthetic probes. Some claims—e.g. “None of these is optional” in the conclusion—are not established by the experiments. To fix this, the paper should either sharply demote the roadmap to discussion/future work or provide stronger empirical evidence that omitting each stage causes failure in a realistic loop.

- **Internal inconsistency in reporting how many roadmap stages “work”** (§4.1 table and following paragraph). Table 1 labels Stage 0 “works,” Stage 2 “works,” Stage 3 “minimal proxy passes; formal alignment not tested,” Stage 1 “conditional,” Stage 0.5 “fails,” and Stage 4 “fails.” The paragraph immediately below says “Three stages work (priors library, experience feedback, alignment),” but the table explicitly says formal alignment is not tested. This overstates the Stage 3 result. The fix is to consistently describe Stage 3 as “a surface-language invariance proxy passes” rather than as a working alignment layer.

- **The paper overweights LLM-judge agreement as evidence of substantive correctness in controls** (§3.6 Exp 68–69). In Exp 68/69, the Bayesian template achieves high pairwise win rates while objective accuracy is already at ceiling or near-ceiling: BASE \(14/15\) or \(15/15\), POSITIVE \(15/15\), and gpt-5.4-mini \(\Delta_{\mathrm{acc}}=0\). The authors acknowledge that this may detect rigor rather than correctness, but still use it to argue the gate detects “substantively-different reasoning.” For this class of paper, separating preference for explicit reasoning from actual task improvement is central. A stronger positive control would require a verifiable task/solver pair with substantial headroom and a prior that increases objective accuracy, not merely judged rigor.

- **The original scaffold-vs-baseline performance claim is not independently audited despite motivating the setup** (§Introduction around Fig. 2; Contribution 3). The paper states that the v20 scaffold rises from \(74\%\) to \(86\%\), and elsewhere \(0.88\) vs baseline on 50 held-out problems, but then notes these were themselves same-family judged and should be read under the standard convention. Because the entire loop depends on this scaffold as the substrate, leaving its gain unaudited weakens the case that the system is a meaningful testbed rather than a judge-preference artifact. The fix is to apply at least L1/L3 and preferably objective or human evaluation to the scaffold-vs-baseline comparison.

- **The held-out exemplar mechanism creates a serious interpretability problem even if applied symmetrically** (§Limitations “Some held-out exposure”; §3.5). The solver retrieves same-domain examples from the held-out evaluation pool itself. Symmetry between base and ext does not guarantee cancellation, because the candidate wisdom may interact with the retrieved exemplar differently from the base library. The authors’ own \(n=100\) with/without exemplar comparison shows large boosts of \(0.06\)--\(0.18\), confirming nontrivial interaction. A clean fix is to rerun the full original gate and audit with strict pool separation from the start, not only as a sensitivity after the fact.

- **The empirical-Bayes selection-bias model appears statistically degenerate and needs more careful treatment** (§3.3). The fitted prior concentration \(\hat\alpha+\hat\beta \approx 6\times10^7\) implies almost no between-candidate heterogeneity, which the authors interpret as tight clustering. But with only 12 candidates and \(n=50\), such an extreme MLE concentration is likely a boundary/identifiability artifact rather than a reliable estimate of the population distribution. This matters because the selection-bias simulation is used to calibrate the drops. A fix would be to report sensitivity to reasonable hyperpriors or bounded concentration priors, and to show whether conclusions hold under less degenerate between-candidate variance.

- **The hierarchical Bayesian analysis is retained but no longer contributes clear inferential value** (§3.4). The authors say the \(n=30\) hierarchical fit was overconfident and falsified by later data, and that it is “an error analysis, not a contribution.” Including it in the main empirical narrative risks confusing readers and inflating the perceived methodological scope. For a cleaner paper, this should be shortened substantially in the main body and moved to an appendix, with only the lesson summarized.

- **The audit stack itself is post-hoc, and the preregistered fresh runs do not fully preregister the whole discovery process** (§Experiments caveat; §Discussion objections). The strongest fresh-data evidence evaluates the original 12 candidates after the audit design has evolved; it does not rerun candidate generation, selection, and auditing under a fully preregistered protocol. For a methodology paper about auditing self-improvement loops, this leaves open whether the audit procedure would have the same behavior prospectively on newly generated candidates. The fix is a fully preregistered second loop from candidate proposal through final audit.

- **The “random-inclusion procedure would deliver ~30%” reference is unclear and unsupported** (Contribution 5). The paper says neither the trigger-conditioned gate’s \(4/21\) nor naive gate’s \(3/21\) pass rate is above the “\(\sim30\%\) reference an arbitrary random-inclusion procedure would deliver.” It is not clear why random inclusion should yield 30%, what population it refers to, or how it is estimated. This should either be derived, cited to a specific experiment, or removed.

- **Some terminology and framing are rhetorically inflated relative to evidence** (Abstract; Introduction; §4). Phrases such as “long-standing target in AI,” “the surface form…without the substance,” “None of these is optional,” and analogies like “Doctor Strange’s time stone” are vivid but not appropriate for a top-venue empirical methodology paper unless backed by stronger formal or empirical support. The fix is to tighten the prose and make the claims more technical and less manifesto-like.

- **The related-work comparison is too checkbox-based** (§Related Work Table 1). The table records whether papers report L1/L3/L4/L6 but does not deeply compare acceptance signals, evaluation settings, or whether cross-family judging is the right audit for each. Some listed systems use environment rewards or weight updates, and the authors mark them n/a, but the surrounding discussion still gestures broadly toward the self-improvement literature. A stronger related-work section would analyze which audit threats apply to which class of systems and avoid implying that absence of L1 is uniformly problematic.

- **L6 faithfulness remains underdeveloped and should not be positioned as part of the reliable audit stack** (§2.5; §Discussion L6). The authors admit embedding-direction alignment and citation strictness are weak proxies and unvalidated. Since L6 is part of the advertised six-layer stack, its weakness dilutes the methodological contribution. The fix is to either validate L6 on synthetic known-causal examples with human labels or clearly separate it as exploratory rather than a recommended default audit layer.

- **Sample sizes in several auxiliary claims are too small for the interpretations drawn** (§3.6 controls; §5 multi-step pilot; §4.1 synthetic roadmap). Exp 67 uses 16 problems, Exp 68/69 use 15, the multi-step pilot uses \(N=3\), and the roadmap validation uses 60 synthetic problems across four families. These are useful diagnostics, but not enough to strongly validate audit-stack sensitivity, sequential-task generality, or stage-level architectural claims. The fix is to treat these as preliminary and avoid using them to buttress central claims, or scale them substantially.

- **The paper is overlong and contains too many experiment IDs and historical detours in the main body** (§3, §Discussion). The detailed chronology is useful for transparency but obscures the main contribution. For a top ML venue, the main paper should prioritize the final design, final preregistered evidence, and clean statistical analyses, moving the lab-notebook sequence, failed intermediate gates, and detailed historical evolution to appendices.

# Questions to the authors

1. Can you rerun a fully preregistered second closed-loop cycle—candidate generation, gate, and audit—under the final audit protocol, rather than only reevaluating the original 12 candidates?

2. How sensitive is the top-3-of-12 selection-bias conclusion to the empirical-Bayes prior concentration? In particular, what happens under bounded or weakly informative hyperpriors that prevent \(\alpha+\beta\) from going to \(10^7\)?

3. Would the main 0/12 conclusion hold under a clean evaluation pool with no same-domain held-out exemplar retrieval anywhere in the original gate, rather than as a later ablation?

4. Can you provide a positive control on a verifiable task where the inserted prior produces a substantial objective accuracy gain, not just a high LLM-preference win rate for more explicit reasoning?

5. What happens if the scaffold-vs-baseline \(74\%\to86\%\) or \(0.88\) win-rate claim is audited with the same L1/L3 machinery? Does the scaffold advantage survive cross-family re-judgment and sample extension?

6. For the \(n=200\) run where \(W_{078}\) reaches inner \(0.590\) and L1 \(0.573\), what is the posterior probability that it exceeds the joint decision threshold under a preregistered hierarchical model including exemplar covariates?

7. How much of the original candidate-generation process depends on Chinese cultural aphorism sources versus generic methodological prompts? Would an English-only loop generate candidates of comparable quality and failure profile?

# Rating

**Weak Reject**

The paper is unusually transparent and contains a valuable negative result: a same-family \(n=50\) LLM A/B gate accepted 3/12 candidates that fail to survive larger and cross-family audits. However, the contribution is still primarily a single-loop case study, while the paper makes broader methodological and architectural claims that are not sufficiently supported. The most serious weaknesses are the narrow empirical base, the post-hoc audit-stack development, the unresolved held-out exemplar interaction, and the speculative six-stage roadmap being presented too prominently relative to its evidence. I would be much more positive on a shorter version focused tightly on the preregistered fresh replications and selection-bias analysis, or on an expanded version applying the final audit protocol prospectively to multiple loops.
