\begin{abstract}
\noindent\textbf{This is a paper about audit methodology, not about
whether self-improving LLM loops work.} The empirical headline is a
\emph{null result}: after building and running a complete autonomous
wisdom-curation loop, \emph{zero} of its committed wisdoms survive a
six-layer independent audit. The scientific contribution is the audit
stack that produced this verdict, and the demonstration that naive
single-family A/B gates — the default in the current self-improvement
literature — can be confidently wrong about their own decisions.

\noindent\textbf{Setup.} Large language models improve markedly when
given retrieval-augmented \emph{methodological scaffolding}: problem
reframes, anti-patterns, evaluation criteria, exemplars of parallel
prior reasoning. A natural next step is an agent that \emph{curates
its own} library of such methodological wisdoms without a human in
the loop. We build this loop as a concrete testbed: a strong scaffolded
solver (v20, \(+28\)pp vs.\ the v16 champion on 100 problems, \(+76\)pp
vs.\ budget-matched baseline on 50 held-out) plus four orchestration
modules (failure generator, success distiller, cross-model distiller,
Darwinian pruner), gated by a \(+10\)pp held-out A/B test.

\noindent\textbf{Inner-loop result.} Over one cycle the library grows
\(75 \to 77 \to 58_{\text{active}} + 19_{\text{deprecated}} \to
59_{\text{active}} + 19_{\text{deprecated}}\). Three new wisdoms
commit (\(W_{076}, W_{077}, W_{078}\)); nine candidates refuse;
nineteen stale entries deprecate. Only \(3/12\) proposals pass — the
gate appears to be doing real work.

\noindent\textbf{The six-layer audit stack.} We then stress-test each
KEEP decision along six \emph{independent} axes: (L1)~cross-family
re-judgment with a different LLM family; (L2)~side-randomization
reseed at fixed content; (L3)~sample extension from \(n=50\) to
\(n=100\); (L4)~cross-solver replication across three families;
(L5)~fresh-domain cross-family re-test; (L6)~embedding-space and
LLM-judged faithfulness of the perturbation direction. These layers
share no data with one another by construction. A self-improvement
claim that is real should survive all six.

\noindent\textbf{Audit verdict: \(\mathbf{0/3}\) KEEPs survive.}
\(W_{076}\) falls from \(0.64\) (inner-loop) to \(0.40\) under
cross-family judge. \(W_{077}\) falls \(0.60 \to 0.47\).
\(W_{078}\) falls \(0.60 \to 0.51\). At \(n=100\) two of three
stabilise below \(0.55\); the third at \(0.57\). Pair-wr
itself is orthogonally falsified: embedding-faithfulness and
LLM-faithfulness both show wisdoms cause real perturbations
(magnitude \([0.71,0.77]\)) but in directions \emph{unrelated}
to what the wisdom self-advertises. On 30 GSM8K problems,
\(W_{078}\) actively \emph{harms} accuracy (29/30 vs 30/30 base).
\textbf{Final validated library delta on external data:
\(\boxed{+0}\).} The three KEEPs fail every single audit axis.

\noindent\textbf{Architectural recovery attempt and its limits.} We
then redesign the gate: decompose pair-wr into three orthogonal
components — trigger-fit, conditional utility, citability — and
combine them as a trigger-conditioned gate (Exp 15). On the original
12 candidates this rescues \(4/12\) (vs \(3/12\) for the naive gate),
including two REVERTs the naive gate missed. But scaling to
\(22\) candidates (Exp 33) yields \(3/22\) PASS at the same
threshold — below the \(30\%\) random-baseline rate. The
architectural redesign recovers \emph{local} positive signal on
the original 12 but does not generalize. We report this honestly
as a partial recovery, not a fix.

\noindent\textbf{Strict-reviewer closure (Exp 25--33).} Nine further
experiments each target a specific publication objection with
concrete evidence, including: 3-family labeling reliability
(\(\kappa\)); researcher/random/always-accept gate baselines (agent
gate F1 \(= 0.40\) beats all); cluster-threshold relaxation
(12 \(\to\) 22 candidates, 83\% increase); and GSM8K standard-benchmark
portability. The single-family self-adjudication failure replicates
across every independent axis.

\noindent\textbf{Contribution.} (i)~The six-layer audit stack, each
layer specified operationally enough to run against any future
self-improving LLM loop. (ii)~Evidence that the default inner-loop
gate can be confidently wrong about \(3/3\) of its own decisions.
(iii)~A concrete testbed (code, 22 candidates, 150 answer pairs,
cross-family logs) on which others can test alternative audit
designs. (iv)~A partial architectural recovery (trigger-conditioned
gate) that locally rescues signal but does not generalize,
demonstrating the audit stack is harder to satisfy than a gate is
to redesign. We argue cross-family re-judgment \emph{and} at least
one non-pair-wr faithfulness axis should become routine reporting
items for any self-improvement claim.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

A large language model asked to ``just solve it'' will typically
underperform a version of itself given a well-matched \emph{cognitive
prosthesis}: a problem reframe, a list of anti-patterns, the memory of
two superficially different but structurally parallel prior cases, and
an evaluation rubric. The gap between the raw solver and the scaffolded
solver is usually attributed to chain-of-thought~\citep{wei2022chain},
retrieval-augmented generation~\citep{lewis2020rag}, self-reflection
and verification~\citep{madaan2023selfrefine, shinn2023reflexion}, or
agentic decomposition~\citep{yao2023react}. We call the artifact that
these methods all point to a \emph{wisdom}: a small piece of
methodological knowledge of the form ``in situations exhibiting pattern
\(P\), the right move is \(M\); the canonical failure mode is \(F\).''

A working \emph{wisdom library} is a retrieval corpus of such entries,
selected per problem, foregrounded in the prompt, and paired with
cross-domain exemplars that show what the wisdom looks like when
instantiated in a specific domain. In our setting (see
\S\ref{sec:method}), we use \(75\) such wisdoms, each authored in the
classical tradition (aphorisms from \emph{Yi Jing}, Munger, Drucker,
Mao, Confucius, folk proverbs, etc.) and unpacked into LLM-legible form.
Over six iterative framework revisions (v13 through v20, see
Figure~\ref{fig:arch}), such a scaffolded solver rises from \(74\%\) to
\(86\%\) win rate against a budget-matched baseline.

The limits of this approach, however, are exactly the limits of human
library curation. Every wisdom that is missed is a residual—a
systematic failure mode the solver walks into repeatedly. Every wisdom
that is wrong becomes dead weight, consuming retrieval slots. And every
wisdom that is \emph{nearly right but poorly phrased} costs the solver
one full pass of the scaffold.

\textbf{The obvious question} — can the agent itself curate its wisdom
library? — has an answer the current literature is already willing
to supply: yes, given a reasonable scaffold and a held-out A/B gate.
\textbf{The question this paper asks instead is the next one: when a
self-improvement loop reports that \(k\) new entries passed its gate,
should we believe it?} Our finding is that under default
reporting conventions the answer is consistently \emph{no}, and that
detecting this failure mode requires audit machinery no single-family
self-adjudicating loop can produce on its own.

Concretely, we build a loop that does everything the literature asks
for (detect residuals, propose entries, filter against a \(+10\)pp
held-out gate, prune, cross-model distill) and then subject each of
its reported KEEP decisions to a six-layer audit stack. Each layer
attacks a different failure mode and shares no data with the others:

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{(L\arabic*)}, leftmargin=*, itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Cross-family re-judgment} — re-score the identical
    answer pairs with a different LLM family (Claude Opus 4.6).
  \item \textbf{Side-randomization reseed} — re-run the same judge
    family with a different A/B side-shuffle seed; content held fixed.
  \item \textbf{Sample extension} — extend surviving candidates from
    \(n = 50\) to \(n = 100\) fresh problems.
  \item \textbf{Cross-solver replication} — regenerate answers with
    three different solver families.
  \item \textbf{Fresh-domain cross-family} — a domain-matched but
    previously-unseen problem pool, re-judged by the full three-family
    panel; and a standard-benchmark port (GSM8K).
  \item \textbf{Faithfulness, orthogonal to pair-wr} — embedding-space
    direction of perturbation and LLM-judged citation strictness,
    both measured on data with zero overlap with the gate signal.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{We report a null result under this audit stack.} The
inner loop produces \(3/12\) KEEPs (gate-positive at \(n=50\),
gemini-judged). After the audit: \(\mathbf{0/3}\) KEEPs
survive any layer, let alone all six. Final validated library delta:
\(\boxed{+0}\). Our contributions are:

\paragraph{Contribution 1 — The six-layer audit stack (primary).} We
specify each layer operationally enough to run against any future
self-improving LLM loop. Each layer is both (i)~cheap to compute on
top of an existing inner-loop artifact and (ii)~capable of
independently invalidating a KEEP decision that the inner-loop gate
was confident about. Cross-family re-judgment (L1) alone flips \(3/3\)
of our loop's KEEPs.

\paragraph{Contribution 2 — Evidence that the default inner-loop
gate is confidently wrong about its own decisions.} Under a
\(+10\)pp single-family A/B at \(n=50\), our loop commits three
wisdoms from disjoint evidence sources; the gate refuses \(9/12\)
proposals (so the gate is not permissive). Yet the three it
\emph{accepts} lose \(20\), \(13\), and \(9\) percentage points
respectively when re-judged cross-family on identical content. On
GSM8K the strongest survivor (\(W_{078}\)) \emph{actively harms}
accuracy. The loop was not dishonest; the gate's measurement is
simply not what it advertises.

\paragraph{Contribution 3 — A strong scaffold as instrument.} The
audit stack only has bite if attached to a scaffold strong enough
to produce candidates in the first place. Our v20 scaffold reaches
\(+28\)pp vs.\ the v16 case-library champion on 100 problems and
\(+76\)pp vs.\ a budget-matched baseline on 50 held-out, with
held-out advantage \emph{larger} than training-split advantage. This
is the substrate on which the audit stack is run; the audit claims
are scaffold-independent.

\paragraph{Contribution 4 — An honest evolution trajectory and its
falsification.} The library moves through
\(75 \to 77 \to 58_{\text{active}} + 19_{\text{deprecated}} \to
59_{\text{active}} + 19_{\text{deprecated}}\) producing three new
wisdoms (W076 \emph{Change with the times}, W077 \emph{No
investigation, no right to speak}, W078 \emph{Is it a mule or a
horse—take it out for a test}). Each is documented with source,
signal, unpacking, exemplars, and evidence trail. Each is
subsequently falsified by the audit stack.

\paragraph{Contribution 5 — A partial architectural recovery that does
not generalize.} Having falsified the naive pair-wr gate, we decompose
it into three independent components (trigger-fit, conditional utility,
citability) and build a trigger-conditioned replacement gate
(\S\ref{sec:trigger-gate}). On the original 12 candidates this
locally rescues \(4/12\), including two REVERTs the naive gate had
missed. But when we scale to 22 candidates the same gate PASSes
only \(3/22\) — below the random-baseline rate of \(\sim\!30\%\).
The architectural redesign recovers signal on the seen set but not
on a superset. \emph{The audit stack is harder to satisfy than a gate
is to redesign.} We report this honestly rather than presenting the
rescued \(4/12\) as the final result.

\paragraph{Contribution 6 — The loop's own introspective bandwidth
is real but insufficient.} A self-rebuttal step run \emph{before} the
cross-family judge predicted exactly the failure mode the cross-judge
then confirmed (all three KEEPs returned \texttt{further\_test} with
judge-preference alignment as the \#1 confound). A separate
meta-audit step diagnoses the inner-loop gate as measurement-fragile
in the \([0.54, 0.62]\) gray zone and proposes a three-tier
replacement. The loop can recognize its own failure modes; it
cannot, without external machinery, produce the disjoint evidence
needed to settle them. This bounds what self-adjudication can
deliver even under honest introspection.

\paragraph{Contribution 7 — Testbed and artifact release.} We release
the full loop (four orchestrator modules, 22 candidates with their
evidence trails, \(3 \times 50 = 150\) cross-family answer pairs,
and the six-layer audit harness) so that alternative audit designs,
stricter gates, or new self-improvement architectures can be
evaluated on the same substrate. Log provenance is preserved end-to-end.

\paragraph{Scope and non-claims.} We do not claim our loop is
representative of all self-improvement schemes — it is one concrete
instantiation designed to be auditable. We do not claim the audit
stack is complete — L6 (faithfulness) in particular is the newest
layer and admits many operationalizations. We do claim that
\emph{a loop reporting ``\(k\) new entries passed the gate'' is not
a publishable result} without at least L1 (cross-family re-judgment)
attached, and that for this class of claim the defaults of the
current literature are systematically too permissive.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Retrieval-augmented and in-context methods.} Chain-of-thought
prompting~\citep{wei2022chain} established that surfacing explicit
reasoning helps; self-consistency~\citep{wang2023selfcons} stabilized
it; reflection/verification~\citep{madaan2023selfrefine,
shinn2023reflexion} closed a one-step inner loop. RAG over document
stores~\citep{lewis2020rag, borgeaud2022retro} and agentic
tool-use~\citep{yao2023react, schick2024toolformer} extended the input
context. Our scaffold (v20) is a retrieval-augmented method over a
\emph{methodological} corpus rather than a factual one.

\paragraph{Self-improvement of language models.}
STaR~\citep{zelikman2022star, zelikman2024star}, self-rewarding
LMs~\citep{yuan2024selfrewarding}, self-play fine-tuning and
instruction-backtranslation~\citep{chen2024selfplay, li2024backtrans}
all close an outer loop at the \emph{weights} level. We close an outer
loop at the \emph{retrieval corpus} level, which is orders of magnitude
cheaper and allows the changes to be auditable.

\paragraph{Case-based reasoning and skill libraries.} Case-based
reasoning~\citep{aamodt1994cbr} and skill-library
agents~\citep{wang2023voyager, zhu2023ghost, sumers2024agentkb} both
maintain a growing store of reusable units. Voyager~\citep{wang2023voyager}
grows a skill library from procedural success in Minecraft; our
setting grows a \emph{methodological} library from A/B-judged open
reasoning. AgentKB~\citep{sumers2024agentkb} focuses on task-specific
routines, whereas we target \emph{domain-general orientations}
(\(\leq 35\)-character aphorisms with cross-domain exemplars).

\paragraph{Curriculum and program synthesis of thoughts.} Automated
discovery of reasoning structures via Self-Discover
\citep{zhou2024selfdiscover} generates per-problem module stacks; Tree
of Thoughts~\citep{yao2023tot} branches the search. Our library
\emph{outlives} any single problem and is acted on by the solver at
retrieval time, not at generation time. We view this as a complementary
axis: one can always add orientation-level retrieval on top of a
better-synthesized per-problem structure.

\paragraph{Darwinian / evolutionary approaches.} Evolutionary prompt
optimization~\citep{promptbreeder, fernando2023promptbreeder} uses a
selection signal on prompts themselves. We share the Darwinian framing
but select at the \emph{unit-of-retrieval} granularity (one wisdom),
which allows finer-grained credit assignment and interpretable audit.

\paragraph{Cross-model distillation.} Using a stronger model as a
teacher is classical~\citep{hinton2015distillation} and modern at the
instruction-tuning level~\citep{taori2023alpaca, peng2024evolinstruct}.
Our \emph{cross-LLM distiller} (\S\ref{sec:cross-llm}) distills not
answers but \emph{orientations}: what the stronger model's
answer-structure foregrounds that the weaker model's scaffolding does
not.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Preliminaries: the scaffolded solver (v20)}
\label{sec:solver}

We use \emph{gemini-3-flash} as the underlying
generator\footnote{Exposed via the ruoli.dev proxy; our pipeline does
not depend on any property specific to this model.} and an independent
LLM judge (same family) to produce A/B verdicts. A \textbf{wisdom}
\(w\) is a record
\[
  w = (\text{id}, \text{aphorism}, \text{source}, \text{signal},
      \text{unpacked\_for\_llm}, \text{cross\_domain\_examples}).
\]
At inference time a retriever selects the top \(K=2\) wisdoms for
problem \(p\). The solver then runs a three-turn procedure:

\begin{description}[leftmargin=1.5em, topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt]
  \item[\textbf{Turn 0 (frame + rewrite).}] A single JSON call produces
  \((\text{frame}, \text{critical\_reframe}, \text{anti\_patterns},
  \text{evaluation\_criteria}, \text{rewritten\_problem},
  \text{what\_changed})\). The ``rewritten problem'' is the
  subject-of-attention the solver will then see.
  \item[\textbf{Turn 1 (execute).}] The solver is given a PRIMARY FRAME
  block (not a ``context bag''), the rewritten problem, retrieved
  wisdoms with per-wisdom same-domain + cross-domain exemplars, and an
  attention-prior block derived from the problem domain/difficulty
  cluster.
  \item[\textbf{Turn 2 (audit).}] A brief self-reflection pass asks
  whether the draft violates the frame's anti-patterns or fails its
  evaluation criteria, and patches accordingly.
\end{description}

\paragraph{Why this matters for the library loop.} Turn 0 produces a
log of \texttt{what\_changed} fields—the solver's own articulation,
per problem, of ``what is this problem really about.'' These are the
signals the \emph{success distiller} (\S\ref{sec:success}) mines.

\subsection{The library as mutable state}
\label{sec:registry}

The library is a versioned registry whose entries track
\texttt{status} \(\in \{\text{active, deprecated, removed}\}\),
\texttt{activation\_count}, \texttt{last\_activated},
\texttt{contribution\_gain}, \texttt{source}, and a \texttt{keep\_reason}
string. Every mutation bumps a semantic version
(\texttt{v20.0} \(\to\) \texttt{v20.1} \(\to \ldots\)). The registry
is the ground truth an auditor would check to verify that an alleged
library change was actually made against a specific A/B outcome.

\subsection{Closed-loop orchestrator}
\label{sec:orchestrator}

Figure~\ref{fig:loop} shows the four-component loop. Each batch of
problems flows through the solver; the signals captured are: judge
verdicts (for failure mining), Turn-0 reframes (for success mining),
retrieval selections (for pruning), and cross-variant gaps (for
cross-model mining).

\paragraph{Candidate generation.} Three generators run asynchronously
against a shared buffer of recent signals:

\subsubsection{Success distiller}
\label{sec:success}

Given \(N\) recent Turn-0 outputs, we embed the concatenation
\texttt{what\_changed || critical\_reframe} with a multilingual
sentence encoder~\citep{reimers2019sbert} and apply agglomerative
clustering with cosine-distance threshold \(0.4\) and minimum cluster
size \(6\). For each surviving cluster, a GPT-class prompter reads
\(\leq 10\) cluster members plus the current library summary and
either emits \texttt{\{"proposal": null, "reason": ...\}} or a complete
wisdom record with rationale and cluster-coverage PIDs. We measure
novelty by maximum cosine similarity of the proposed
\texttt{unpacked\_for\_llm} embedding against all active entries; we
reject when \(\text{sim} > 0.78\). The rationale: wisdom candidates
are \emph{latent orientations in the solver's own reasoning}, surfaced
by repetition, not by failure.

\subsubsection{Failure generator}
\label{sec:failure}

A symmetric generator listens to judge verdicts and accumulates
problems where the solver lost or tied into a failure buffer. When the
buffer size passes a threshold, a prompter asks: ``do these
\(\geq 3\) problems share a systematic residual pattern a new wisdom
could address?'' This generator is conservative; in our run it never
fired a proposal (buffer grew \(4 \to 9 \to 11 \to 13\); threshold
\(15\)). We treat this as informative and discuss it in
\S\ref{sec:discussion}.

\subsubsection{Cross-LLM distiller}
\label{sec:cross-llm}

For each problem where the solver's answer lost to a comparison
variant's answer by a judge margin \(\geq 1\), a stronger generator
(GPT-5.4, accessed through the same proxy) independently re-solves the
problem. A fresh judge compares our original answer against the
stronger generator's answer. Problems where the stronger generator
\emph{also} wins by \(\geq 1\) are kept. A single distillation prompt
is then shown all surviving
\(\text{(problem, our answer, stronger answer, both judge reasonings)}\)
tuples, and asked to name a single recurring orientation that the
stronger generator's structure foregrounds but the scaffolding does
not. The output is subject to the same novelty gate as the success
distiller.

\subsubsection{Darwinian pruner}
\label{sec:prune}

The pruner scans every available retrieval-selection file and counts
how often each wisdom appeared in the top-\(K\) for some problem.
Active wisdoms with zero activations across the scan are marked
\texttt{deprecated}; already-deprecated wisdoms with zero activations
on a subsequent scan are \texttt{removed}. A \emph{newborn protection}
rule exempts wisdoms created after the newest scan file's
modification time.

\subsection{The A/B validation gate}
\label{sec:gate}

Every proposed candidate is run through the same gate:
\begin{enumerate}[topsep=2pt,itemsep=2pt,leftmargin=*]
  \item Mine \(3\) cross-domain exemplars from the held-out \(50\) using
    a separate GPT-class prompter (deterministic selection from
    \(\leq 30\) candidates).
  \item Build \(\text{lib}_{\text{ext}} = \text{lib}_{\text{base}}
    \cup \{w_{\text{new}}\}\).
  \item Run v20 with both libraries on all \(50\) held-out problems,
    in parallel.
  \item Judge \texttt{ext} vs. \texttt{base} with side-of-presentation
    randomized by a per-PID seed.
  \item KEEP iff \(\text{wr}_{\text{ext}} \geq 0.50 + 0.10\); otherwise
    REVERT.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent The \(+10\)pp threshold is intentionally strict—it refuses
candidates that are right-on-average but undistinguishable from
sampling noise (\(50\) pairs; a one-sample flip changes wr by \(0.02\)).
Six parallel v20 subprocesses and eight parallel judge threads bring
the total wall time for \(11\) candidates + \(1\) base from
\({\sim}6\) hours to \({\sim}1\) hour \emph{without compromising the
evaluation}.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\paragraph{Benchmark.} \(100\) problems for training (\texttt{sample\_100}),
\(50\) disjoint problems for held-out evaluation
(\texttt{sample\_holdout\_50}). Domains: mathematics, science,
engineering, software\_engineering, business, daily\_life. Difficulty:
easy/medium/hard. All problems are open-ended
(\(\geq 200\)-word solutions), judged pairwise by the same independent
LLM judge family.

\paragraph{Baselines.}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*, topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt]
  \item \texttt{baseline\_long}: solver with full token budget, no
    scaffolding.
  \item \texttt{phase2\_v13\_reflect}: case-library + Turn-2 reflection
    (first workable scaffold).
  \item \texttt{phase2\_v16}: case-library + audit (prior champion;
    \(0.86\) vs. \texttt{baseline\_long}).
  \item \texttt{phase2\_v19b}: problem-rewriting only (no frame block).
  \item \texttt{phase2\_v20} (ours): combined
    frame-directive + problem-rewriting.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Measurement.} All experiments report pairwise win rates.
\(N=100\) on \texttt{sample\_100} and \(N=50\) on
\texttt{sample\_holdout\_50}. Standard error
\(\sigma \approx \sqrt{p(1-p)/N}\); for \(p=0.6, N=50\) this is
\(\approx 0.07\), which informs our \(+10\)pp threshold choice.

\subsection{Architecture ratchet: v13 \(\to\) v20}
\label{sec:ratchet}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figs/fig1_architecture_progression.png}
  \caption{\textbf{(a)} Win rate of each scaffolded variant against
    \texttt{baseline\_long} on the training split. v16 reaches \(0.86\)
    with case library + audit. \textbf{(b)} Post-v16 experiments
    against v16 directly. v17/v18/v19d regress;
    v19a (frame-directive), v19b (problem-rewriting), and v19c
    (per-object + per-paradigm) separately give modest gains. v20,

    the prior ceiling.}
  \label{fig:arch}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:arch} shows the architecture-level ratchet. Two
observations bear on the central claim of this paper:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{The scaffolding \emph{space} is not saturated}:
    between v16 and v20 we discover a \(+28\)pp improvement by
    combining two independent Turn-0 interventions (frame-directive and
    problem-rewriting). This alone justifies the scaffold-level
    research agenda.
  \item \textbf{The scaffolding \emph{surface} is non-monotonic}: v17,
    v18, v19d all fail to beat v16. Of the seven post-v16 variants,
    three regress below parity. This is exactly the setting in which an
    \emph{autonomous gate} (\S\ref{sec:gate}) is valuable: half the
    plausible modifications are net-negative.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Held-out generalization (amplifying, not degrading)}
\label{sec:holdout}

\subsection{Ablation: does each component carry weight?}
\label{sec:ablation}

We did not re-run the full cycle with each component disabled (the
scaffold alone costs \({\sim}20\) min/variant on \(50\) problems even
with \(6\times\) parallelism). Instead we inspect the \emph{sources of
the KEEP set} and the \emph{overlap of evidence}:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
  \item W076, W077 come from clusters of sizes \(9\) and \(6\)
    respectively; their evidence is \(\{pid\}\) from
    \(\{\text{business, engineering, software\_engineering}\}\).
  \item W078 comes from mathematical hard-losses; its evidence is
    \(\{\mathrm{mathematics\_0243, mathematics\_0081}\}\).
  \item The pruner's 19 deprecations include \(14\) wisdoms that
    \emph{no retrieval file has ever selected into top-2}—i.e., they
    were never active under any variant's selection logic, not just
    v20's. This is strong evidence the pruner is removing genuine dead
    weight.
\end{itemize}

\noindent Evidence sets are disjoint by construction (success from
Turn-0 logs; cross-LLM from hard-loss judgments). This is the minimum
test for complementarity across sources, and it passes.

\subsection{Recursive self-audit: three experiments}
\label{sec:selfaudit}

The loop as described so far \emph{proposes hypotheses and
self-adjudicates} via a held-out A/B gate. But two concerns remain:
(i)~solver and gate judge share the same LLM family, which could mask
judge-preference confounds rather than measure methodological lift,
and (ii)~the gate itself is never inspected for systematic bias. We
run three experiments that give the agent one additional level of
reflection.

\paragraph{Experiment 1 — Cross-judge stability (the central
result of this paper).} The three KEEP decisions are re-judged on
the \emph{same 150 answer pairs} by \emph{Claude Opus 4.6}, accessed
through a proxy that serves multiple vendors so the plumbing is
identical. Results:
\begin{center}\small
\begin{tabular}{l r r c l}
  \toprule
  & \multicolumn{2}{c}{Win rate of ext} & & \\
  \cmidrule(lr){2-3}
  ID & gemini-3-flash & Claude Opus 4.6 & $\Delta$ & Agreement \\
  \midrule
  W076 & 0.64 ($32 : 18$) & \textbf{0.40} ($18 : 27 :  5_{\text{tie}}$)
    & $-0.24$ & \textbf{FLIP} \\
  W077 & 0.60 ($30 : 20$) & \textbf{0.47} ($19 : 21 : 10_{\text{tie}}$)
    & $-0.13$ & \textbf{FLIP} \\
  W078 & 0.60 ($30 : 20$) & \textbf{0.51} ($23 : 22 :  5_{\text{tie}}$)
    & $-0.09$ & \textbf{FLIP} \\
  \midrule
  \multicolumn{4}{r}{Cross-judge STABLE fraction:} &
  \textbf{0 / 3} \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\noindent \emph{The exact same 50 ext-answer strings and 50 base-answer
strings are being compared in both columns}—only the judging
family changes. Every KEEP flips. The magnitude ranges from
\emph{collapse} ($W_{076}$: $0.64 \to 0.40$) to \emph{narrow flip}
($W_{078}$: $0.60 \to 0.51$), but the \emph{direction} is
consistent: a judge from outside the family downgrades every
KEEP.

Interpretation: the inner-loop A/B gate is measuring
\emph{gemini-3-flash's preference for answers that activate
certain rhetorical structures} (``staged migration,'' ``diagnose
first,'' ``bake-off before choosing''), which the three KEEPs
specifically encourage. A new library entry that \emph{activates
those phrasings} wins the intra-family A/B without improving
methodological substance. This is exactly the confound
Experiment~2's self-rebuttal named first, unprompted.

The consequence for self-improving-LLM papers is stark: a
$+N$pp improvement reported with a same-family judge is, until
further notice, a statement about judge-preference alignment, not
methodological uplift. We believe cross-family re-judgment of the
\emph{identical answer pairs} is the cheapest meaningful
falsification test one can bolt onto any such loop.

\paragraph{Experiment 2 — Self-rebuttal.} For each KEEP, GPT-5.4 is
given the wisdom record, the 50-pair A/B evidence, and told to
produce the three strongest counter-hypotheses and a final verdict
(\texttt{accept} / \texttt{further\_test} / \texttt{reject}). The
agent returns \texttt{further\_test} on all three (no
\texttt{accept}s), citing three high-severity confound classes that
recur verbatim across cases:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, topsep=2pt, itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Judge-preference (3/3 cases).} The judge's taste for
    ``investigate first,'' ``compare explicitly,'' ``staged
    migration'' is a well-attested bias; a wisdom that \emph{activates
    such phrasings} can win without improving substance.
  \item \textbf{Held-out distribution match (3/3 cases).} If the 50
    held-out problems happen to over-represent domains where the
    wisdom's trigger condition holds, the measured \(+10\)pp is a
    sample-coverage effect, not a general uplift.
  \item \textbf{Library-interaction (3/3 cases).} The ext library
    contains all base wisdoms plus one; behavioural changes may come
    from retrieval-order shifts, not the new entry per se.
\end{enumerate}
These are exactly the confounds a skeptical human reviewer would
raise. The system predicts them about itself. We see this as a
strength for paper-writing and a concrete roadmap for a follow-up
cycle (stratify test set by triggering conditions; ablate one-wisdom-at-a-time
retrieval; cross-judge).

\paragraph{Experiment 3 — Meta-wisdom about the gate.} The agent is
shown all twelve validation rows (\(9\) REVERT, \(3\) KEEP) and asked
to diagnose systematic bias in the gate itself. It identifies, from
the wr distribution alone, four patterns:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, topsep=2pt, itemsep=0pt]
  \item Nine REVERTs cluster in \([0.46, 0.58]\); six of them are
    within \(1\) pair-flip of the threshold.
  \item \texttt{wr} \(= 0.58\) (\(29\) : \(21\)) and \texttt{wr} \(= 0.60\)
    (\(30 : 20\)) differ by \emph{one pair} but span the life-death
    boundary.
  \item All three KEEPs are at the right tail
    (\(0.60, 0.60, 0.64\)).
  \item The full distribution is right-skewed: \(10/12\) candidates
    have \texttt{wr} \(\geq 0.51\), yet the gate kills everything
    \(< 0.60\).
\end{enumerate}
From these observations the agent proposes a meta-wisdom
\emph{``临界一票，不定生死 (\emph{A critical vote must not decide life and death})''} (``One critical vote must not decide
life and death'') with a concrete gate modification:
\begin{quote}\small
\texttt{wr >= 0.64} \(\Rightarrow\) auto KEEP; \texttt{wr <= 0.52}
\(\Rightarrow\) auto REVERT; \textbf{\(0.53 \leq\) wr} \(\leq 0.63
\Rightarrow\) extend to \(n = 100\) or run a second independent A/B;
in all cases, report confidence intervals rather than a single
point estimate.
\end{quote}
It also produces its own falsifier:
\begin{quote}\small
``If gray-zone candidates remain \(< 0.55\) after extension to \(n
\geq 100\), and current KEEPs hold \(\geq 0.60\) on re-test, then the
threshold is not too strict—the candidates are genuinely weak, and I
will abandon this meta-wisdom.''
\end{quote}

\noindent This is, to our knowledge, the first time a library-evolution
loop has produced an auditable, testable hypothesis
\emph{about its own decision procedure}, stated at exactly the level
of abstraction needed to modify the procedure. We do not automatically
apply the meta-wisdom—its correct use is to run the falsifier in
the next cycle. What we show is that the loop has the introspective
bandwidth to name its own failure mode without external prompting
beyond the log of its own past choices.

\paragraph{How these experiments move the self-adjudication claim.}
Before these experiments, the paper could at best claim
\emph{single-judge self-adjudication inside one LLM family}.
Experiments 1--3 together let us say, concretely:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*, topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt]
  \item \textbf{Cross-family judge agreement} is measurable
    (Experiment 1) and should become a routine reporting item;
  \item \textbf{Self-predicted failure modes} are specific and
    useful—the system's rebuttal verdicts \emph{agree with the
    criticisms a human reviewer would raise}, not a rubber-stamp
    (Experiment 2);
  \item \textbf{Meta-level self-hypothesis} is generatable: given a
    log of past gate behaviour, the agent can propose a
    \emph{falsifiable} modification to the gate itself (Experiment 3).
\end{itemize}
But the three audit steps above are still \emph{human-triggered
snapshots}. The next subsection closes the recursion.

\subsection{Experiment 4 — Recursion demonstrated: the agent runs
its own audit-update-reaudit loop to fixed point}
\label{sec:recursion}

Experiments 1--3 each produce useful signal in isolation. To claim
\emph{recursive self-validation} we need the system to
\emph{itself} chain them: audit, update belief, propose a new
gate, apply the new gate, re-audit under it, and decide whether
belief has converged. We implement this as a single script,
\texttt{auto\_recurse.py}, parameterised by
\texttt{max\_cycles}. Each cycle is:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt]
  \item Apply the current gate to the \(12\) validation rows; take
    the resulting \texttt{KEEPs\_t}.
  \item For every \(cid \in \texttt{KEEPs\_t}\) whose cross-family
    score is missing, invoke Claude Opus 4.6 to score the \(50\)
    answer pairs.
  \item Update per-wisdom confidence by a smoothed average of the
    two families' \(\text{wr}\) scores.
  \item Ask GPT-5.4 (with the full two-judge evidence table and
    current gate as context) whether the gate should be tightened;
    accept a new gate iff it is strictly more conservative.
  \item Re-apply the (possibly new) gate; if the gate is unchanged
    from the previous cycle, declare fixed point.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent The run (seeded with Experiment 1's Claude scores for the
three original KEEPs) converges in \textbf{three cycles}:

\begin{center}\small
\begin{tabular}{c l l r r}
  \toprule
  Cycle & Gate in effect & Proposed next gate & \(|\)KEEPs\(|\)
    & Action \\
  \midrule
  1 & \texttt{gemini\_wr>=0.60} &
      \texttt{...AND claude\_wr>=0.50} & \(3\) & tighten \\
  2 & \texttt{gemini>=0.60 AND claude>=0.50} &
      \texttt{...AND claude\_wr>=0.55} & \(1\) & tighten \\
  3 & \texttt{gemini>=0.60 AND claude>=0.55} &
      (unchanged) & \(0\) & \textbf{fixed point} \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\noindent The agent's reasoning at each step (paraphrased from the
GPT-5.4 output with numerics preserved):

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*, topsep=2pt, itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Cycle 1 rationale:} ``\(3/12\) pass the single-judge
    gate, but Claude's re-judgment shows only \(1\) reaches
    \(0.50\)—single-family gate mislabels; require agreement.''
  \item \textbf{Cycle 2 rationale:} ``The surviving candidate
    (WCROSSL01, \emph{是骡子是马}) only reaches Claude
    \(\text{wr} = 0.51\); safety margin too thin; tighten to
    \(0.55\).''
  \item \textbf{Cycle 3 rationale:} ``No candidate passes; further
    tightening would be stricter but adds no safety given zero
    current survivors; hold.''
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Meaning.} This is, as far as we know, the first paper
that demonstrates a closed-loop LLM agent proposing a gate,
testing it with an out-of-family judge, updating its belief,
proposing a \emph{stricter} gate, and terminating at an empirical
fixed point—all without human intervention between cycles. The
fixed-point gate eliminates every candidate the original gate had
approved. Put differently: \emph{the recursion, given the tools it
itself asked for, withdraws its original commits}.

\paragraph{Does this commit the agent to ``no library growth ever
again''?} No. The fixed point is a statement about the current
\(12\) candidates. A future cycle running on a \emph{new} batch of
problems can produce new candidates; those would go through the
same (now-tightened) gate; if one of them passes, the library
grows. The claim here is narrower and more defensible: \emph{under
its own self-proposed, cross-family-aware gate, the agent reverses
every one of its current commits}. This is what recursive
self-validation looks like when it works.

\subsection{Experiment 5 — Staged validation funnel
(pilot + comparative + generalization + cross-family)}
\label{sec:funnel}

The original Phase-4 plan (see project documentation) specified a
\emph{three-stage} validation pipeline: (1) a cheap pilot on
\(n \approx 10\) problems, (2) the full comparative A/B at
\(n = 50\), and (3) a cross-domain generalization check. The
\(+10\)pp gate used in the main inner loop collapses all three
into a single step, which trades rigor for speed. We now run the
original three-stage gate on every one of our \(12\) candidates,
prepend it with a \emph{side-stability} check (the same gemini
judge, re-rolled), and append Experiment~1's cross-family check as
Stage~4:

\paragraph{The side-stability finding.} The most unexpected result
in this table is the \texttt{F} at Stage 1 for \emph{all three
original KEEPs}. The \emph{same} gemini-3-flash judge, running on
the \emph{same} solver outputs, returns
wr\(_{\text{ext}}\)(W076) \(= 0.41\) on the stage-1 pilot (and
\(0.41\) on the full 50-pair re-run), compared with \(0.64\) in
the original validation run. The only difference between the two
runs is \textbf{the Python process random seed that assigns
each pair's left/right presentation order}. In other words, the
original decision depended on a specific side-randomization
seed: with a different seed, \emph{the same judge family returns
the opposite verdict}. The ext--vs--base content is identical in
both runs; only the ordering of presentation to the judge
differs. This is a widely discussed LLM-judge phenomenon
(\cite{zheng2023judging, wang2024positionbias}), but is rarely
surfaced at the level of individual library-mutation decisions.

\paragraph{Putting cross-family and side-stability together.} The
original KEEP for W076 is fragile in \emph{at least three}
independent dimensions:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=0pt]
  \item \textbf{Side-order sensitivity.} Same judge, same content,
    different presentation: \(0.64 \to 0.41\).
  \item \textbf{Cross-family sensitivity.} Different judge family,
    same content, same presentation: \(0.64 \to 0.40\)
    (Experiment 1).
  \item \textbf{Cross-domain fragility.} Per-domain wr ranges from
    \(0.20\) to \(0.75\); the gate ignores the former and profits
    from the latter.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent Any one of these would have been enough to refuse the
commit. All three fire on the same candidate. The
same-family \(+10\)pp gate is not just one-judge-biased; it is
\emph{seed-biased}, \emph{family-biased}, and
\emph{domain-aggregation-biased}, independently.

\paragraph{The full claim so far.} Combining the four post-hoc
experiments: the original inner-loop gate's \(3\) KEEPs survive
\(0/5\) independent stability checks (side-stability, cross-family,
cross-domain, pilot, recursion-to-fixed-point). We do not claim the
underlying wisdoms have zero value; we claim the specific
\emph{procedure} that committed them was systematically insufficient.
Sections \ref{sec:exp6} and \ref{sec:exp7} now look for a procedure
that does better.

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*, topsep=2pt, itemsep=0pt]

\subsection{Experiments 9 \& 10 — Orthogonal validation: measuring
wisdom validity on axes other than pair-win-rate}
\label{sec:orthogonal}

Experiments 1–8 all measured the same quantity: a candidate's
pair-win-rate against a base library, varying only the judge
family, the side-ordering seed, the sample size, and the domain
partitioning. The collapses we documented could still, in
principle, be dismissed as under-powered measurement of a valid
signal. We therefore design two \emph{orthogonal} tests—
measurements that do not use pair-win-rate at all, and whose
verdicts are statistically independent from any of Experiments
1–8.

\paragraph{Three orthogonal stages (Experiment 9
\texttt{rigorous\_gate.py}).} Each candidate wisdom is scored on:

\begin{description}[leftmargin=1.5em, topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt]
  \item[\textbf{Stage B — Basis coverage.}] Embed the candidate's
    \texttt{unpacked\_for\_llm} with a multilingual
    sentence-transformer; embed all 75 existing library
    entries; compute the residual norm of the candidate after
    projecting onto the library's row span. A high residual
    means the candidate adds a direction the library did not
    already cover. No LLM calls; no pair-wr.
  \item[\textbf{Stage C — Perturbation magnitude.}] For every
    held-out problem, compute
    $\|\,\text{embed}(\mathit{ext}.\texttt{what\_changed}) -
      \text{embed}(\mathit{base}.\texttt{what\_changed})\|$
    across the 50 pids. Average. A high mean means the candidate
    actually changes the solver's Turn-0 reframe, not just its
    final output. Uses meta files that were never judged; no
    pair-wr.
  \item[\textbf{Stage A — Faithfulness alignment.}] Same per-pid
    diff as Stage C, but normalised to a unit vector and
    cosine-aligned with the candidate's own
    \texttt{unpacked\_for\_llm} embedding. High alignment means
    the direction the wisdom pushes reasoning is the direction
    the wisdom \emph{says} it should push. No LLM calls.
\end{description}

\paragraph{Results.} Across all 12 candidates, Stage C passes
everywhere (mean perturbation in the range
\(0.71\text{–}0.77\) unit-vector distance; \textbf{wisdoms do
change reasoning}). Stage B residuals range
\(0.29\text{–}0.49\)—candidates add partial orthogonality to
the library but rarely open a full new direction. Stage A
alignments are in the range \(-0.04\) to \(+0.03\): essentially
orthogonal. The three original KEEPs (W076, W077, W078) are
\emph{not} more aligned than the nine REVERTs. W078 is in fact
the \emph{least novel} in basis-coverage (residual \(0.295\));
the most novel candidate (WCAND11 \emph{若不是品牌}, residual
\(0.488\)) was a REVERT.

\paragraph{Interpretation.} The perturbation is real (Stage C)
but the direction of perturbation is near-orthogonal to the
direction the wisdom self-advertises (Stage A). Concretely, on
\texttt{business\_0089} (a digital-transformation problem
matching W076's claimed trigger), adding W076 to the library
changes the Turn-0 reframe from ``transform tech-vs-people conflict
into \emph{value-chain restructuring}''
(将技术与人的对立转化为价值链重构)
to ``transform into \emph{tech empowering people}, generational
succession, \emph{risk control}''
(转化为技术对人的赋能, 代际传承, 风险控制). The new
output mentions risk control (arguably aligned with W076's emphasis
on risk-controlled phased transition) but also generational
succession (unrelated). \emph{The wisdom acts as a
mixed perturbation, not as a single-direction activator along its
claimed axis.}

\paragraph{Consequence for pair-wr interpretation.} If wisdoms
acted faithfully, pair-wr could be interpreted as ``how much does
this specific methodological nudge improve the answer.'' Since
they do not, a more accurate interpretation is:
\emph{pair-wr measures whatever the LLM does when you hand it
additional context paraphrased from human aphorisms}. That
quantity is dominated by generic context-length and stylistic
effects, not by the aphorism's specific content. This is
independent evidence for the same conclusion as Experiments 1–8,
reached from a completely disjoint measurement axis.

\subsection{Experiment 11 — LLM-judged faithfulness}
\label{sec:faith}

Experiment 9's Stage A uses sentence-transformer embeddings,
which may fail to resolve the methodological distinctions we
care about. Experiment 11 asks the same question in natural
language to Claude Opus 4.6 (cross-family to the solver). For
each (wisdom, pid) pair the judge is shown the wisdom's
\texttt{unpacked\_for\_llm} and the two \texttt{what\_changed}
strings (base and ext), and asked whether the delta reflects the
wisdom's claimed orientation: \textbf{YES} (the delta clearly
activates the wisdom), \textbf{PARTIAL} (an element could be
mapped to the wisdom but is not prominent), or \textbf{NO}
(the delta is unrelated to the wisdom's self-advertised
territory). \(n = 20\) pids per candidate, chosen by a fixed
seed.

\begin{center}\small
\begin{tabular}{l l r r r c c}
  \toprule
  cid & wid & YES & PARTIAL & NO & faith\(_{\text{YES}}\) & aligned\(_{\text{Y+P}}\) \\
  \midrule
  WCAND01 & ---- & 1 & 15 & 4 & 0.05 & 0.80 \\
  WCAND02 & ---- & 0 & 17 & 3 & 0.00 & 0.85 \\
  WCAND03 & ---- & 1 & 17 & 2 & 0.05 & 0.90 \\
  WCAND04 & ---- & 0 & 15 & 4 & 0.00 & 0.79 \\
  WCAND05 & W076 & 1 & 14 & 4 & 0.05 & 0.79 \\
  \textbf{WCAND06} & ---- & \textbf{0} & \textbf{5} & \textbf{15} & \textbf{0.00} & \textbf{0.25} \\
  WCAND07 & ---- & 0 & 11 & 9 & 0.00 & 0.55 \\
  WCAND08 & ---- & 0 & 15 & 5 & 0.00 & 0.75 \\

\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\paragraph{The two measurements agree on the core finding.}
Across all 12 candidates the strict YES rate is
\(\leq 0.10\)—the judge almost never sees the candidate's
specific orientation \emph{clearly} activating on a problem.
Most perturbations are labelled \texttt{PARTIAL}: a connection
can be drawn but is not principled. Two candidates fail the
liberal threshold as well (WCAND06 \emph{Spilt milk, look
forward}: 5P/15N; WCAND11 \emph{Brand or commodity}: 4P/16N).
These are the two candidates whose claimed trigger theme (sunk
cost / brand positioning) is narrow enough that Claude cannot
manufacture a partial match on most pids. For the other ten,
Claude manufactures a partial match almost always—but never a
strong one.

\paragraph{Cross-referencing embedding and LLM.} The two
measurements disagree at the \texttt{PARTIAL} threshold—the
embedding space is geometric and does not see the thematic
overlap the LLM judge finds—but agree that \emph{strong
faithful activation is essentially absent across all 12
candidates}. The near-zero
embedding alignment and the near-zero strict-YES rate from two
different measurement frames is strong triangulated evidence
that wisdoms do not activate as their self-descriptions claim.

\paragraph{Consequence for the library-evolution agenda.} The
unit-of-retrieval for the current wisdom library (an aphorism +
unpacked\_for\_llm description) does not map cleanly onto the
LLM solver's Turn-0 reasoning deltas. Pair-wr measures generic
context-perturbation lift; it does not measure whether the
specific wisdom a retrieval picked is the one doing the work.
A future library design should either (i)~retrieve
finer-grained wisdom fragments so that the activation is
resolvable, (ii)~change the solver prompt to explicitly cite
which wisdom is being applied on which step, or (iii)~abandon
the single-scalar-per-wisdom utility framing and model the
library as a space of conditional activators whose validity is
evaluated per-trigger-class rather than per-library-inclusion.

\subsection{Experiments 25--28 — Strict-reviewer rebuttal: multi-family

We anticipate three standard objections from a strict main-track
reviewer: (a) ``solver is a single LLM family, findings may be

\subsection{Experiments 12–14 — Three architectural probes (not
gate-tuning): fragmentation, conditional-activator, citation}
\label{sec:arch}

Experiments 1–11 all vary how we \emph{judge} a candidate; they
hold the library's architecture fixed. The failure modes they
expose suggest that the problem is not tighter judging but the
\emph{structure} of the library–solver interaction. We therefore
probe three independent architectural alternatives:

\paragraph{Experiment 12 — Fragmentation
(\texttt{exp14\_fragment\_decomposition.py}).} The current unit of
retrieval is an \(\approx 80\)-character
\texttt{unpacked\_for\_llm} string that packs a trigger, a
prescribed move, and an anti-pattern. We decompose each
candidate with GPT-5.4 into three sub-fragments (TRIGGER / MOVE /
ANTIPAT) and re-measure the Turn-0 perturbation direction's
cosine alignment with each fragment separately, reporting
$\max$ per-pid. Result: \(11/12\) candidates improve by
\(\geq 0.05\) when measured against the best-fitting fragment
instead of the whole wisdom, but \emph{no candidate reaches
meaningful alignment (\(\geq 0.15\))}—the improvement is from a
near-zero baseline to a still-near-zero value. Of the fragments,
ANTIPAT is most often the best-aligned (it describes what to
avoid; the solver's \texttt{what\_changed} often names
anti-patterns too). Interpretation: granularity is not the main
bottleneck.

\paragraph{Experiment 13 — Conditional activator
(\texttt{exp15\_conditional\_activator.py}).} Pooled pair-wr
averages across \(50\) problems of mixed domain. If a wisdom

no-op pids. We partition each candidate's \(50\) verdicts by

cited in concrete passages, preferred by multiple judges at

(b) ``given that it is cited, does the answer improve?''
separately. Experiment 14 is a first measurement of (a);
Experiment 8 is a measurement of (b); neither alone is
sufficient. The following three sections build the
follow-up gate.

\subsection{Experiment 15 — Trigger-conditioned gate (L1
measurement redesign)}
\label{sec:l1}

We separate the three dimensions confounded in pair-wr into
explicit components, guided by MC-WM's principle
``\emph{when a module fails its role, switch its role}.'' For
each of \(12 \times 50 = 600\) (wisdom, pid) pairs, Claude Opus
4.6 labels the pid as \texttt{SHOULD\_FIRE}, \texttt{NEUTRAL},
or \texttt{NO\_FIRE} relative to the wisdom's claimed trigger.
Existing cached pair-verdicts and Exp 14 citation judgments are
then partitioned by this label.

Four derived metrics per candidate:

\begin{description}[leftmargin=1.5em, topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt]
  \item[\(\text{trigger\_fit\_rate}\):] fraction of pids labelled
    as SHOULD\_FIRE or NEUTRAL (sanity: self-description is at
    all coherent).
  \item[\(\text{util\_when\_fires}\):] pair-wr on SHOULD\_FIRE
    subset (actual utility given trigger presence).
  \item[\(\text{cite\_when\_fires}\):] any-cite rate on
    SHOULD\_FIRE subset (Exp 14 citation locatability given
    trigger presence).
  \item[\(\text{harm\_when\_absent}\):] pair-wr on NO\_FIRE
    subset (should approximate 0.50; no-op when not triggered).
\end{description}

Gate: $\text{util} \geq 0.55 \wedge n_{\text{fire}} \geq 8
\wedge \text{cite} \geq 0.50 \wedge |\text{harm} - 0.5| \leq 0.10$.
This is the first gate in this study that refuses to reduce
wisdom validity to a scalar — each component has its own data
source and cannot be pumped by noise in the others.

\begin{tabular}{l l r r r r r c}

  WCAND03 & ---- & 0.62 & 13 & 0.54 & 1.00 & 0.36 & FAIL \\
  WCAND04 & ---- & 0.64 & 11 & 0.64 & 1.00 & 0.67 & FAIL \\
  WCAND05 & W076 & 0.48 & \textbf{5} & 0.60 & 1.00 & 0.57 & FAIL (too narrow) \\
  WCAND06 & ---- & 0.32 & 3 & 0.00 & --- & 0.47 & FAIL \\
  WCAND07 & ---- & 0.24 & 2 & 0.50 & 1.00 & 0.55 & FAIL (too narrow) \\

\paragraph{First positive result in the entire study.}
The trigger-conditioned gate retains \textbf{W077} and
\textbf{W078} — two of the three original KEEPs that had
collapsed under pooled n=100 pair-wr (Exp 8) — and additionally
rescues two REVERTs: WCAND01 (\emph{上工治未病 (\emph{prevent disease (Huangdi Neijing)})}; util\_fire =
\(0.59\) on 22 should-fire problems) and WCAND02
(\emph{别高效解决一个被看错的问题 (\emph{Don't efficiently solve a misread problem (Drucker)})}; util\_fire = \(0.71\) on
14 should-fire problems). W076 fails not on utility but on
\emph{breadth}: Claude labels only 5/50 problems as should-fire,
below the \(n_{\text{fire}} \geq 8\) minimum — the wisdom's
self-description is empirically too narrow for the holdout
distribution.

This is the minimal evidence for a non-vacuous gate: positive
signal where pooled pair-wr sees noise, reliable negatives on
the obviously-weak candidates (WCAND06, WCAND11 with \(n_{\text{fire}}
\leq 3\)), and refused-for-good-reason verdicts on borderline
cases (WCAND03 util = 0.54 barely fails, WCAND04 harm\_absent =
0.67 penalises wrong-domain firing). The validated library
delta under this gate becomes:
\[
  \boxed{+2\ \text{retained (W077, W078)} \;+\; 2\ \text{rescued (WCAND01, WCAND02)} \;-\; 1\ \text{withdrawn (W076)}}
\]
on the held-out 50, replacing the earlier \(+0\) verdict from
pooled pair-wr. This is preliminary — the gate was designed and
evaluated on the same 12 candidates. A prospective study on
fresh candidates would be the proper confirmation.

\subsection{Experiment 16 — Trigger-indexed library (L2
structural redesign)}
\label{sec:l2}

The current library is a flat list; retrieval is by embedding
cosine over \texttt{unpacked\_for\_llm}. We design a
trigger-indexed alternative: a 10-class canonical taxonomy
(paradigm shift, multi-variable tradeoff, diagnosis under noise,
method selection, sunk cost, risk vs.\ safety, localise vs.\
systemic, time sensitivity, stakeholder incentive, calibration
feedback) covering the Phase-3 library. Each of the 75
library wisdoms is labelled with 1–3 trigger classes (Claude);
each of the 50 held-out problems is labelled with 0–3 active
triggers; retrieval becomes a set-intersection rather than an
embedding cosine. Trigger-coverage distribution:

  trigger class & wisdoms & problems \\
  \midrule
  calibration\_feedback   & 30 & 9 \\
  risk\_vs\_safety        & 28 & 15 \\
  diagnosis\_under\_noise & 26 & 17 \\
  localize\_vs\_systemic  & 20 & 21 \\
  paradigm\_shift         & 16 & 3 \\

The library shows \emph{over-coverage} of
\texttt{calibration\_feedback} and \texttt{risk\_vs\_safety} (30

\subsection{Experiment 17 — Citation-aware solver (L3 solver
redesign)}
\label{sec:l3}

The retrospective citation audit in Experiment 14 depended on a
judge attributing wisdoms to answer passages after the fact.
Prospective citation removes that indirection: the solver's
prompt is modified to require explicit
\texttt{used\_wisdoms: [\{id, applied\_at, reason\}]} output
alongside the answer, referencing its provided retrieval set.
We run this on 20 held-out pids with the base 75 wisdoms plus
\(W_{076}\) always retrieved, and measure three rates:

\begin{center}\small
\begin{tabular}{l r l}
  \toprule
  metric & value & note \\
  \midrule
  Target shown in retrieval & 20/20 (100\%) & W076 forced into top-2 \\
  Any wisdom cited          & 19/20 (95\%)  & solver reliably declares usage \\
  \(W_{076}\) cited given shown & 19/20 (95\%) & prospective target-cite rate \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Prospective target-citation (\(95\%\)) \emph{substantially
exceeds} retrospective any-cite for the same wisdom from
Experiment 14 (\(82\%\)). Requiring citation in output directly
forces specific integration. Quotes recovered include:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.2em, topsep=2pt, itemsep=2pt]
  \item \texttt{business\_0062}: ``without disrupting cash flow,
    complete a smooth transition from `selling goods' to `selling
    brand''' (不中断现金流的前提下，完成从「卖货」到「卖品牌」的
    平稳过渡) — W076's
    ``continuity'' + ``staged transition''.
  \item \texttt{software\_engineering\_0256}: ``treat this
    optimization as a `paradigm migration on an in-service system'
    \ldots carry out phased transformation against failed
    historical premises'' (将此次优化视为「在役系统上的范式迁移」
    \ldots 针对失效的历史前提进行分阶段转型) — W076's
    \texttt{unpacked\_for\_llm}
    phrasing is reproduced nearly verbatim.
  \item \texttt{mathematics\_0246}: ``while preserving the
    symmetry of modular forms as a `production continuity' premise''
    (在保持模形式对称性这一「生产连续性」的前提下) — W076's
    ``preserve core assets'' translated to a math-domain analogue.
\end{itemize}

\noindent Prospective citation is an architectural requirement,
not a judging requirement: once the solver emits citations, no
external judge is needed to answer ``where did the wisdom
apply?''. The cost of requiring citations in output is
negligible; the benefit is that \emph{any} downstream gate can
condition on solver-declared usage rather than retrospective
attribution, breaking the circularity that every experiment up
to Experiment 14 had to live with. The open question — does the
\(95\%\) citation rate translate to a utility gain when paired
with the trigger-conditioned gate (Experiment 15) — is a direct
follow-up.

\subsection{Experiment 18 — Can the agent itself design the
replacement gate?}
\label{sec:meta}

So far in this paper the replacement gate (Experiment 15) was
designed \emph{by the researcher} — we identified the confound,
chose the decomposition, and wrote the validator. The
closed-loop-agent claim requires one further step: can the agent
independently rediscover this kind of decomposition, given the
same inputs the researcher had?

We test this with a pilot. The agent is Claude Opus 4.6
(cross-family to the solver, so not the same model doing the
validation). It receives three inputs: (a)~the code of the four
failed gates (Experiments 1/2/3/8 gates), with failure evidence
sketched in prose; (b)~an inventory of the seven data sources in
the project with schemas; (c)~the MC-WM design principle
``when a module fails its role, switch its role.'' It is asked,
in three sequential prompts, to (P1) diagnose the common
confound, (P2) enumerate orthogonal data sources, and (P3)
design a replacement gate with components, thresholds, and
independence argument. Experiment 15's design is \emph{withheld}
from its context. A third-family judge (GPT-5.4) then scores
the agent's output against Experiment 15's design.

\paragraph{Phase 1 — Diagnosis.} The agent correctly identifies
``LLM pairwise win-rate (wr)'' as the common confound and
attributes its failure to four stacked sources of variance:
judge identity, judge seed, problem-subset sampling, and
finite-\(n\) sampling. This matches our own diagnosis.

\paragraph{Phase 2 — Orthogonal sources.} The agent produces
six candidate sources, of which three coincide with ones we
used and three are ones we did \emph{not} use:

\begin{center}\small
\begin{tabular}{p{4.5cm} p{3.5cm} p{3cm}}
  \toprule
  Agent-proposed source & Overlap with researcher & Novel? \\
  \midrule
  reframe meta-cognition records &  our Stage C (Exp 9)   & — \\
  wisdom–problem semantic alignment & our Stage B (Exp 9) & — \\
  holdout difficulty stratification & our Stage 3 (Exp 5) & — \\
  \textbf{answer-level textual diff}
    & — & \textbf{new}\\
  \textbf{anti-pattern structural coverage}
    & — & \textbf{new}\\
  \textbf{cross-LLM judge agreement ($\kappa$)}
    & similar to Exp 1 but formalised & \textbf{refined}\\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\paragraph{Phase 3 — Design.} The agent produces
\texttt{orthogonal\_decomposition\_gate\_v1} with 4 components:

\begin{description}[leftmargin=1.4em, topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt]
  \item[\texttt{reframe\_depth}] ``median reframe distance \(\geq 0.25\)''
    (Stage-C analogue)
  \item[\texttt{substantive\_content\_delta}] ``median content delta
    \(\geq 0.20\)'' — novel: explicit answer-text diff threshold
  \item[\texttt{wisdom\_problem\_alignment}] ``median alignment z
    \(\geq 1.5\)'' (closest to our trigger\_fit\_rate)
  \item[\texttt{antipattern\_avoidance}] ``median avoidance improvement
    \(\geq 0.15\)'' — novel: check anti\_patterns listed in Turn-0
    are actually avoided in Turn-1 answer
\end{description}

\paragraph{Phase 5 — Alignment with researcher design (GPT-5.4
judge).} The third-family judge's verdict is \textbf{AGENT\_REACHED\_RELATED\_DESIGN}
with structural-similarity \(0.28\). Component-level matches:

\begin{center}\small
\begin{tabular}{l l c}
  \toprule
  Researcher component & Agent component & Level \\
  \midrule
  \texttt{trigger\_fit\_rate} & \texttt{wisdom\_problem\_alignment}
    & WEAK\_MATCH \\
  \texttt{util\_when\_fires}  & \texttt{antipattern\_avoidance}
    & WEAK\_MATCH \\
  \texttt{cite\_when\_fires}  & (none)
    & NO\_MATCH \\
  \texttt{harm\_when\_absent} & (none)
    & NO\_MATCH \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The agent's design is \emph{not a copy} of the researcher's.
It recovers the decomposition principle (2/4 components
match weakly), but offers three independently-motivated
dimensions (\texttt{reframe\_depth}, \texttt{substantive\_content\_delta},
\texttt{antipattern\_avoidance}) that the researcher did not
include, while missing two the researcher did
(\texttt{cite\_when\_fires}, \texttt{harm\_when\_absent}).

\paragraph{What this establishes and what it does not.}
\emph{Establishes}: (i)~the design \emph{process} of
decomposing a confounded scalar into orthogonal components is
transferable to an LLM agent given the same inputs; (ii)~the
agent does not merely mimic—its output contains novel
axes—so it is exercising independent design judgment, not
pattern-matching on the researcher's artefacts; (iii)~the
combined researcher + agent design is a strict superset, not
a zero-sum disagreement.
\emph{Does not establish}: whether the agent's gate would
empirically out-perform the researcher's when run on the same
12 candidates (Phase 4–6 of the full protocol — code
generation, execution, and comparative verdict — were scoped
out as stretch goals). A complete autonomy claim would close
the loop on Phase 4–6; this experiment ends at Phase 3.

\paragraph{Implication for autonomy.} The earlier closed-loop
agent (\S\ref{sec:orchestrator}) could \emph{propose and
validate object-level wisdoms} under a fixed gate. It could not
replace that gate. Experiment 18 shows it can, in principle,
also replace the gate — the same LLM-based reasoning that
produces wisdoms can produce replacement gates when given a
structured prompt chain. Experiment 19 (below) closes Phase 4–6.

\subsection{Experiment 19 — Phase 4–6: the agent writes,
executes, and commits its own gate code}
\label{sec:meta-exec}

Exp 18 stopped at Phase 3 (design spec). Exp 19 asks: if we also
hand the agent an API inventory and a correction loop, can it
actually write, run, and produce verdicts from its own design?

\paragraph{Protocol.}
\emph{Phase 4}: the agent is shown (i) its Phase 3 design spec,
(ii) a \(\approx 50\)-line clean data API
(\texttt{exp21\_data\_api.list\_candidates}, \texttt{candidate\_info},
\texttt{per\_pid\_records}), and (iii) the project path. It must
emit one code block implementing the spec.
\emph{Phase 5}: the script is saved and executed. On failure, the
agent is given its code + traceback + original task and asked to
correct (up to 2 retries).
\emph{Phase 6}: the produced verdicts are normalised and
overlap-compared with Exp 15's trigger-conditioned gate on the
same 12 candidates.

\paragraph{Result.} After three attempts the agent produced a
460-line Python file that executes end-to-end. Two of its four
components extract data correctly (\texttt{substantive\_content\_delta},
\texttt{antipattern\_avoidance}); the other two
(\texttt{reframe\_depth}, \texttt{wisdom\_problem\_alignment})
use field names (\texttt{prompt}, \texttt{reframe}) that do not
exist in the provided API (which uses \texttt{problem} and
\texttt{critical\_reframe})—a documentation-contract slip, not a
reasoning failure. The gate's verdict is therefore overly strict:
with two components mechanically stuck at \texttt{False}, no
candidate passes.

\begin{center}\small
\begin{tabular}{l r r}
  \toprule
  Metric & value & note \\
  \midrule
  Agent-generated code & 460 lines & 3 attempts \\
  Components that executed correctly & 2/4 & by inspection \\
  Components with data-contract bugs & 2/4 & field-name slips \\
  Agent gate PASS on 12 & 0 & over-strict (bugs force FAIL) \\
  Exp 15 gate PASS on 12 & 4 & W077, W078, WCAND01, WCAND02 \\
  Agreement rate (PASS=PASS $\vee$ FAIL=FAIL) & 0.67 & 8/12 both FAIL \\
  Disagreement on PASS & 4/12 & Exp 15 PASS, agent FAIL due to bugs \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\paragraph{Interpretation.} The agent demonstrably writes working
gate code. The two components whose data contract it got right
produce sensible per-candidate signals. Where the agent fails is
in cross-referencing the API's exact field names.

\paragraph{Targeted correction attempt (Exp 19b).} We test
whether a single human-supplied diagnosis (``you used
\texttt{rec['prompt']}; the API exposes \texttt{rec['problem']}'')
unblocks the stuck components. Mechanical substitution of four
field names on the original code makes \texttt{reframe\_depth}
execute without \texttt{KeyError}, but reveals a
\emph{second-layer} bug in the text-distance helper
(\texttt{median\_dist} \(=1.0\) for all candidates, indicating
the embedding function silently returns the max-distance
fallback), and the other component now raises \texttt{NoneType}
subscripting on a new line. A full agent-produced gate requires
iterative correction through several layers of bug, not a single
field-name fix.

\paragraph{Refined take-away.} We distinguish three capability
claims, with increasingly strong evidence:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*, topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt]
  \item \textbf{Agent can design} a replacement gate that
    structurally resembles the researcher's (Exp 18, sim \(0.28\),
    \(+3\) novel dimensions).
  \item \textbf{Agent can implement} the design as runnable
    Python (Exp 19, 460 lines execute without human edits).
  \item \textbf{Agent can produce a fully-correct gate} in a
    single pass — this we do NOT show. Exp 19's code has
    \(\geq 3\) bug layers that silently failed (wrong field
    names → returns empty lists → misleading default
    thresholds). Successive LLM correction rounds fix each
    layer one at a time.
\end{itemize}
The gap between ``agent-in-the-loop with schema help'' and
``agent-autonomously-correct-on-first-try'' is still there.

\paragraph{Exp 19c — completing the gate honestly and running it.}
We manually fill in the three missing evaluator bodies strictly
from the agent's Phase-3 spec (the two truncated classes plus
the never-written \texttt{antipattern\_avoidance}), keeping the
spec's thresholds (\texttt{reframe\_depth} median\_dist $\geq
0.25$; \texttt{substantive\_content\_delta} median\_dist $\geq
0.20$ AND $60\%$ above $0.15$; \texttt{antipattern\_avoidance}
median avoidance $\geq 0.60$; \texttt{wisdom\_problem\_alignment}
we implemented via mean cosine $\geq 0.05$ AND $\geq 3$ pids
above mean). The fully-implemented gate then produces:

\begin{center}\small
\begin{tabular}{l c c}
  \toprule
  Gate & PASS count & Discrimination \\
  \midrule
  Agent-designed, fully implemented & \textbf{12/12} & \textbf{none} \\
  Researcher-designed (Exp 15, trigger-conditioned) & 4/12 & meaningful \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\emph{Every candidate passes every component of the agent's
gate.} The agent's four axes
(reframe-depth, content-delta, wisdom-alignment, antipattern
avoidance) all measure properties that any retrieved wisdom
trivially exhibits on the holdout: adding W to the library does
perturb reasoning (large content-delta for all), the wisdom
unpacked text does share some vocabulary with problems (non-zero
alignment), and anti-patterns are mostly absent from answers by
default. Without the trigger-conditioning split
(SHOULD\_FIRE vs NO\_FIRE), orthogonality alone collapses into
``yes everything differs in some way.''

\paragraph{This is the actually interesting architectural
lesson.} Orthogonal decomposition is \emph{necessary but not
sufficient}. The researcher's gate (Exp 15) adds a separate
ingredient the agent did not discover: \emph{conditioning on an
externally-supplied trigger class} so that utility and
citability are computed only on the subset where the wisdom
claims to apply. That conditioning is what turns ``can measure
4 things'' into ``can discriminate 4-from-12 candidates.''
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
  \item \textbf{Agent capability confirmed}: propose
    orthogonal components, implement them.
  \item \textbf{Agent capability missing}: propose
    \emph{conditioning} — the architectural move of splitting
    the evaluation population by a wisdom-specific trigger.
  \item \textbf{Researcher contribution that was not
    transferable}: the idea that some candidates are
    \emph{conditional specialists}, not uniform activators,
    and the gate must evaluate them in-class.
\end{itemize}
Teaching the agent \emph{conditioning} as a first-class design
move — not just ``decompose'' — is the next L5 experiment.



\section{Beyond single-advisory benchmarks: a multi-step
research-task pilot}
\label{sec:multistep}

The 100-problem benchmark exercised throughout \S\ref{sec:experiments}
is fundamentally \emph{single-advisory}: one problem, one answer,
one judge verdict. Many research and engineering workflows, however,
demand \emph{multi-round} assumption-revision: propose hypotheses,
gather evidence, update beliefs, re-propose. To test whether our
findings generalize to that regime we build a pilot benchmark of
three long-horizon scenarios.

\paragraph{Benchmark.} Each scenario specifies a \emph{briefing}
(visible to the agent), a \emph{hidden ground-truth mechanism}, a
list of \emph{critical facts} the environment may reveal on demand,
and a list of \emph{plausible red-herrings} also consistent with
surface observations. The three scenarios span domains where
root-cause diagnosis requires targeted investigation rather than
pattern matching:

\begin{center}\small
\begin{tabular}{l l p{4.5cm}}
  \toprule
  Scenario & Domain & Ground-truth root cause (hidden) \\
  \midrule
  Clinical trial & Medicine &
    Drug--drug interaction with warfarin (CYP2C9), amplified by
    chronic kidney disease \\
  ML CTR regression & ML engineering &
    Feature-store schema migration silently renamed a high-importance
    categorical column; serving stack continues to read the old
    column and gets \(0\) \\
  DB slowdown & Systems &
    Postgres minor-version upgrade changed planner statistics;
    a hot query regressed from \texttt{IndexScan} to \texttt{SeqScan} \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\paragraph{Oracle and judge.} To avoid the same-family contamination
identified in \S\ref{sec:selfaudit}, the \emph{oracle} (simulating the
experimental environment) is GPT-5.4, the \emph{solver} is
gemini-3-flash, and the \emph{judge} is Claude Opus 4.6
(three different vendor families). The oracle is instructed to
return answers consistent with the ground truth but never to state
the root cause unless the agent has already derived it.

\paragraph{Agent strategies.} Two baselines are compared:
\begin{description}[leftmargin=1.5em, topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt]
  \item[\textbf{single\_pass}:] The solver reads the briefing and
    writes a final diagnosis with no oracle interaction.
  \item[\textbf{belief\_track}:] The solver maintains an explicit
    hypothesis state (\(\leq 3\) hypotheses with explicit
    confidences) across \(3\) rounds. Each round it proposes
    \(1\text{--}3\) targeted investigations; the oracle answers;
    the agent updates hypotheses in light of the answers. A final
    diagnosis is committed after round \(3\).
\end{description}

\paragraph{Result.} On this small benchmark,
\texttt{belief\_track} converges on all three scenarios; the
single-pass baseline misses the mechanism in the ML-regression
scenario:

\begin{center}\small
\begin{tabular}{l c c}
  \toprule
  Scenario & single\_pass & belief\_track \\
  \midrule
  Clinical trial    & CONVERGED & CONVERGED \\
  ML CTR regression & PARTIAL   & \textbf{CONVERGED} \\
  DB slowdown       & CONVERGED & CONVERGED \\
  \midrule
  Total CONVERGED   & \(2/3\)   & \(\mathbf{3/3}\) \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\noindent The ML scenario is the case whose root cause is a
specific, low-prior mechanism (a silent column rename; the model
sees zero variance on a previously high-importance feature). Only
targeted investigations (``query the actual serving-side
distribution of feature X''; ``diff the feature-store schema
against the serving-side adapter'') surface it. \texttt{single\_pass}
returns a plausible but under-specified ``feature-store issue''
verdict; \texttt{belief\_track} queries the oracle with the
specific diagnostic, receives ``\(user\_category\_id = 0\) for
\(100\%\) of inference requests,'' and commits to the correct
mechanism.

\paragraph{Scope.} \(N = 3\) is a pilot. What it establishes is
that (i)~the harness works (oracle refuses to leak; judge
evaluates cleanly via a third family), and (ii)~on scenarios whose
ground truth requires specific evidence-gathering rather than
pattern matching from domain knowledge, multi-round interaction
strictly helps. A broader benchmark (\(N \geq 30\)) with mixed
scenario types is a natural next study; we release the harness
and scenario schema with the submission.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{The single most important question: do the KEPT wisdoms
have \emph{any} value?} Cross-family re-judgment says ``not as much
as the inner loop thought.'' But it does not say ``zero.'' Claude's
scores are $0.40, 0.47, 0.51$—one collapse and two near-parities.
A future cycle could: (a)~commit wisdoms only if \emph{both} judges
return $\geq 0.55$, turning the gate into an AND; (b)~commit only
when the \emph{judge-family-average} wr
$\bar{\text{wr}} \geq 0.58$; (c)~keep the same gate but require a
second A/B on a completely new held-out split. Any of these would
have rejected all three of our current KEEPs. The library would
still grow—just from a smaller, more stable candidate set.

\paragraph{What does this mean for the prior literature?} Many
recent reports of self-improving LLM loops—self-rewarding LMs,
self-play fine-tuning, instruction-backtranslation, STaR-style
bootstrap—use a judge (or reward model) from the same family as
the generator. Our experiment does not falsify these directly (they
target the \emph{weights}, not a retrieval library), but it raises a
specific testable question: are the reported gains intra-family
judge-preference alignments or true capability gains? A drop-in
cross-family re-judge, on the \emph{identical} generator outputs
from the paper's most-improved checkpoint, is cheap and decisive.

\paragraph{Why did the failure generator never fire?} The intended
signal was ``judge gave B \(\geq\) 8-point margin, accumulate
\(\geq 3\) such in a cluster.'' In our run judges almost always
separate by \(1\) point; true multi-point gaps are rare
(\(1/50\) in the held-out-vs-baseline judgments). Consequently the
buffer fills slowly. We read this as a property of the underlying
judge, not the buffer design: if a stronger discriminator judge were
used, the failure buffer would fire more often and distinguishably.
This is an empirical claim we can test in future work.

\paragraph{The \(+10\)pp gate is strict by design.} A symmetric
relaxation to \(+5\)pp would have kept \(6/11\) success-distilled
candidates (see Figure~\ref{fig:distribution}); \(+8\)pp would have
kept \(4/11\). We believe the tighter gate is the right setting for
a library intended for long-horizon accumulation: a \(+5\)pp false
positive is a candidate that is almost certainly inside the
\(2\sigma\) noise band of a \(50\)-pair test. At a larger test size
(say \(N = 200\)), the gate could be tightened numerically while
increasing true-positive yield.

\paragraph{Cross-LLM is a narrow lens, not a broad one.} W078's
evidence set is \(\{2\) math problems\(\}\). The distilled orientation
(``bake-off'') is domain-general in principle but empirically emerged
from a narrow source. The held-out A/B verifies generality
(\(\text{wr}_{\text{ext}} = 0.60\) across mixed domains), but this is
exactly the kind of KEEP where a future cycle may override the earlier
decision—which is, in turn, exactly why the registry tracks
\texttt{contribution\_gain} and \texttt{activation\_count} on an
ongoing basis.

\paragraph{Is this a solution or an interesting artifact?} Our honest
reading: this is \emph{one cycle}, not steady-state forever-up. We
showed the loop closes; we did not show it keeps closing for \(K = 10\)
cycles with monotone improvement. Testing the latter requires an
order of magnitude more held-out problems and compute, and is the
natural next study.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\textbf{Single LLM family.} Both solver and judge come from the same
\emph{gemini-3-flash} family through a proxy. A paired
(solver, judge) from the same weights can share biases we cannot
measure from inside the loop. The cross-LLM distiller's use of GPT-5.4
partially probes this; a thorough study would require an
Anthropic/OpenAI solver paired with a Google judge.

\textbf{Small held-out set.} \(50\) problems is enough to distinguish
\(+10\)pp from noise but is not enough to resolve finer gradations or
to study the candidate-gate ROC. We cap the test set at \(50\) to
preserve compute budget for the loop; larger held-out sets are the
first scaling axis.

\textbf{Source-credentialed aphorisms.} Our library entries are
rooted in real human authorship (Confucius, Drucker, Munger, etc.).
The success distiller refuses to fabricate sources when asked. Whether
the loop \emph{could} be run with fully self-authored aphorisms is an
open question and probably deserves its own paper.

\textbf{One evolution cycle.} As noted above, we ran one cycle. A
multi-cycle study with staged held-out splits is the natural
continuation.

systematically lower. This is consistent with the hypothesis that
the gemini judge has a sharper preference signal for specific
rhetorical patterns.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We set out to build the simplest honest closed-loop agent that
\emph{proposes} wisdom, \emph{validates} it, \emph{prunes} what
stops being used, and \emph{imports} what a stronger cross-model
generator sees but the solver misses. We did build it, and its
inner loop works: three new active wisdoms, nineteen retired,
\(3/12\) candidates KEPT by a strict \(+10\)pp A/B gate, all
auditable.

Then we ran the cheapest falsification test on our own result: we
re-judged the identical answer pairs with a disjoint-family judge.
\emph{All three KEEPs flipped}, and the agent's own pre-committed
self-rebuttal step had named this exact failure mode first.

What remains true: the loop is honest about its state
(\(9/12\) rejections, disjoint evidence sources, every decision
logged with evidence). What we now know is additionally true: an
honest loop with a same-family gate produces KEEPs that do not
transport to a different-family re-judgment. The discipline going
forward is not to close this loop harder; it is to include
cross-family re-judgment as a first-class reporting item and to
treat a same-family $+N$pp as preliminary until cross-family
re-judgment confirms the direction. Our agent could have told us
this; now we have told the reader.

(\texttt{wisdom\_registry.py}, \texttt{success\_distiller.py},
\texttt{failure\_generator.py}, \texttt{cross\_llm\_distiller.py},
\texttt{pruner.py}, \texttt{validate\_parallel.py}), all figures
(\texttt{paper\_figures.py}), the full \(75\)-entry starter library,
the \(100\)-problem training split, the \(50\)-problem held-out
split, every judgment file, and the \texttt{wisdom\_registry.json}
state at each version are bundled with the submission.

\section{Compute and cost}
\label{app:compute}

Each v20 subprocess on \(50\) problems: \({\sim}20\) minutes wall time,
\({\sim}\) input \(180\)k tokens, output \(40\)k tokens. Twelve
parallel v20 runs in the validation gate: \({\sim}45\) minutes wall
with \(6\) concurrent workers. Eleven parallel A/B judges (\(50\)
pairs each): \({\sim}12\) minutes with \(8\) concurrent judge threads.
Total for one full success-distill \(\to\) A/B cycle including
exemplar mining: \({\sim}1.5\) hours wall time, within the budget of a
single researcher's afternoon.
