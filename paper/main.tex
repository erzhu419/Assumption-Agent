\begin{abstract}
\noindent\textbf{This is a paper about audit methodology, not about
whether self-improving LLM loops work.} The empirical headline is a
\emph{differentiated} result: after building and running a complete
autonomous wisdom-curation loop, none of its three committed
wisdoms clear the gate's own \(+10\)pp threshold on the cached
answer pairs under a six-layer conditionally-distinct audit; but
re-evaluating the original 12 candidates against the inner gate + L1
on 30 preregistered fresh problems with thresholds frozen in advance
(Exp~47, \emph{not} a fresh full loop --- candidate generation and
pruning are not rerun), \(\mathbf{2/3}\) of the original KEEPs
(W077, W078) clear
both the inner gate and a cross-family L1 gate at the
preregistration's laxer threshold (\(0.55\)) on disjoint data, and
the third (W076) collapses on fresh data as well. The scientific
contribution is the audit stack itself, plus a single-loop
demonstration that combining (i) cross-family re-judgment,
(ii)~selection-bias modelling on the inner-loop ranking, and
(iii)~a preregistered fresh-data replication \emph{produces a
diagnostic pattern consistent with} separating selection-driven
cached-data drops (W077, W078) from genuine non-replication
(W076), at this case study's small \(n\) and across only three
KEEPs --- this is suggestive evidence rather than a demonstrated
classification. A formal selection-adjusted analysis
(Exp~45) shows that the L1 drops on cached data are statistically
consistent with regression to the mean on top-3-of-12 noisy gate
selections — so cached-data drops alone cannot tell ``spurious''
from ``real but shrunk''; the preregistered fresh-data replication
is what does. We do not generalise to a field-level claim about
all self-improving LLM loops; we release the audit stack so others
can run the same checks on theirs.

\noindent\textbf{Setup.} Large language models improve markedly when
given retrieval-augmented \emph{methodological scaffolding}: problem
reframes, anti-patterns, evaluation criteria, exemplars of parallel
prior reasoning. A natural next step is an agent that \emph{curates
its own} library of such methodological wisdoms without a human in
the loop. We build this loop as a concrete testbed: a strong
scaffolded solver (v20; pairwise win rate \(0.64\) vs.\ the v16
case-library champion on 100 problems and \(0.88\) vs.\ a
budget-matched baseline on 50 held-out, both same-family
LLM-judged --- see Fig.~\ref{fig:arch} and \S\ref{sec:ratchet})
plus four orchestration modules (failure generator, success
distiller, cross-model distiller, Darwinian pruner), gated by a
\(+10\)pp held-out A/B test.

\noindent\textbf{Inner-loop result.} Over one cycle the library grows
\(75 \to 77 \to 58_{\text{active}} + 19_{\text{deprecated}} \to
59_{\text{active}} + 19_{\text{deprecated}}\). Three new wisdoms
commit (\(W_{076}, W_{077}, W_{078}\)); nine candidates refuse;
nineteen stale entries deprecate. Only \(3/12\) proposals pass — the
gate appears to be doing real work.

\noindent\textbf{The six-layer audit stack.} We then stress-test each
KEEP decision along six \emph{conditionally distinct} axes: (L1)~cross-family
re-judgment with a different LLM family; (L2)~side-randomization
reseed at fixed content; (L3)~sample extension from \(n=50\) to
\(n=100\); (L4)~cross-solver replication across three families;
(L5)~fresh-domain cross-family re-test; (L6)~embedding-space and
LLM-judged faithfulness of the perturbation direction. The layers
intervene on different nuisance variables (judge family, position
seed, sample size, solver family, problem distribution, and
non-pair-wr measurement axis), but they reuse the same KEEPs, the
same cached answer pairs (L1, L2), and overlap in solver and
problem pool (L3, L4); we therefore describe them as conditionally
distinct stress tests rather than as independent evidence streams.
A self-improvement claim that is intended to generalise across
the nuisance variables these layers stress-test --- judge family,
position seed, sample size, solver family, problem distribution,
and faithfulness of the reasoning trace --- should survive
substantively across the layers that target nuisance variables in
its target population. We do not claim ``a real wisdom must clear
every layer'': a wisdom that is genuinely useful for a specific
solver family or problem distribution can fail a layer that
intervenes on that variable; what the layers do is help identify
which estimand (which population, which judge, which solver) the
inner-loop's same-family \(+10\)pp signal actually applies to.

\noindent\textbf{Cached-data audit verdict: \(\mathbf{0/3}\)
KEEPs clear the gate's own threshold on the original cached pairs.}
\(W_{076}\) falls from \(0.64\) (inner-loop) to \(0.40\) under
cross-family judge. \(W_{077}\) falls \(0.60 \to 0.47\).
\(W_{078}\) falls \(0.60 \to 0.51\). \emph{However}, a formal
selection-adjusted analysis (Exp~45) shows that all three of
these L1 drops fall \emph{within} the 95\% range expected from
pure regression-to-the-mean on top-3-of-12 noisy \(n=50\)
measurements; L1 alone therefore cannot distinguish
``judge-fragility'' from ``selection bias on a noisy gate.''
The other audit layers, which selection bias does not predict,
do carry signal beyond regression-to-the-mean: at \(n=100\) two
of three KEEPs stabilise below \(0.55\); embedding-faithfulness
and LLM-faithfulness both show wisdoms cause real perturbations
(magnitude \([0.71,0.77]\)) but in directions \emph{unrelated}
to what the wisdom self-advertises. On 30 GSM8K problems,
\(W_{078}\) ext\ accuracy (29/30) is \emph{below} that run's
gemini-base (30/30); a later run (Exp~42) shows the same direction
on \(W_{076}\) and \(W_{077}\) (each \(26/30\) vs.\ that run's
\(28/30\) base, the latter run's base having drifted from
Exp~32's).
Scaling the L1 panel to expensive-tier judges (Claude Opus 4.6,
GPT-5.4) on all three KEEPs gives six wr\_ext measurements in
\([0.43, 0.54]\); none reach the \(0.60\) gate as a point estimate
(\(0/6\)), though \(3/6\) Wilson 95\% CIs do contain the
threshold, so the gate is not formally rejected at \(95\%\)
confidence on those three cells. Cohen's \(\kappa\) on the full
\(5 \times 50\) cheap\(\cup\)expensive verdict matrix is
\(+0.25\) cross-tier, comparable to within-tier
\(+0.18\)--\(+0.26\) --- the gate's failure to reproduce is not
a judge-tier artifact.
\textbf{Final library delta on the original cached data under
the inner-loop gate's own threshold:} \(\boxed{+0}\). No KEEP
reaches \(\mathrm{wr}_{\mathrm{ext}} \geq 0.60\) as a point
estimate under L1, L3, L5, or the expensive-judge audit. The one
exception is L4 cross-solver (Exp~40): when the solver is changed
to claude-haiku or gpt-mini, \(7/18\) (KEEP, judge) cells reach
\(\geq 0.60\), but no (KEEP, solver) clears \(0.60\) under all
three judges (\(0/6\) under 3-family consensus).

\noindent\textbf{Preregistered fresh-data replication (Exp~47)
recovers some signal.} Applying the audit-stack thresholds frozen
in advance (\texttt{PREREGISTRATION\_v2.md}; inner gate
\(\geq 0.60\), L1 cross-family \(\geq 0.55\) per the
preregistration's laxer replication bound) to a 30-pid fresh
problem split disjoint from all prior samples, \textbf{3 of 12
candidates pass both gates}: \(W_{077}\) (inner \(0.66\), L1
\(0.57\)), \(W_{078}\) (inner \(0.63\), L1 \(\mathbf{0.71}\)),
and \emph{WCAND03 ``凡事预则立''} which the original loop had
REVERTED. \(W_{076}\) does not replicate (fresh inner \(0.41\)).
\textbf{The strict ``\(0/3\) survive'' framing is therefore a
property of the original cached \texttt{sample\_extend\_50}
answer pairs at the \(\mathrm{wr} \geq 0.60\) threshold, not a
universal null.} \(2/3\) of the original KEEPs pass the
preregistered protocol on fresh data \emph{at the point estimate}.
Joint posterior on the exact preregistered decision rule
(\(\theta_{\text{inner}} > 0.60 \land \theta_{\text{L1}} > 0.55\),
Beta(2,2) prior, independence between judges) is W078 \(0.539\),
W077 \(0.370\), WCAND03 \(0.424\) --- meaningful but not
individually decisive at \(n = 30\). The headline ``2/3
replicate'' is therefore a point-estimate statement; the
posterior reading is a probabilistic ordering W078 \(>\)
WCAND03 \(\approx\) W077 \(\gg\) W076.

\noindent\textbf{Honest scope.} The empirical base is twelve
candidates from one loop cycle on one solver family with no
human-annotated ground truth. The paper is a case study, not a
literature-level claim. The preregistered fresh-data replication
is the strongest replication test we have run; combined with
Exp~45's selection-bias finding, the overall reading is that
the audit stack is a useful methodology that catches some real
non-replication and some regression-to-mean, recovers some real
signal on fresh data, and operates with sample sizes too small
to discriminate sharply at threshold \(\pm 0.05\).

\noindent\textbf{Architectural recovery attempt and its limits.} We
then redesign the gate: decompose pair-wr into three orthogonal
components --- trigger-fit, conditional utility, citability ---
and combine them as a trigger-conditioned gate (Exp 15). On the
original 12 candidates the trigger-conditioned gate PASSes
\(4/12\) (vs the naive \(+10\)pp gate's \(3/12\)), including two
REVERTs the naive gate missed. Applied prospectively to the
9 new candidates from Exp 29 (no re-tuning, see Exp 33), the
trigger-conditioned gate PASSes \(0/9\). Combined performance:
\textbf{trigger-conditioned gate \(4/21\)} on the full
\(12{+}9\) set; \textbf{naive \(+10\)pp gate \(3/21\)} on the
same set (the original three KEEPs plus zero from the new
nine). Neither pass rate is above the \(\sim 30\%\) reference
an arbitrary random-inclusion procedure at this threshold would
deliver (\(30\%\) is a reference, not a principled utility
baseline). The architectural redesign recovers \emph{local}
positive signal on the original 12 but
does not generalize: a partial recovery,
not a fix.

\noindent\textbf{Strict-reviewer closure (Exp 25--42).} Further
experiments each target a specific publication objection with
concrete evidence, including: 3-family labeling reliability
(\(\kappa\)); cluster-threshold relaxation (12 \(\to\) 21
candidates); expensive-tier judge scale-up (Exp~35;
cross-tier \(\kappa\) in Exp~37); structured non-panel rating by
gpt-5.5 on \emph{all three KEEPs} (Exp~38, Exp~41 --- 4-dim
Likert: combined-mean preference base \(>\) ext on \(11/30\),
\(13/30\), \(14/30\) for W078/W077/W076 respectively, with three
of twelve dim-candidate cells slightly above zero, the rest below);
minimal English-language replication on MT-Bench
(Exp~39, 0/6 English wisdoms clear \(0.60\) under
\emph{3-family consensus} in either of two independent runs;
\(1/6\) clears single-family gemini in run~1 only and not in
run~2 --- the across-run instability matches the Chinese loop
pattern); cross-solver L4 full audit
(Exp~40, three KEEPs across two new solver families: 7/18 cells
reach a single-judge wr \(\geq 0.60\), but 0/6 (KEEP, solver)
combinations clear \(0.60\) under all three judges); and GSM8K
objective-accuracy probe extended from W078 (Exp~32) to
\emph{all three KEEPs} (Exp~42, all three give a small negative
point estimate on objective accuracy by \(1\)--\(2\) of 30
problems --- direction uniform but not individually significant
by McNemar). The
judge-fragility finding replicates across every conditionally-distinct
axis we have run, and once across a language switch.

\noindent\textbf{Contribution.} (i)~The six-layer audit stack, each
layer specified operationally enough to run against any future
self-improving LLM loop. (ii)~Single-loop evidence that on the
specific Chinese open-ended setup studied here, the default
inner-loop gate's decisions do not reproduce as point estimates
under the cached-data audit at the original \(0.60\) threshold;
but a preregistered fresh-data replication at the
preregistration's laxer L1 threshold of \(0.55\) (Exp~47) shows
\(2/3\) of the original KEEPs cross both gates as point estimates
on disjoint fresh data, plus one originally-rejected candidate.
A genuine Bayesian multilevel model (Exp~51, Metropolis-within-
Gibbs on the full \(50 \times 5 \times 3\) cached + fresh verdict
array, with random effects for candidate, judge family, and pid)
gives joint posterior on the exact preregistered decision rule
\(\{\theta_{\text{inner}} \geq 0.60\} \cap
\{\theta_{\text{L1}} \geq 0.55\}\): W078 \(\mathbf{= 0.763}\),
W077 \(= 0.448\), W076 \(= 0.014\). This properly accounts for
within-pid clustering (the dominant variance component, \(\hat
\sigma_\gamma \approx 0.80\)) — naive per-cell Beta(2,2) gave the
weaker W078 \(= 0.539\) under independence. The hierarchical
posteriors yield a probabilistic ordering W078 \(\gg\) W077
\(\gg\) W076: W078 has \(>76\%\) posterior probability of
clearing both gates; W076 has \(<2\%\); W077 sits at moderate
\(\sim 45\%\). The audit-stack pattern is therefore
\emph{consistent with} a separation between \emph{regression-to-
mean non-reproduction} (W077, W078: cached weak, fresh joint
posterior moderate-to-strong) and \emph{real non-reproduction}
(W076: cached weak, fresh inner wr \(=0.41\), joint posterior
\(<2\%\)). W078's ``replicate'' label is well-supported under
hierarchical inference; W077's is weaker; W076's ``collapse''
label is robust to the modelling choice.
(iii)~A concrete testbed (code, 21 candidates, 150 answer pairs,
cross-family logs) on which others can test alternative audit
designs. (iv)~A partial architectural recovery (trigger-conditioned
gate) that locally rescues signal but does not generalize,
demonstrating the audit stack is harder to satisfy than a gate is
to redesign. We argue cross-family re-judgment \emph{and} at least
one non-pair-wr faithfulness axis should become routine reporting
items for any self-improvement claim.
\end{abstract}

A large language model asked to ``just solve it'' will typically
underperform a version of itself given a well-matched \emph{cognitive
prosthesis}: a problem reframe, a list of anti-patterns, the memory of
two superficially different but structurally parallel prior cases, and
an evaluation rubric. The gap between the raw solver and the scaffolded
solver is usually attributed to chain-of-thought~\citep{wei2022chain},
retrieval-augmented generation~\citep{lewis2020rag}, self-reflection
and verification~\citep{madaan2023selfrefine, shinn2023reflexion}, or
agentic decomposition~\citep{yao2023react}. We call the artifact that
these methods all point to a \emph{wisdom}: a small piece of
methodological knowledge of the form ``in situations exhibiting pattern
\(P\), the right move is \(M\); the canonical failure mode is \(F\).''

A working \emph{wisdom library} is a retrieval corpus of such entries,
selected per problem, foregrounded in the prompt, and paired with
cross-domain exemplars that show what the wisdom looks like when
instantiated in a specific domain. In our setting (see
\S\ref{sec:method}), we use \(75\) such wisdoms, each authored in the
classical tradition (aphorisms from \emph{Yi Jing}, Munger, Drucker,
Mao, Confucius, folk proverbs, etc.) and unpacked into LLM-legible form.
Over six iterative framework revisions (v13 through v20, see
Figure~\ref{fig:arch}), such a scaffolded solver rises from \(74\%\) to
\(86\%\) win rate against a budget-matched baseline
(Fig.~\ref{fig:wisdom-prosthesis}).

The limits of this approach, however, are exactly the limits of human
library curation. Every wisdom that is missed is a residual—a
systematic failure mode the solver walks into repeatedly. Every wisdom
that is wrong becomes dead weight, consuming retrieval slots. And every
wisdom that is \emph{nearly right but poorly phrased} costs the solver
one full pass of the scaffold.

\textbf{The obvious question} — can the agent itself curate its wisdom
library? — has an answer the current literature is already willing
to supply: yes, given a reasonable scaffold and a held-out A/B gate.
\textbf{The question this paper asks instead is the next one: when a
self-improvement loop reports that \(k\) new entries passed its gate,
should we believe it?} Our finding is that under default
reporting conventions the answer is consistently \emph{no}, and that
detecting this failure mode requires audit machinery no single-family
self-adjudicating loop can produce on its own.

Concretely, we build a loop that does everything the literature asks
for (detect residuals, propose entries, filter against a \(+10\)pp
held-out gate, prune, cross-model distill) and then subject each of
its reported KEEP decisions to a six-layer audit stack. Each layer
intervenes on a different nuisance variable; the layers are not
statistically independent (L1, L2, L6 reuse cached answer pairs;
L3 and L4 overlap in solver and problem pool), but each targets a
distinct failure mode:

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{(L\arabic*)}, leftmargin=*, itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Cross-family re-judgment} — re-score the identical
    answer pairs with a different LLM family (Claude Opus 4.6).
  \item \textbf{Side-randomization reseed} — re-run the same judge
    family with a different A/B side-shuffle seed; content held fixed.
  \item \textbf{Sample extension} — extend surviving candidates from
    \(n = 50\) to \(n = 100\) fresh problems.
  \item \textbf{Cross-solver replication} — regenerate answers with
    three different solver families.
  \item \textbf{Fresh-domain cross-family} — a domain-matched but
    previously-unseen problem pool, re-judged by the full three-family
    panel; and a standard-benchmark port (GSM8K).
  \item \textbf{Faithfulness, orthogonal to pair-wr} — embedding-space
    direction of perturbation and LLM-judged citation strictness,
    both measured on data with zero overlap with the gate signal.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{We report a differentiated result.} The inner loop
produces \(3/12\) KEEPs (gate-positive at \(n=50\), gemini-judged).
On the cached data, \(\mathbf{0/3}\) KEEPs clear the \(0.60\)
threshold as a point estimate under L1, L3, L5, or the
expensive-judge audit (Fig.~\ref{fig:audit-sieve}). The cross-
solver layer (Exp~40) is one exception (\(7/18\) cells single-
judge \(\geq 0.60\), but \(0/6\) under 3-family consensus). The
\emph{preregistered fresh-data replication} (Exp~47) is the
strongest test: under the preregistration's laxer L1 threshold
(\(0.55\)), \(W_{077}\) and \(W_{078}\) reproduce and one
originally-rejected candidate (WCAND03) is recovered;
\(W_{076}\) collapses to \(\mathrm{wr} = 0.41\), well below
parity. Final library delta on the cached data at the
\(\geq\!0.60\) threshold: \(\boxed{+0}\); on the preregistered
fresh-data replication at the \(\geq\!0.55\) threshold: \(\boxed{+3}\)
(W077, W078, WCAND03). Our contributions are:

\paragraph{Contribution 1 — The six-layer audit stack (primary).} We
specify each layer operationally enough to run against any future
self-improving LLM loop. Each layer is both (i)~cheap to compute on
top of an existing inner-loop artifact and (ii)~capable of
independently destabilising a KEEP decision the inner-loop gate
treated as confirmed. Cross-family re-judgment (L1) on the cached
data moves \(3/3\) of our loop's KEEPs from above to below the
\(+10\)pp threshold on identical content; preregistered fresh-data
re-evaluation of the original candidate set at the
preregistration's laxer L1 threshold (\(0.55\))
recovers \(2/3\) of these KEEPs on disjoint fresh data. We read this
not as universal judge-fragility but as \emph{the audit stack
distinguishing the two failure modes} --- W076 truly fails to
generalise (fresh inner wr \(0.41 \ll 0.50\)), while W077/W078
were within selection-bias range on cached data and survive
preregistered fresh evaluation.

\paragraph{Contribution 2 — Single-loop evidence that the default
inner-loop gate's verdicts are unreliable on cached data.} Under
a \(+10\)pp single-family A/B at \(n=50\), our loop commits three
wisdoms from disjoint evidence sources; the gate refuses \(9/12\)
proposals (so the gate is not permissive). On the cached data
the three accepts lose \(20\), \(13\), \(9\) percentage points
respectively when re-judged cross-family. \emph{The selection-bias
analysis (Exp~45) shows these drops are statistically consistent
with regression-to-mean on top-3-of-12 noisy gate scores}; L1
alone is therefore \emph{not} sufficient to call them
judge-fragility. On GSM8K \(W_{078}\) ext is below base accuracy
on the same problems; on the preregistered fresh-data
re-evaluation of the original candidate set (Exp~47) \(W_{077}\)
and \(W_{078}\) recover but
\(W_{076}\) does not. The audit stack delivers a
\emph{differentiated} verdict, not a uniform one.

\paragraph{Contribution 3 — A strong scaffold as instrument
(itself reported under same-family judging).} The audit stack
only has bite if attached to a scaffold strong enough to produce
candidates in the first place. Our v20 scaffold's pairwise win
rate (Fig.~\ref{fig:arch}) is \(0.64\) against the v16
case-library champion on 100 problems (\(+28\)pp above parity)
and \(0.88\) against a budget-matched baseline on 50 held-out
problems (\(+76\)pp above parity), with held-out advantage
\emph{larger} than training-split advantage. \textbf{We
note that these scaffold-vs-baseline gains were themselves
measured by a same-family pairwise LLM judge}; under this paper's
own critique of same-family judging, they should be read as
``produced under the standard reporting convention'' rather than
as independently audited results. The audit-stack claims that
follow are about the inner-loop wisdom-acceptance gate, not about
v20-vs-baseline; we do not re-audit the scaffold comparisons in
this paper.

\paragraph{Contribution 4 — Evolution trajectory and the
differentiated audit verdict.} The library moves through
\(75 \to 77 \to 58_{\text{active}} + 19_{\text{deprecated}} \to
59_{\text{active}} + 19_{\text{deprecated}}\) producing three new
wisdoms (W076 \emph{Change with the times}, W077 \emph{No
investigation, no right to speak}, W078 \emph{Is it a mule or a
horse—take it out for a test}). Each is documented with source,
signal, unpacking, exemplars, and evidence trail. On the cached
audit, none of the three clears the original \(0.60\) gate as a
point estimate; on Exp~47's preregistered fresh-data
re-evaluation at the laxer L1 \(\geq 0.55\), W077 and W078 recover
while W076 collapses. The audit verdict is therefore
\emph{differentiated}, not a uniform falsification --- W076 is
the cleanest non-replicating cell, W078 clears both thresholds
on fresh data, and W077 clears only the laxer L1 threshold.

\paragraph{Contribution 5 — A partial architectural recovery that does
not generalize.} Having destabilised the naive pair-wr gate, we
decompose it into three components (trigger-fit, conditional utility,
citability) and build a trigger-conditioned replacement gate
(\S\ref{sec:l1}). On the original 12 candidates this
locally PASSes \(4/12\), including two REVERTs the naive gate had
missed. Applied prospectively (no re-tuning) to the 9 new
candidates from Exp~29, the trigger-conditioned gate PASSes
\(0/9\). Combined: trigger-conditioned gate \(\mathbf{4/21}\) on
the full 12+9 set; the naive \(+10\)pp gate's pass rate on the
same 21 is \(\mathbf{3/21}\). Neither is above the \(\sim 30\%\)
reference an arbitrary random-inclusion procedure would
deliver.
The architectural redesign recovers signal on the seen set but not
on a superset. \emph{The audit stack is harder to satisfy than a gate
is to redesign.} The rescued \(4/12\) is therefore reported as
training-set discrimination, not as a validated library delta;
the prospective \(0/9\) figure is the more representative number.

\paragraph{Contribution 6 — The loop's own introspective bandwidth
is real but insufficient.} A self-rebuttal step run \emph{before} the
cross-family judge predicted exactly the failure mode the cross-judge
then confirmed (all three KEEPs returned \texttt{further\_test} with
judge-preference alignment as the \#1 confound). A separate
meta-audit step diagnoses the inner-loop gate as measurement-fragile
in the \([0.54, 0.62]\) gray zone and proposes a three-tier
replacement. The loop can recognize its own failure modes; it
cannot, without external machinery, produce the disjoint evidence
needed to settle them. This bounds what self-adjudication can
deliver: the loop can flag its own uncertainty but cannot resolve
it in-house.

\paragraph{Contribution 7 — Testbed and artifact release.} We release
the full loop (four orchestrator modules, 21 candidates with their
evidence trails, \(3 \times 50 = 150\) cross-family answer pairs,
and the six-layer audit harness) so that alternative audit designs,
stricter gates, or new self-improvement architectures can be
evaluated on the same substrate. Log provenance is preserved end-to-end.

\paragraph{Scope and non-claims.} We do not claim our loop is
representative of all self-improvement schemes — it is one concrete
instantiation designed to be auditable. We do not claim the audit
stack is complete — L6 (faithfulness) in particular is the newest
layer and admits many operationalizations. Within the scope of our
single-loop case study, we recommend that for retrieval-level
self-improvement claims of the form ``\(k\) new entries passed the
held-out gate,'' L1 (cross-family re-judgment) be reported as a
default audit item. We do not generalise this to a normative claim
about whether such reports are publishable in the absence of L1;
that would require evidence from more than one loop and more than
one solver-judge configuration than we have run here.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Retrieval-augmented and in-context methods.} Chain-of-thought
prompting~\citep{wei2022chain} established that surfacing explicit
reasoning helps; self-consistency~\citep{wang2023selfcons} stabilized
it; reflection/verification~\citep{madaan2023selfrefine,
shinn2023reflexion} closed a one-step inner loop. RAG over document
stores~\citep{lewis2020rag, borgeaud2022retro} and agentic
tool-use~\citep{yao2023react, schick2024toolformer} extended the input
context. Our scaffold (v20) is a retrieval-augmented method over a
\emph{methodological} corpus rather than a factual one.

\paragraph{Self-improvement of language models.}
STaR~\citep{zelikman2022star, zelikman2024star}, self-rewarding
LMs~\citep{yuan2024selfrewarding}, self-play fine-tuning and
instruction-backtranslation~\citep{chen2024selfplay, li2024backtrans}
all close an outer loop at the \emph{weights} level. We close an outer
loop at the \emph{retrieval corpus} level, which is orders of magnitude
cheaper and allows the changes to be auditable.

\paragraph{Audit practices in adjacent LLM-as-judge self-improvement
papers.} Our recommendation --- that L1 cross-family re-judgment
be reported as a default for retrieval-level self-improvement
claims --- rests partly on the observation that this axis is not
routinely reported in the works most directly adjacent to ours.
Table~\ref{tab:audit-practices} restricts attention to
LLM-judged self-improvement: works whose primary acceptance signal
is a same-family LLM preference verdict on open-ended outputs.
Papers that rely chiefly on environment rewards
(e.g.\ Voyager's procedural success signal in Minecraft) or
weight-level supervised/RL training signals (e.g.\ STaR's
fine-tuning on filtered chain-of-thought rationales) do not face
the same judge-fragility issue we describe and are listed with a
``n/a'' rather than a missing-column dash.

\paragraph{Case-based reasoning and skill libraries.} Case-based
reasoning~\citep{aamodt1994cbr} and skill-library
agents~\citep{wang2023voyager, zhu2023ghost, sumers2024agentkb} both
maintain a growing store of reusable units. Voyager~\citep{wang2023voyager}
grows a skill library from procedural success in Minecraft; our
setting grows a \emph{methodological} library from A/B-judged open
reasoning. AgentKB~\citep{sumers2024agentkb} focuses on task-specific
routines, whereas we target \emph{domain-general orientations}
(\(\leq 35\)-character aphorisms with cross-domain exemplars).

\paragraph{Curriculum and program synthesis of thoughts.} Automated
discovery of reasoning structures via Self-Discover
\citep{zhou2024selfdiscover} generates per-problem module stacks; Tree
of Thoughts~\citep{yao2023tot} branches the search. Our library
\emph{outlives} any single problem and is acted on by the solver at
retrieval time, not at generation time. We view this as a complementary
axis: one can always add orientation-level retrieval on top of a
better-synthesized per-problem structure.

\paragraph{Darwinian / evolutionary approaches.} Evolutionary prompt
optimization~\citep{promptbreeder, fernando2023promptbreeder} uses a
selection signal on prompts themselves. We share the Darwinian framing
but select at the \emph{unit-of-retrieval} granularity (one wisdom),
which allows finer-grained credit assignment and interpretable audit.

\paragraph{Cross-model distillation.} Using a stronger model as a
teacher is classical~\citep{hinton2015distillation} and modern at the
instruction-tuning level~\citep{taori2023alpaca, peng2024evolinstruct}.
Our \emph{cross-LLM distiller} (\S\ref{sec:cross-llm}) distills not
answers but \emph{orientations}: what the stronger model's
answer-structure foregrounds that the weaker model's scaffolding does
not.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Preliminaries: the scaffolded solver (v20)}
\label{sec:solver}

We use \emph{gemini-3-flash} as the underlying
generator\footnote{Exposed via the ruoli.dev proxy; our pipeline does
not depend on any property specific to this model.} and an independent
LLM judge (same family) to produce A/B verdicts. A \textbf{wisdom}
\(w\) is a record
\[
  w = (\text{id}, \text{aphorism}, \text{source}, \text{signal},
      \text{unpacked\_for\_llm}, \text{cross\_domain\_examples}).
\]
At inference time a retriever selects the top \(K=2\) wisdoms for
problem \(p\). The solver then runs a three-turn procedure:

\begin{description}[leftmargin=1.5em, topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt]
  \item[\textbf{Turn 0 (frame + rewrite).}] A single JSON call produces
  \((\text{frame}, \text{critical\_reframe}, \text{anti\_patterns},
  \text{evaluation\_criteria}, \text{rewritten\_problem},
  \text{what\_changed})\). The ``rewritten problem'' is the
  subject-of-attention the solver will then see.
  \item[\textbf{Turn 1 (execute).}] The solver is given a PRIMARY FRAME
  block (not a ``context bag''), the rewritten problem, retrieved
  wisdoms with per-wisdom same-domain + cross-domain exemplars, and an
  attention-prior block derived from the problem domain/difficulty
  cluster.
  \item[\textbf{Turn 2 (audit).}] A brief self-reflection pass asks
  whether the draft violates the frame's anti-patterns or fails its
  evaluation criteria, and patches accordingly.
\end{description}

\paragraph{Why this matters for the library loop.} Turn 0 produces a
log of \texttt{what\_changed} fields—the solver's own articulation,
per problem, of ``what is this problem really about.'' These are the
signals the \emph{success distiller} (\S\ref{sec:success}) mines.

\subsection{The library as mutable state}
\label{sec:registry}

The library is a versioned registry whose entries track
\texttt{status} \(\in \{\text{active, deprecated, removed}\}\),
\texttt{activation\_count}, \texttt{last\_activated},
\texttt{contribution\_gain}, \texttt{source}, and a \texttt{keep\_reason}
string. Every mutation bumps a semantic version
(\texttt{v20.0} \(\to\) \texttt{v20.1} \(\to \ldots\)). The registry
is the ground truth an auditor would check to verify that an alleged
library change was actually made against a specific A/B outcome.

\subsection{Closed-loop orchestrator}
\label{sec:orchestrator}

Figure~\ref{fig:loop} shows the four-component loop. Each batch of
problems flows through the solver; the signals captured are: judge
verdicts (for failure mining), Turn-0 reframes (for success mining),
retrieval selections (for pruning), and cross-variant gaps (for
cross-model mining).

  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.9\linewidth]{figs/fig3_library_evolution.png}
  \caption{Library evolution over one closed-loop cycle. The library
    moves from \(75\) original wisdoms to \(77\) (adding W076 and W077
    from the success distiller), is pruned to \(58\) active +

  \label{fig:loop}

\paragraph{Candidate generation.} Three generators run asynchronously
against a shared buffer of recent signals:

\subsubsection{Success distiller}
\label{sec:success}

Given \(N\) recent Turn-0 outputs, we embed the concatenation
\texttt{what\_changed || critical\_reframe} with a multilingual
sentence encoder~\citep{reimers2019sbert} and apply agglomerative
clustering with cosine-distance threshold \(0.4\) and minimum cluster
size \(6\). For each surviving cluster, a GPT-class prompter reads
\(\leq 10\) cluster members plus the current library summary and
either emits \texttt{\{"proposal": null, "reason": ...\}} or a complete
wisdom record with rationale and cluster-coverage PIDs. We measure
novelty by maximum cosine similarity of the proposed
\texttt{unpacked\_for\_llm} embedding against all active entries; we
reject when \(\text{sim} > 0.78\). The rationale: wisdom candidates
are \emph{latent orientations in the solver's own reasoning}, surfaced
by repetition, not by failure.

\subsubsection{Failure generator}
\label{sec:failure}

A symmetric generator listens to judge verdicts and accumulates
problems where the solver lost or tied into a failure buffer. When the
buffer size passes a threshold, a prompter asks: ``do these
\(\geq 3\) problems share a systematic residual pattern a new wisdom
could address?'' This generator is conservative; in our run it never
fired a proposal (buffer grew \(4 \to 9 \to 11 \to 13\); threshold
\(15\)). We treat this as informative and discuss it in
\S\ref{sec:discussion}.

\subsubsection{Cross-LLM distiller}
\label{sec:cross-llm}

For each problem where the solver's answer lost to a comparison
variant's answer by a judge margin \(\geq 1\), a stronger generator
(GPT-5.4, accessed through the same proxy) independently re-solves the
problem. A fresh judge compares our original answer against the
stronger generator's answer. Problems where the stronger generator
\emph{also} wins by \(\geq 1\) are kept. A single distillation prompt
is then shown all surviving
\(\text{(problem, our answer, stronger answer, both judge reasonings)}\)
tuples, and asked to name a single recurring orientation that the
stronger generator's structure foregrounds but the scaffolding does
not. The output is subject to the same novelty gate as the success
distiller.

\subsubsection{Darwinian pruner}
\label{sec:prune}

The pruner scans every available retrieval-selection file and counts
how often each wisdom appeared in the top-\(K\) for some problem.
Active wisdoms with zero activations across the scan are marked
\texttt{deprecated}; already-deprecated wisdoms with zero activations
on a subsequent scan are \texttt{removed}. A \emph{newborn protection}
rule exempts wisdoms created after the newest scan file's
modification time.

\subsection{The A/B validation gate}
\label{sec:gate}

Every proposed candidate is run through the same gate:
\begin{enumerate}[topsep=2pt,itemsep=2pt,leftmargin=*]
  \item Mine \(3\) cross-domain exemplars from the held-out \(50\) using
    a separate GPT-class prompter (deterministic selection from
    \(\leq 30\) candidates).
  \item Build \(\text{lib}_{\text{ext}} = \text{lib}_{\text{base}}
    \cup \{w_{\text{new}}\}\).
  \item Run v20 with both libraries on all \(50\) held-out problems,
    in parallel.
  \item Judge \texttt{ext} vs. \texttt{base} with side-of-presentation
    randomized by a per-PID seed.
  \item KEEP iff \(\text{wr}_{\text{ext}} \geq 0.50 + 0.10\); otherwise
    REVERT.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent The \(+10\)pp threshold is intentionally strict—it refuses
candidates that are right-on-average but undistinguishable from
sampling noise (\(50\) pairs; a one-sample flip changes wr by \(0.02\)).
Six parallel v20 subprocesses and eight parallel judge threads bring
the total wall time for \(11\) candidates + \(1\) base from
\({\sim}6\) hours to \({\sim}1\) hour \emph{without compromising the
evaluation}.

\subsection{What the A/B gate actually measures (decomposition)}
\label{sec:theory}

Before presenting experiments that attack the gate, we state formally
what pair-wr \emph{is} and what it \emph{cannot separate}. Fix a
solver \(S\), a retriever \(R\), a judge \(J\), a wisdom \(w\), and
a problem distribution \(P\). Let \(a_0(p) = S(R(\emptyset, p), p)\)
be the baseline answer and \(a_w(p) = S(R(\{w\}, p), p)\) the ext
answer. The observed quantity is
\[
  \mathrm{wr}_{\mathrm{ext}}(w; S, J, P) \;=\;
  \mathbb{E}_{p \sim P}\!\left[\mathbb{1}\!\left\{
    J(p, a_w(p), a_0(p)) = w\right\}\right].
\]

\paragraph{Conceptual decomposition (informal, not a theorem).}
Let \(Z_w(p) := \mathbb{1}\{J(p, a_w, a_0) = w\} - \tfrac12\). Then
\(\mathrm{wr}_{\mathrm{ext}}(w) - \tfrac12 = \mathbb{E}_p Z_w(p)\).
We find it conceptually useful to think of three sources of lift
that can drive \(\mathbb{E}_p Z_w\) above zero:
\begin{description}[leftmargin=1.5em,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
  \item[\(Z_w^{\mathrm{specific}}\)] lift attributable to the
    \emph{content} of \(w\) --- the particular orientation \(w\)
    advertises, instantiated at \(p\).
  \item[\(Z_w^{\mathrm{generic}}\)] lift attributable to
    \emph{any} extra context of matching length and register
    perturbing Turn-0 away from a default basin (operationally:
    what one would measure by swapping \(w\) with a
    length-matched placebo wisdom).
  \item[\(Z_w^{\mathrm{style}}\)] lift attributable to \(w\)
    producing surface features (register shifts, phrase patterns)
    that \(J\) happens to prefer, independent of problem-specific
    fit.
\end{description}
This is \emph{not} a unique algebraic decomposition, and we do
not claim the three terms are estimable from any single
experiment. Treating the labels as informal probes, the audit
stack's interventions can each be argued to attack one term more
than the others (judge swap stresses \(Z^{\mathrm{style}}\),
length-matched placebo swap stresses \(Z^{\mathrm{generic}}\),
faithfulness probes attempt to estimate \(Z^{\mathrm{specific}}\)
directly). Below we use this informal labelling to motivate the
audit layers, not to prove identifiability.

\paragraph{What cross-family re-judgment can and cannot deliver.}
A single-family \((S, J)\) measurement of
\(\mathrm{wr}_{\mathrm{ext}}(w)\) at fixed \(n\) cannot
distinguish \(Z_w^{\mathrm{specific}}\) from
\(Z_w^{\mathrm{generic}} + Z_w^{\mathrm{style}}\). A measurement
that swaps \(J\) for \(J'\) at fixed \(a_0, a_w\) attacks the
\(J\)-specific style component but \emph{cannot} on its own
identify \(Z_w^{\mathrm{specific}}\). Reasons: (a)~judge families
differ in calibration, verbosity preferences, tie propensity, and
competence on specific problem types, so a swap conflates
``\(J\)-specific style'' with ``\(J\)-vs-\(J'\) full
disagreement''; (b)~two families may share style preferences that
a third would not; (c)~the swap leaves \(Z_w^{\mathrm{generic}}\)
untouched (the generic ``any extra context'' lift is invariant to
which family judges it). What cross-family re-judgment delivers
is therefore a falsifier of the claim ``the original wr\_ext is
robust to the choice of \(J\),'' which is what we use it for. We
do not claim it identifies \(Z^{\mathrm{specific}}\).

\paragraph{Empirical use of the labelling.}
\(W_{078}\) wr\_ext at \(n = 50\) across five judges:
\(\{0.60, 0.51, 0.57, 0.43, 0.43\}\) (gemini, haiku, gpt-mini,
opus, gpt-5.4) --- a spread of \(0.17\), wider than the sampling
CI width of \({\sim}0.13\). With Cohen's \(\kappa\) fair-to-low
across pairs (Exp~37, mean \(\approx 0.18\)--\(0.26\)),
cross-family agreement is itself weak; conditions under which a
strong identifiability claim about \(Z^{\mathrm{specific}}\)
would hold are not met. We therefore use the decomposition as a
conceptual organising frame for the audit stack and as a
descriptive interpretation of the empirical findings, not as a
formal identification result. The cached-data null at the
original \(0.60\) threshold survives under this weaker reading
\emph{when measured on the original solver family with the audit
panel of judges}: no cached-data
audit layer in this configuration assigns a
\(\mathrm{wr} \geq 0.60\) point estimate to the three KEEPs.
\emph{We do not extend this null to other solver families or to
fresh data}: under cross-solver re-runs (Exp~40, L4) several
single-judge cells exceed \(0.60\) for individual KEEPs, and on
fresh data (Exp~47) W078 reaches \(0.63\) inner / \(0.71\) L1
under the original gemini solver. The qualitative picture is
therefore: \emph{cached audit panel + original solver: 0/3 clear
0.60}; \emph{cross-solver and fresh-data evaluations: a
heterogeneous mixture, dominated by W078 clearing on fresh and
some single-judge cross-solver cells exceeding 0.60 but no
3-family-consensus cross-solver cell clearing}. The full
qualitative picture --- not just the cached-data null --- is
documented in the \emph{differentiated finding} of \S Conclusion.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.92\linewidth]{figs/intuition_decomposition.png}
  \caption{Schematic only --- not a formal identification claim.
    \textbf{Left:} the observed inner-loop wr\_ext above the
    \(0.50\) baseline is informally decomposed into three
    contributions (\(Z_{\mathrm{specific}}\): wisdom content;
    \(Z_{\mathrm{generic}}\): any extra context; \(Z_{\mathrm{style}}\):
    judge stylistic preference). \textbf{Right:} each audit layer
    \emph{stresses} (not cancels --- the labelling is informal,
    see \S\ref{sec:theory}) one non-specific source: judge swap
    pressures \(Z_{\mathrm{style}}\), placebo / fresh-domain
    pressures \(Z_{\mathrm{generic}}\), faithfulness probes try to
    estimate \(Z_{\mathrm{specific}}\) directly. We do not claim the
    layers identify the components; we use the labelling only to
    organise the audit-stack interventions.}
  \label{fig:decomposition}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:decomposition} is the schematic of this
theoretical content. Each audit layer \emph{stresses} one nuisance
variable rather than \emph{causally cancelling} a non-specific
term --- the model is not identifiable, so we cannot subtract
components, only stress-test their plausibility:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
  \item \textbf{L1 (cross-family judge)} varies \(J\) while
    holding \(a_0, a_w\) fixed: stresses any \(J\)-specific
    stylistic-preference contribution to the gate verdict.
    Cross-family agreement lets the original verdict be read as
    less judge-dependent; cross-family disagreement exposes that
    a non-trivial fraction of the inner verdict could be
    \(J\)-specific (with stylistic preference shared between
    both families remaining as a residual we cannot rule out).
  \item \textbf{L2 (side-reseed)} varies A/B position while
    everything else is held fixed: stresses position-induced
    judge bias.
  \item \textbf{L3 (\(n\) extension)} shrinks the CI without
    changing the estimand; exposes when an inner-loop
    \(0.60 \pm 0.12\) was noise about a true value
    near \(0.50\).
  \item \textbf{L4 (cross-solver)} varies \(S\) while holding
    \(w, J, P\) fixed: stresses solver-specific interactions
    with the wisdom phrasing.
  \item \textbf{L5 (fresh domain / GSM8K)} varies \(P\): exposes
    when \(Z_w^{\mathrm{specific}}\) was specific to the training-like
    distribution rather than the population the solver will face.
  \item \textbf{L6 (faithfulness)} \emph{indirectly probes}
    \(Z_w^{\mathrm{specific}}\) from outside pair-wr via two
    weak proxies: embedding cosine between perturbation direction
    and \(w\)'s self-description, and LLM-judged citation
    strictness. Neither is a direct estimate of the causal
    component; both are descriptive correlates that can fail in
    either direction.
\end{itemize}

\emph{The decomposition is a taxonomy of confounds, not an
identifiable causal model.} The three terms are not independently
estimable from the experiments we run --- judge family, solver
family, problem distribution, and generic-context effects can
interact, and our interventions do not isolate any single
component. The qualitative reading we draw from
Table~\ref{tab:ci} is that audit layers which target non-specific
terms move the measurement toward \(0.50\); we do \emph{not}
claim this proves \(Z^{\mathrm{specific}} \approx 0\) by
elimination. A formal causal claim would require placebo,
length-matched, and known-effect interventions on each component
separately, which we have not run.

\emph{What the theory does not say.} It does not say wisdom
injection cannot work — only that the default gate cannot
demonstrate it. It does not say the three non-specific terms are
uniformly bad — \(Z^{\mathrm{generic}}\) is likely why solvers
benefit from almost any coherent scaffolding, including random
length-matched context. It does identify exactly which
experimental interventions isolate \(Z^{\mathrm{specific}}\), and
it is the absence of such interventions in current self-improvement
reports that our paper advocates correcting.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\paragraph{A note on staged stress testing (not pre-registration).}
The order in which the six audit layers were added bears directly
on hindsight-bias concerns, so we report it explicitly.
We did \emph{not} pre-register the full stack before running the
inner loop. Instead, the stack grew through staged reviewer
simulation, with each layer added in response to a specific
objection and evaluated with thresholds fixed \emph{before} the
layer's data was observed: \textbf{L1} (cross-family judge,
Exp~1--3) was the first audit, in response to the ``same-family
self-adjudication'' concern; \textbf{L2} (side-reseed, Exp~5) was
added when we noticed position effects on small samples;
\textbf{L3} (\(n{=}50 \to 100\), Exp~8) was added when the CI
analysis showed the \(+10\)pp gate sat inside the gray band;
\textbf{L4} (cross-solver, Exp~26) was added to answer the
solver-family objection; \textbf{L5} (fresh domain + GSM8K,
Exp~31--32) was added to answer the ``no standard benchmark''
objection; \textbf{L6} (non-pair-wr faithfulness, Exp~9--14) was
added to answer the ``pair-wr alone is not identifying the right
quantity'' objection. Layer thresholds (wr \(\geq 0.50\) boundary,
\(+10\)pp margin, \(n_{\mathrm{fire}} \geq 8\), etc.) were chosen
from statistical defaults before each layer's data was observed,
so \emph{threshold hindsight} is bounded; \emph{layer-selection
hindsight} is bounded to the extent that new layers were always
added in response to an external objection, not a desired
conclusion. Timestamps for each layer's first commit are preserved
in the supplementary repository.

\subsection{Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\paragraph{Benchmark.} \(100\) problems for training (\texttt{sample\_100}),
\(50\) disjoint problems for held-out evaluation
(\texttt{sample\_holdout\_50}). Domains: mathematics, science,
engineering, software\_engineering, business, daily\_life. Difficulty:
easy/medium/hard. All problems are open-ended
(\(\geq 200\)-word solutions), judged pairwise by the same independent
LLM judge family.

\paragraph{Baselines.}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*, topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt]
  \item \texttt{baseline\_long}: solver with full token budget, no
    scaffolding.
  \item \texttt{phase2\_v13\_reflect}: case-library + Turn-2 reflection
    (first workable scaffold).
  \item \texttt{phase2\_v16}: case-library + audit (prior champion;
    \(0.86\) vs. \texttt{baseline\_long}).
  \item \texttt{phase2\_v19b}: problem-rewriting only (no frame block).
  \item \texttt{phase2\_v20} (ours): combined
    frame-directive + problem-rewriting.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Measurement.} All experiments report pairwise
win rates. \(N=100\) on \texttt{sample\_100} and \(N=50\) on
\texttt{sample\_holdout\_50}. Standard error \(\sigma \approx
\sqrt{p(1-p)/N}\); for \(p=0.6, N=50\) this is \(\approx 0.07\),
which informs our \(+10\)pp threshold choice.

\paragraph{Tie handling and effective \(n\) (used uniformly).}
Throughout the paper, \(\mathrm{wr}_{\mathrm{ext}}\) is computed
as
\[
  \mathrm{wr}_{\mathrm{ext}} \;=\; \frac{\#\{\text{ext wins}\}}
  {\#\{\text{ext wins}\} + \#\{\text{base wins}\}},
\]
i.e.\ \emph{ties are excluded from the denominator}, and
confidence intervals (Wilson, 95\%) are computed against the
non-tie effective \(n\). Tie counts are reported in the per-cell
verdict tallies and in
Table~\ref{tab:ci}. We use the pre-tie \(n=50\) only as a label
for the original held-out set size; whenever an inferential claim
is made, the effective \(n\) (after tie exclusion) is what
appears in the CI.

\subsection{Ablation: does each component carry weight?}
\label{sec:ablation}

We did not re-run the full cycle with each component disabled (the
scaffold alone costs \({\sim}20\) min/variant on \(50\) problems even
with \(6\times\) parallelism). Instead we inspect the \emph{sources of
the KEEP set} and the \emph{overlap of evidence}:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
  \item W076, W077 come from clusters of sizes \(9\) and \(6\)
    respectively; their evidence is \(\{pid\}\) from
    \(\{\text{business, engineering, software\_engineering}\}\).
  \item W078 comes from mathematical hard-losses; its evidence is
    \(\{\mathrm{mathematics\_0243, mathematics\_0081}\}\).
  \item The pruner's 19 deprecations include \(14\) wisdoms that
    \emph{no retrieval file has ever selected into top-2}---i.e.,
    they were never active under any variant's selection logic,
    not just v20's, on the problem distributions and retriever
    used in this paper. This shows the pruner removes entries
    that are inactive under the current retriever and current
    sample, not that those entries are useless under other
    retrievers, future cycles, or different problem distributions.
    We therefore call these entries \emph{inactive under the
    current retriever and sample}, not \emph{dead weight}.
\end{itemize}

\noindent Evidence sets are disjoint by construction (success from
Turn-0 logs; cross-LLM from hard-loss judgments). This is the minimum
test for complementarity across sources, and it passes.

\subsection{Recursive self-audit: three experiments}
\label{sec:selfaudit}

The loop as described so far \emph{proposes hypotheses and
self-adjudicates} via a held-out A/B gate. But two concerns remain:
(i)~solver and gate judge share the same LLM family, which could mask
judge-preference confounds rather than measure methodological lift,
and (ii)~the gate itself is never inspected for systematic bias. We
run three experiments that give the agent one additional level of
reflection.

\paragraph{Experiment 1 — Cross-judge stability (the central
result of this paper).} The three KEEP decisions are re-judged on
the \emph{same 150 answer pairs} by \emph{Claude Opus 4.6}, accessed
through a proxy that serves multiple vendors so the plumbing is
identical. Results:
\begin{center}\small
\begin{tabular}{l r r c l}
  \toprule
  & \multicolumn{2}{c}{Win rate of ext} & & \\
  \cmidrule(lr){2-3}
  ID & gemini-3-flash & Claude Opus 4.6 & $\Delta$ & Agreement \\
  \midrule
  W076 & 0.64 ($32 : 18$) & \textbf{0.40} ($18 : 27 :  5_{\text{tie}}$)
    & $-0.24$ & \textbf{FLIP} \\
  W077 & 0.60 ($30 : 20$) & \textbf{0.47} ($19 : 21 : 10_{\text{tie}}$)
    & $-0.13$ & \textbf{FLIP} \\
  W078 & 0.60 ($30 : 20$) & \textbf{0.51} ($23 : 22 :  5_{\text{tie}}$)
    & $-0.09$ & \textbf{FLIP} \\
  \midrule
  \multicolumn{4}{r}{Cross-judge STABLE fraction:} &
  \textbf{0 / 3} \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\noindent \emph{The exact same 50 ext-answer strings and 50 base-answer
strings are being compared in both columns}—only the judging
family changes. Every KEEP flips. The magnitude ranges from
\emph{collapse} ($W_{076}$: $0.64 \to 0.40$) to \emph{narrow flip}
($W_{078}$: $0.60 \to 0.51$), but the \emph{direction} is
consistent: a judge from outside the family downgrades every
KEEP.

Interpretation: the inner-loop A/B gate is measuring
\emph{gemini-3-flash's preference for answers that activate
certain rhetorical structures} (``staged migration,'' ``diagnose
first,'' ``bake-off before choosing''), which the three KEEPs
specifically encourage. A new library entry that \emph{activates
those phrasings} wins the intra-family A/B without improving
methodological substance. This is exactly the confound
Experiment~2's self-rebuttal named first, unprompted.

The consequence for self-improving-LLM papers is stark: a
$+N$pp improvement reported with a same-family judge is, until
further notice, a statement about judge-preference alignment, not
methodological uplift. We believe cross-family re-judgment of the
\emph{identical answer pairs} is the cheapest meaningful
falsification test one can bolt onto any such loop.

\paragraph{Experiment 2 — Self-rebuttal.} For each KEEP, GPT-5.4 is
given the wisdom record, the 50-pair A/B evidence, and told to
produce the three strongest counter-hypotheses and a final verdict
(\texttt{accept} / \texttt{further\_test} / \texttt{reject}). The
agent returns \texttt{further\_test} on all three (no
\texttt{accept}s), citing three high-severity confound classes that
recur verbatim across cases:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, topsep=2pt, itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Judge-preference (3/3 cases).} The judge's taste for
    ``investigate first,'' ``compare explicitly,'' ``staged
    migration'' is a well-attested bias; a wisdom that \emph{activates
    such phrasings} can win without improving substance.
  \item \textbf{Held-out distribution match (3/3 cases).} If the 50
    held-out problems happen to over-represent domains where the
    wisdom's trigger condition holds, the measured \(+10\)pp is a
    sample-coverage effect, not a general uplift.
  \item \textbf{Library-interaction (3/3 cases).} The ext library
    contains all base wisdoms plus one; behavioural changes may come
    from retrieval-order shifts, not the new entry per se.
\end{enumerate}
These are exactly the confounds a skeptical human reviewer would
raise. The system predicts them about itself. We see this as a
strength for paper-writing and a concrete roadmap for a follow-up
cycle (stratify test set by triggering conditions; ablate one-wisdom-at-a-time
retrieval; cross-judge).

\paragraph{Experiment 3 — Meta-wisdom about the gate.} The agent is
shown all twelve validation rows (\(9\) REVERT, \(3\) KEEP) and asked
to diagnose systematic bias in the gate itself. It identifies, from
the wr distribution alone, four patterns:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, topsep=2pt, itemsep=0pt]
  \item Nine REVERTs cluster in \([0.46, 0.58]\); six of them are
    within \(1\) pair-flip of the threshold.
  \item \texttt{wr} \(= 0.58\) (\(29\) : \(21\)) and \texttt{wr} \(= 0.60\)
    (\(30 : 20\)) differ by \emph{one pair} but span the life-death
    boundary.
  \item All three KEEPs are at the right tail
    (\(0.60, 0.60, 0.64\)).
  \item The full distribution is right-skewed: \(10/12\) candidates
    have \texttt{wr} \(\geq 0.51\), yet the gate kills everything
    \(< 0.60\).
\end{enumerate}
From these observations the agent proposes a meta-wisdom
\emph{``临界一票，不定生死 (\emph{A critical vote must not decide life and death})''} (``One critical vote must not decide
life and death'') with a concrete gate modification:
\begin{quote}\small
\texttt{wr >= 0.64} \(\Rightarrow\) auto KEEP; \texttt{wr <= 0.52}
\(\Rightarrow\) auto REVERT; \textbf{\(0.53 \leq\) wr} \(\leq 0.63
\Rightarrow\) extend to \(n = 100\) or run a second independent A/B;
in all cases, report confidence intervals rather than a single
point estimate.
\end{quote}
It also produces its own falsifier:
\begin{quote}\small
``If gray-zone candidates remain \(< 0.55\) after extension to \(n
\geq 100\), and current KEEPs hold \(\geq 0.60\) on re-test, then the
threshold is not too strict—the candidates are genuinely weak, and I
will abandon this meta-wisdom.''
\end{quote}

\noindent This is, to our knowledge, the first time a library-evolution
loop has produced an auditable, testable hypothesis
\emph{about its own decision procedure}, stated at exactly the level
of abstraction needed to modify the procedure. We do not automatically
apply the meta-wisdom—its correct use is to run the falsifier in
the next cycle. What we show is that the loop has the introspective
bandwidth to name its own failure mode without external prompting
beyond the log of its own past choices.

\paragraph{How these experiments move the self-adjudication claim.}
Before these experiments, the paper could at best claim
\emph{single-judge self-adjudication inside one LLM family}.
Experiments 1--3 together let us say, concretely:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*, topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt]
  \item \textbf{Cross-family judge agreement} is measurable
    (Experiment 1); we recommend it as a default reporting item
    for retrieval-level self-improvement claims of the form
    studied here;
  \item \textbf{Self-predicted failure modes} are specific and
    useful—the system's rebuttal verdicts \emph{agree with the
    criticisms a human reviewer would raise}, not a rubber-stamp
    (Experiment 2);
  \item \textbf{Meta-level self-hypothesis} is generatable: given a
    log of past gate behaviour, the agent can propose a
    \emph{falsifiable} modification to the gate itself (Experiment 3).
\end{itemize}
But the three audit steps above are still \emph{human-triggered
snapshots}. The next subsection closes the recursion.

\subsection{Experiment 4 — Recursion demonstrated: the agent runs
its own audit-update-reaudit loop to fixed point}
\label{sec:recursion}

Experiments 1--3 each produce useful signal in isolation. To claim
\emph{recursive self-validation} we need the system to
\emph{itself} chain them: audit, update belief, propose a new
gate, apply the new gate, re-audit under it, and decide whether
belief has converged. We implement this as a single script,
\texttt{auto\_recurse.py}, parameterised by
\texttt{max\_cycles}. Each cycle is:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt]
  \item Apply the current gate to the \(12\) validation rows; take
    the resulting \texttt{KEEPs\_t}.
  \item For every \(cid \in \texttt{KEEPs\_t}\) whose cross-family
    score is missing, invoke Claude Opus 4.6 to score the \(50\)
    answer pairs.
  \item Update per-wisdom confidence by a smoothed average of the
    two families' \(\text{wr}\) scores.
  \item Ask GPT-5.4 (with the full two-judge evidence table and
    current gate as context) whether the gate should be tightened;
    accept a new gate iff it is strictly more conservative.
  \item Re-apply the (possibly new) gate; if the gate is unchanged
    from the previous cycle, declare fixed point.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent The run (seeded with Experiment 1's Claude scores for the
three original KEEPs) converges in \textbf{three cycles}:

\begin{center}\small
\begin{tabular}{c l l r r}
  \toprule
  Cycle & Gate in effect & Proposed next gate & \(|\)KEEPs\(|\)
    & Action \\
  \midrule
  1 & \texttt{gemini\_wr>=0.60} &
      \texttt{...AND claude\_wr>=0.50} & \(3\) & tighten \\
  2 & \texttt{gemini>=0.60 AND claude>=0.50} &
      \texttt{...AND claude\_wr>=0.55} & \(1\) & tighten \\
  3 & \texttt{gemini>=0.60 AND claude>=0.55} &
      (unchanged) & \(0\) & \textbf{fixed point} \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\noindent The agent's reasoning at each step (paraphrased from the
GPT-5.4 output with numerics preserved):

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*, topsep=2pt, itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Cycle 1 rationale:} ``\(3/12\) pass the single-judge
    gate, but Claude's re-judgment shows only \(1\) reaches
    \(0.50\)—single-family gate mislabels; require agreement.''
  \item \textbf{Cycle 2 rationale:} ``The surviving candidate
    (WCROSSL01, \emph{是骡子是马}) only reaches Claude
    \(\text{wr} = 0.51\); safety margin too thin; tighten to
    \(0.55\).''
  \item \textbf{Cycle 3 rationale:} ``No candidate passes; further
    tightening would be stricter but adds no safety given zero
    current survivors; hold.''
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Meaning.} The pattern instantiated here --- a
closed-loop LLM agent proposing a gate, testing it with an
out-of-family judge, updating its belief, proposing a
\emph{stricter} gate, and terminating at an empirical fixed
point without human intervention between cycles --- composes
existing pieces (LLM-as-judge cross-family rejudgment, agent-
designed gates, fixed-point termination) into a self-auditing
configuration we have not seen reported in this exact form. The
fixed-point gate eliminates every candidate the original gate had
approved. Put differently: \emph{the recursion, given the tools it
itself asked for, withdraws its original commits}.

\paragraph{Does this commit the agent to ``no library growth ever
again''?} No. The fixed point is a statement about the current
\(12\) candidates. A future cycle running on a \emph{new} batch of
problems can produce new candidates; those would go through the
same (now-tightened) gate; if one of them passes, the library
grows. The claim here is narrower and more defensible: \emph{under
its own self-proposed, cross-family-aware gate, the agent reverses
every one of its current commits}. This is what recursive
self-validation looks like when it works.

\subsection{Experiment 5 — Staged validation funnel

\label{sec:funnel}

The original Phase-4 plan (see project documentation) specified a
\emph{three-stage} validation pipeline: (1) a cheap pilot on
\(n \approx 10\) problems, (2) the full comparative A/B at
\(n = 50\), and (3) a cross-domain generalization check. The
\(+10\)pp gate used in the main inner loop collapses all three
into a single step, which trades rigor for speed. We now run the
original three-stage gate on every one of our \(12\) candidates,
prepend it with a \emph{side-stability} check (the same gemini
judge, re-rolled), and append Experiment~1's cross-family check as
Stage~4:

\paragraph{The side-stability finding.} The most unexpected result
in this table is the \texttt{F} at Stage 1 for \emph{all three
original KEEPs}. The \emph{same} gemini-3-flash judge, running on
the \emph{same} solver outputs, returns
wr\(_{\text{ext}}\)(W076) \(= 0.41\) on the stage-1 pilot (and
\(0.41\) on the full 50-pair re-run), compared with \(0.64\) in
the original validation run. The only difference between the two
runs is \textbf{the Python process random seed that assigns
each pair's left/right presentation order}. In other words, the
original decision depended on a specific side-randomization
seed: with a different seed, \emph{the same judge family returns
the opposite verdict}. The ext--vs--base content is identical in
both runs; only the ordering of presentation to the judge
differs. This is a widely discussed LLM-judge phenomenon
(\cite{zheng2023judging}), but is rarely
surfaced at the level of individual library-mutation decisions.

\paragraph{Putting cross-family and side-stability together.} The
original KEEP for W076 is fragile in \emph{at least three}
conditionally-distinct dimensions:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=0pt]
  \item \textbf{Side-order sensitivity (with a temporal-drift
    caveat).} Same judge family, same content, different
    presentation seed: \(0.64 \to 0.41\). \textbf{Caveat:} the
    side-reseed run was not in the same batch as the original
    judging; \S\ref{sec:kappa} documents that the same gemini
    family at \texttt{temperature}\(=0.0\) drifts wr by up to
    \(\sim 0.17\) on identical content across re-runs. The
    side-reseed effect therefore conflates side bias with
    same-family temporal drift and should be read as ``the
    original \(0.64\) is not stable to side shuffle \emph{or} to
    re-running the same judge a second time,'' not as ``side
    randomization alone explains the move.''
  \item \textbf{Cross-family sensitivity.} Different judge family,
    same content, same presentation: \(0.64 \to 0.40\)
    (Experiment 1).
  \item \textbf{Cross-domain fragility.} Per-domain wr ranges from
    \(0.20\) to \(0.75\); the gate ignores the former and profits
    from the latter.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent Any one of these would have been enough to refuse the
commit. All three fire on the same candidate. The
same-family \(+10\)pp gate is not just one-judge-biased; it is
\emph{seed-biased}, \emph{family-biased}, and
\emph{domain-aggregation-biased}, independently.

\paragraph{The full claim so far.} Combining the four post-hoc
experiments on the cached data: the original inner-loop gate's
\(3\) KEEPs do not clear the \(0.60\) threshold under any of
five \emph{conditionally distinct} stability checks
(side-stability, cross-family, cross-domain, pilot,
recursion-to-fixed-point). These checks share KEEPs, cached
answer pairs, and partially overlapping problem pools, so they
are not statistically independent --- they target distinct
nuisance variables (position seed, judge family, problem
distribution, multi-round agent decisions, etc.) but jointly
provide \emph{cumulative evidence on dependent measurements},
not \(5\) independent confirmations. We do not claim the
underlying wisdoms have zero value; we claim the specific
\emph{procedure} that committed them was insufficient under this
cached-data audit at this threshold.
Sections \ref{sec:exp6} and \ref{sec:exp7} now look for a procedure
that does better.

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*, topsep=2pt, itemsep=0pt]

    extended to \(n = 100\), no KEEP passes even a 2-of-3
    majority at the \(\geq 0.55\) threshold.

(\(W_{076}\)) with no robust family agreement at \(n = 100\).
The agent's own meta-wisdom predicted this; the extension
confirmed it. The loop now has \emph{documented} empirical
grounds for insisting on \(n = 100\) cross-family evidence
before committing, not just \(n = 50\) single-family.

\subsection{Experiments 9 \& 10 — Orthogonal validation: measuring
wisdom validity on axes other than pair-win-rate}
\label{sec:orthogonal}

Experiments 1–8 all measured the same quantity: a candidate's
pair-win-rate against a base library, varying only the judge
family, the side-ordering seed, the sample size, and the domain
partitioning. The collapses we documented could still, in
principle, be dismissed as under-powered measurement of a valid
signal. We therefore design two \emph{orthogonal} tests—
measurements that do not use pair-win-rate at all, and whose
verdicts are statistically independent from any of Experiments
1–8.

\paragraph{Three orthogonal stages (Experiment 9
\texttt{rigorous\_gate.py}).} Each candidate wisdom is scored on:

\begin{description}[leftmargin=1.5em, topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt]
  \item[\textbf{Stage B — Basis coverage.}] Embed the candidate's
    \texttt{unpacked\_for\_llm} with a multilingual
    sentence-transformer; embed all 75 existing library
    entries; compute the residual norm of the candidate after
    projecting onto the library's row span. A high residual
    means the candidate adds a direction the library did not
    already cover. No LLM calls; no pair-wr.
  \item[\textbf{Stage C — Perturbation magnitude.}] For every
    held-out problem, compute
    $\|\,\text{embed}(\mathit{ext}.\texttt{what\_changed}) -
      \text{embed}(\mathit{base}.\texttt{what\_changed})\|$
    across the 50 pids. Average. A high mean means the candidate
    actually changes the solver's Turn-0 reframe, not just its
    final output. Uses meta files that were never judged; no
    pair-wr.
  \item[\textbf{Stage A — Faithfulness alignment.}] Same per-pid
    diff as Stage C, but normalised to a unit vector and
    cosine-aligned with the candidate's own
    \texttt{unpacked\_for\_llm} embedding. High alignment means
    the direction the wisdom pushes reasoning is the direction
    the wisdom \emph{says} it should push. No LLM calls.
\end{description}

\paragraph{Results.} Across all 12 candidates, Stage C passes
everywhere (mean perturbation in the range
\(0.71\text{–}0.77\) unit-vector distance; \textbf{wisdoms do
change reasoning}). Stage B residuals range
\(0.29\text{–}0.49\)—candidates add partial orthogonality to
the library but rarely open a full new direction. Stage A
alignments are in the range \(-0.04\) to \(+0.03\): essentially
orthogonal. The three original KEEPs (W076, W077, W078) are
\emph{not} more aligned than the nine REVERTs. W078 is in fact
the \emph{least novel} in basis-coverage (residual \(0.295\));
the most novel candidate (WCAND11 \emph{若不是品牌}, residual
\(0.488\)) was a REVERT.

\paragraph{Interpretation.} The perturbation is real (Stage C)
but the direction of perturbation is near-orthogonal to the
direction the wisdom self-advertises (Stage A). Concretely, on
\texttt{business\_0089} (a digital-transformation problem
matching W076's claimed trigger), adding W076 to the library
changes the Turn-0 reframe from ``transform tech-vs-people conflict
into \emph{value-chain restructuring}''
(将技术与人的对立转化为价值链重构)
to ``transform into \emph{tech empowering people}, generational
succession, \emph{risk control}''
(转化为技术对人的赋能, 代际传承, 风险控制). The new
output mentions risk control (arguably aligned with W076's emphasis
on risk-controlled phased transition) but also generational
succession (unrelated). \emph{The wisdom acts as a
mixed perturbation, not as a single-direction activator along its
claimed axis.}

\paragraph{Consequence for pair-wr interpretation.} If wisdoms
acted faithfully, pair-wr could be interpreted as ``how much does
this specific methodological nudge improve the answer.'' Since
they do not, a more accurate interpretation is:
\emph{pair-wr measures whatever the LLM does when you hand it
additional context paraphrased from human aphorisms}. That
quantity is dominated by generic context-length and stylistic
effects, not by the aphorism's specific content. This is
conditionally-distinct evidence for the same conclusion as
Experiments 1--8, reached from a measurement axis that does not
share judge or pair-wr structure (it does still share KEEPs and
the same problem pool, so it is not statistically independent of
those layers).

\subsection{Experiment 11 — LLM-judged faithfulness}
\label{sec:faith}

Experiment 9's Stage A uses sentence-transformer embeddings,
which may fail to resolve the methodological distinctions we
care about. Experiment 11 asks the same question in natural
language to Claude Opus 4.6 (cross-family to the solver). For
each (wisdom, pid) pair the judge is shown the wisdom's
\texttt{unpacked\_for\_llm} and the two \texttt{what\_changed}
strings (base and ext), and asked whether the delta reflects the
wisdom's claimed orientation: \textbf{YES} (the delta clearly
activates the wisdom), \textbf{PARTIAL} (an element could be
mapped to the wisdom but is not prominent), or \textbf{NO}
(the delta is unrelated to the wisdom's self-advertised
territory). \(n = 20\) pids per candidate, chosen by a fixed
seed.

  \textbf{WCAND06} & ---- & \textbf{0} & \textbf{5} & \textbf{15} & \textbf{0.00} & \textbf{0.25} \\

\paragraph{The two measurements agree on the core finding.}
Across all 12 candidates the strict YES rate is
\(\leq 0.10\)—the judge almost never sees the candidate's
specific orientation \emph{clearly} activating on a problem.
Most perturbations are labelled \texttt{PARTIAL}: a connection
can be drawn but is not principled. Two candidates fail the
liberal threshold as well (WCAND06 \emph{Spilt milk, look
forward}: 5P/15N; WCAND11 \emph{Brand or commodity}: 4P/16N).
These are the two candidates whose claimed trigger theme (sunk
cost / brand positioning) is narrow enough that Claude cannot
manufacture a partial match on most pids. For the other ten,
Claude manufactures a partial match almost always—but never a
strong one.

\paragraph{Cross-referencing embedding and LLM.} The two
measurements disagree at the \texttt{PARTIAL} threshold—the
embedding space is geometric and does not see the thematic
overlap the LLM judge finds—but agree that \emph{strong
faithful activation is essentially absent across all 12
candidates}. The near-zero
embedding alignment and the near-zero strict-YES rate from two
different measurement frames is strong triangulated evidence
that wisdoms do not activate as their self-descriptions claim.

\paragraph{Consequence for the library-evolution agenda.} The
unit-of-retrieval for the current wisdom library (an aphorism +
unpacked\_for\_llm description) does not map cleanly onto the
LLM solver's Turn-0 reasoning deltas. Pair-wr measures generic
context-perturbation lift; it does not measure whether the
specific wisdom a retrieval picked is the one doing the work.
A future library design should either (i)~retrieve
finer-grained wisdom fragments so that the activation is
resolvable, (ii)~change the solver prompt to explicitly cite
which wisdom is being applied on which step, or (iii)~abandon
the single-scalar-per-wisdom utility framing and model the
library as a space of conditional activators whose validity is
evaluated per-trigger-class rather than per-library-inclusion.

\subsection{Experiments 25--28 — Strict-reviewer rebuttal: multi-family
solver, multi-family labeler, baseline gates}
\label{sec:rebuttal}

We anticipate three standard objections from a strict main-track
reviewer: (a) ``solver is a single LLM family, findings may be
solver-specific,'' (b) ``subset labels in Exp 15/19 are produced by a
single LLM family without inter-rater audit,'' (c) ``no computational
baseline shows your gate beats random or always-accept.'' Experiments
25--28 address each with concrete evidence.

\paragraph{Experiment 25 — Multi-family subset labeling with Cohen's κ.}
Three cheap-tier families from disjoint vendors — gemini-3-flash

\begin{center}\small

\paragraph{Experiment 27 — Self-consistency of gate variants

\label{sec:exp27}

\textbf{We retract the F1-ranking framing.} The audit stack
subsequently rejects \(W_{078}\), so labels derived from the
inner-loop are not external validation; using them to rank gate
variants is circular relative to the paper's central conclusion.
We retain the experiment as a measure of \emph{gate-to-inner-loop
self-consistency} only:

\begin{center}\small
\begin{tabular}{l r r}

\paragraph{Experiment 28 — Robustness of agent gate to labeler
family.} We rerun the agent v2 gate three times with different

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*, topsep=1pt, itemsep=1pt]
  \item \texttt{claude\_only\_full}: single-family (Claude) labels,

  \item \texttt{majvote\_full}: 3-family majority-vote labels, all

  \item \texttt{majvote\_drop\_anti}: majority-vote labels, drop

\paragraph{Reviewer-objection summary.} Of the six anticipated
objections listed in \S\ref{sec:limitations}:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*, topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt]
  \item \checkmark \textbf{\#2 single solver family}:
    closed by Exp 26 (W076 null across 3 solver families)
  \item \checkmark \textbf{\#3 single-family labeler}:
    closed by Exp 25 (κ per subset) + Exp 28 (agent gate
    invariant to labeler)
  \item $\times$ \textbf{\#11 no baseline}:
    \emph{not closed}. An earlier draft cited the Exp~27 F1
    ranking, but that ranking used inner-loop labels which the
    audit stack subsequently rejects (see retraction in
    \S\ref{sec:exp27}). A genuinely external baseline (human
    labels or a pre-registered held-out scoring scheme) remains
    future work.
  \item $\times$ \#1 small N (12 candidates, 50 held-out):
    open; requires compute scale-up
  \item $\times$ \#4 no standard benchmark (GSM8K / MATH):
    open; requires pipeline migration
  \item $\times$ \#5 wisdom ontology hand-picked:
    framing, not experimental — writing tightening only
\end{itemize}

Three of the six anticipated objections have experimental
responses at this scale; we use ``response,'' not ``closure,''
because the responses are themselves single-loop measurements at

\subsection{Experiment 34 — Expensive-tier judge robustness}
\label{sec:exp34}

A reasonable reviewer objection to the audit stack as presented
so far is that all three families in our judge panel (gemini-3-flash,
claude-haiku, gpt-5.4-mini) are in the \emph{cheap} tier. A stronger
signal could plausibly be visible only to higher-capacity judges.
We re-run layer L1 on the strongest surviving KEEP
(\(W_{078}\), the only one that reached majority-of-3 at \(n=50\)
in Exp~7) using expensive-tier judges: \emph{claude-opus-4-6} and
\emph{gpt-5.4}. Identical 50 answer pairs; identical side-randomization
seed as the original L1 run; only the judge family changes.

\textbf{Result: the null replicates under expensive judges.}
\(W_{078}\) wr\_ext: \(0.43\) (claude-opus-4-6, \(n = 46\) non-tie,
95\% Wilson CI \([0.30, 0.58]\)); \(0.43\) (gpt-5.4, \(n = 49\)
non-tie, CI \([0.30, 0.57]\)). Both families agree; neither CI's
upper bound reaches the \(+10\)pp gate at \(0.60\), and both lower
bounds sit at \(0.30\). The mean of \(0.43\) is in fact
\emph{lower} than the cheap-tier L1 wr of \(0.51\) (claude-haiku)
reported in Exp~1, suggesting that higher-capacity judges are, if
anything, \emph{less} convinced by the candidate, not more. Total
cost: 100 API calls, \(\sim\$6.5\), log at
\texttt{phase four/autonomous/exp34\_expensive\_judges\_log.json}.

\textbf{Implication for the audit stack.} The L1 verdict is not a
cheap-tier artifact. A self-improvement loop whose gate decisions
fail under cheap L1 will also fail under expensive L1 — the judge
budget is not where the gate's fragility lives.

within the range of families and \(\kappa\) precision tested.

reinforces the central argument: \emph{an n=50 single-family A/B
verdict is itself not stable over time}, even holding model family
fixed. The audit stack's null, read conservatively, is a finding
about a \emph{distribution of verdicts} rather than a single
reproducible number.

\paragraph{Winner's curse / selection-adjusted analysis (Exp~45).}
The three KEEPs are not random candidates; they are the top of
\(12\) inner-loop proposals selected by passing a noisy \(n = 50\)
gate. Under any noisy measurement, the maxima of a candidate set
are expected to drop on remeasurement even with no underlying
judge-fragility. We fit an empirical-Bayes Beta prior to the 12
observed wr's via maximum marginal likelihood and obtain a sharp
prior centred at \(0.555\) with very high concentration (the 12
observed wr's are tightly clustered in \([0.46, 0.64]\), giving
the marginal likelihood little room to spread the prior). Under
this fit:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Empirical-Bayes shrinkage of the top-3.} The
    posterior expected wr for each KEEP shrinks to \(\approx
    0.555\) (the prior mean): \(W_{076}\) shrinks
    \(0.64 \to 0.56\), \(W_{077}\) and \(W_{078}\) each
    \(0.60 \to 0.56\).
  \item \textbf{Monte-Carlo simulation of selection bias under
    the fitted prior.} Drawing 12 candidates with true wr from
    the fitted Beta and observing each at \(n=50\) yields the
    expected top-1/2/3 inner-loop \emph{maxima} of
    \(0.668 / 0.633 / 0.610\) (vs.\ observed \(0.640 / 0.600 /
    0.600\) --- the observed maxima are \emph{below} the
    selection-only expectation), and expected \emph{drops} on
    remeasurement of \(0.115 / 0.077 / 0.056\), with 95\% upper
    tails at \(0.280 / 0.220 / 0.200\).
  \item \textbf{Comparison to observed L1 drops.} The observed
    inner-loop \(\to\) L1 (cross-family Opus) drops are
    \(0.240\) (W076), \(0.125\) (W077), \(0.089\) (W078).
    \emph{All three observed drops fall WITHIN the
    selection-bias 95\% range.} The W076 drop \(0.240\) is at
    \(0.86\) of the upper-tail \(0.280\); W077's \(0.125\) is
    well inside the \(0.220\) tail; W078's \(0.089\) is barely
    above the mean expected drop \(0.056\).
\end{itemize}

\textbf{This is the strongest interpretation-narrowing result in
the paper.} Pure selection bias (regression-to-the-mean on
top-3-of-12 noisy \(n=50\) measurements) is statistically
sufficient to explain the observed L1 drops. We \emph{cannot},
from L1 alone, distinguish ``judge-fragility'' from
``selection-bias on a noisy gate.'' The audit-stack still has
information beyond L1 --- L3 sample extension, L4 cross-solver,
L5 fresh-domain, L6 faithfulness, and the gpt-5.5 structured
rating (Exp~38, 41) and GSM8K objective accuracy (Exp~32, 42)
each carry signal beyond regression-to-the-mean --- but the
``L1 drop alone'' interpretation is now unsupported. We retain
L1 as evidence for \emph{judge-non-robustness of the verdict}
and cite Exp~45 as the formal selection-adjusted bound. Log:
\texttt{phase four/autonomous/exp45\_selection\_adjusted\_log.json}.

\subsection{A worked example: tier-divided judges on one problem}
\label{sec:worked-example}

To make the audit-stack claim concrete we walk through one (pid,
candidate) pair end-to-end. Problem \texttt{business\_0208} asks
the solver to advise the head of a small consultancy stuck below
break-even after \(18\) months: is the bottleneck product-market
fit, sales execution, or industry-cycle inertia? The candidate is
\(W_{078}\) (\emph{是骡子是马，拉出来遛遛}, ``mule or horse, take
it out for a test'' --- a bake-off orientation: list candidates,
set metrics, run tests, then commit).

\paragraph{Base solver answer (\texttt{library}\(_{v20.1}\), no
additional candidate).} Lays out a three-step
\emph{diagnose-validate-deliver} loop: a three-layer funnel
diagnosis (product / sales / GTM mismatch), a 90-day
``lightweight wedge'' MVI (a low-priced standardised diagnostic
module as a Trojan horse for paid scale-out), and a stakeholder-
management track with explicit milestones at days 30/60/90. Closes
with ``demonstrate self-correction capability to the investors.''

\paragraph{Library-augmented answer (\(+ W_{078}\)).} Same overall
three-section scaffold, but the framing shifts from ``run an MVI
to validate'' to \emph{``isolate variables to identify root cause,
then run a ladder of tests against KPIs.''} New content: an
explicit \emph{cash-flow-positive \(>\) model-validation \(>\)
scale-up} priority ladder; a vertical-niche focus that ``stops
all-industry expansion'' to drive a \(30\%+\) per-project delivery-
cost reduction; and an investor-KPI list (LTV/CAC, delivery-
standardisation rate, key-stage conversion-rate uplift). The
W078 wisdom is visibly active: every recommendation now travels
through a ``measure-then-decide'' filter rather than a
``believe-then-execute'' one.

\paragraph{Verdict spread across the five-judge panel.} On this
specific pid (\(W_{078}\) re-judged on cached Exp~10 base/ext
pair, identical side-randomization seed across all five judges):

\begin{center}\small
\begin{tabular}{l l c}
  \toprule
  Tier & Judge & verdict \\
  \midrule
  cheap     & gemini-3-flash    & base \\
  cheap     & claude-haiku-4.5  & ext  \\
  cheap     & gpt-5.4-mini      & ext  \\
  \midrule
  expensive & claude-opus-4.6   & base \\
  expensive & gpt-5.4           & base \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\paragraph{What this teaches.} Two cheap-tier families prefer the

disputed.}

\subsection{Experiment 38 — Structured non-panel rating as a
human-annotation substitute}
\label{sec:exp38}

The audit stack so far has treated A/B preference as the unit of
verdict. A reasonable reviewer concern is that A/B preference
under-determines what is actually being measured: judges may
agree the wisdom-augmented answer is ``different'' but disagree on
\emph{whether different is better}. Real human annotation is
logistically expensive; we use \emph{gpt-5.5} (a stronger model
not in the 5-judge audit panel) as a substitute, asking it to
produce \emph{structured Likert (1--5)} scores on four
dimensions: substantive content, methodological soundness,
problem fit, and prescriptive burden (reverse-scored). This is not
human annotation and we do not present it as ground truth, but it
gives an external, non-panel reading of the same answer pairs that
goes beyond A/B preference.

Protocol: 30 random pids from the cached \(W_{078}\) base/ext
pool; gpt-5.5 sees both answers in random order and rates each on
all four dimensions. The dimensions and scoring rubric are written
in advance and frozen.

\begin{center}\small
\begin{tabular}{l c c c c}
  \toprule
  Dimension & ext mean & base mean & \(\Delta\) & ext\(>\)base count \\
  \midrule
  substantive content    & \(4.60\) & \(4.73\) & \(-0.13\) & \(3/30\) \\
  methodological soundness & \(3.60\) & \(3.77\) & \(-0.17\) & \(5/30\) \\
  problem fit            & \(4.77\) & \(4.87\) & \(-0.10\) & \(3/30\) \\
  prescriptive burden\(^*\)  & \(3.30\) & \(3.33\) & \(-0.03\) & \(6/30\) \\
  \midrule
  4-dim mean (combined)  & \multicolumn{4}{c}{ext\(>\)base \(=7/30\); base\(>\)ext \(=11/30\); tie \(=12/30\)} \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}\\
\small\(^*\) higher score = lower prescriptive burden = better.
\end{center}

\textbf{Finding.} A stronger non-panel rater doing structured
multi-dimensional Likert finds the wisdom-augmented answer
slightly worse on every dimension, with a combined-mean
preference of \(11/30\) base-better vs.\ \(7/30\) ext-better.
This is evidence \emph{against} the strongest version of a
``judges-just-disagree-stylistically; substance is fine''
rebuttal --- when forced to score on substance and methodology
separately rather than express A/B preference, gpt-5.5 does not
prefer the wisdom-augmented answer either. We do not claim this
rules out the alternative explanation entirely (gpt-5.5 is a
stronger LLM, not a human). The audit stack's null is
corroborated from outside
the panel.

\textbf{Honest scope.} gpt-5.5 is a stronger LLM, not a human.
This experiment is closer to ``a more capable LLM under structured
evaluation'' than to ``human annotation.'' A real human study with
\(n \geq 30\) raters across at least two demographic backgrounds
remains future work and is acknowledged in
\S\ref{sec:limitations}.

\subsection{Experiment 39 — English-language minimal replication}
\label{sec:exp39}

The original loop is Chinese open-ended; the language-scope
critique is therefore real. We run a minimal English replication
under a frozen, English-authored protocol to test whether the
judge-fragility pattern survives a language switch.

\paragraph{Protocol.}
\textbf{Problems}: 30 prompts from MT-Bench (\texttt{writing,
roleplay, reasoning} categories — open-ended, English-authored).
\textbf{Wisdom library}: a 6-entry English-only library, each
entry an English-authored maxim with English-source attribution
and English unpacking (Munger's hammer/nail; Drucker's measure /
manage; the approximately-right-vs-precisely-wrong tradition;
Munger on incentives; the bake-off / 70\%-now-vs-95\%-late
heuristic; Knight's known/unknown/unknowable distinction). No
Chinese content, no translation. \textbf{Solver}: same
gemini-3-flash, same temperature. \textbf{Judges}: the same
3-family cheap-tier panel used as the inner-loop judges in the
Chinese pipeline. \textbf{Decision protocol frozen before
running}: a wisdom is judge-fragile iff at least one family
scores it above \(0.60\) and at least one family scores it below
\(0.50\) on the same answer pairs.

\paragraph{Result.}

\begin{center}\small
\begin{tabular}{l c c c c}
  \toprule
  Wisdom & gemini & haiku & gpt-mini & 3-family mean \\
  \midrule
  EW01 hammer/nail (Munger)         & \(0.38\) & \(0.37\) & \(0.30\) & \(0.35\) \\
  EW02 measure/manage (Drucker)     & \(0.30\) & \(0.29\) & \(0.34\) & \(0.31\) \\
  EW03 approximately right          & \(0.44\) & \(0.37\) & \(0.45\) & \(0.42\) \\
  EW04 incentives (Munger)          & \(0.52\) & \(0.33\) & \(0.31\) & \(0.39\) \\
  EW05 roughly right early          & \(0.45\) & \(0.40\) & \(0.41\) & \(0.42\) \\
  EW06 known / unknown / unknowable & \(0.38\) & \(0.36\) & \(0.27\) & \(0.34\) \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\(\mathbf{0/6}\) English wisdoms reach wr \(\geq 0.60\) under any
single cheap-tier family, let alone the strict
``all-three-families'' criterion. All six 3-family means sit
strictly below \(0.50\): on MT-Bench prompts, the English-only
wisdom library makes the answers \emph{worse}, not better, in the
panel's collective judgement. Mean across-family spread is
\(\mathbf{0.099}\), comparable to the Chinese inner loop's
\(0.09\).

\paragraph{Two-run instability is itself informative.} An
independent earlier run of the same code on the same 30 prompts
produced a different distribution: \(1/6\) with a single family
\(\geq 0.60\) (EW06: gemini \(0.63\), haiku \(0.48\), mini
\(0.37\)) and mean across-family spread \(0.151\). Same prompts,
same code, same model identifiers, different stochastic
outcomes. The two-run gap on identical inputs (\(\sim 0.10\) on
several wisdoms) is itself an instance of the cross-time
instability documented in \S\ref{sec:kappa}: an n=30 single-loop
single-family A/B is not stable across re-runs. We retain the
later (and currently saved) numbers in the table above. The
relevant headline fact \emph{is not} ``no English wisdom clears
under any family in either run'' (the earlier-run EW06 gemini
\(0.63\) cell does cross \(0.60\) under one family, exactly as
in the Chinese inner loop). The relevant headline fact
\emph{is}: ``no English wisdom clears \(\geq 0.60\) under
3-family consensus, in either run.'' Single-family clears do
appear in run 1 (1/6) and were already absent in the saved
run 2 (0/6).

\paragraph{Reading.} The English replication probe shows the
same instability structure as the Chinese loop: at \(n=30\),
single-family same-language A/B verdicts are not stable
across-run or across-family. Specifically, no English wisdom
clears \(\geq 0.60\) under \emph{3-family consensus} in either
of two independent runs; exactly one wisdom (EW06) clears
single-family gemini in one run and not in the other. This
reproduces the Chinese cached-audit pattern (high disagreement
across families and runs) on a frozen English protocol; we do
not extrapolate this to a confidence claim about English
self-improvement loops generally. We do not extrapolate this to
all retrieval-level self-improvement claims --- two language
loops with one solver family and one judge panel is still a case
study --- but it removes ``Chinese-only'' from the list of
possible explanations of the original finding. Cost:
\({\sim}\$10\), \({\sim}30\) min wall time per run. Log:
\texttt{phase four/autonomous/exp39\_english\_replication\_log.json}.

\subsection{Experiment 44 — Positive / negative controls for the
audit stack}
\label{sec:exp44}

A reasonable reviewer concern is that the audit stack might
reject anything: maybe it has high false-negative rate, and the
\(0/3\) verdict says nothing about the candidates' actual
quality. We construct three control ``wisdoms'' and run each
through the same inner-loop gate (gemini judge, \(n=50\) on
\texttt{sample\_extend\_50}) and L1 cross-family
(claude-haiku-4.5):

\begin{description}[leftmargin=1.5em,topsep=2pt,itemsep=2pt]
  \item[\(C\)-PLACEBO] a methodologically vacuous aphorism
    (``\emph{万事互通，举一反三}''  with universally applicable
    unpacking that says nothing operational).
  \item[\(C\)-RANDOM] a non-methodological literary quote (Wang
    Bo's Tang poem ``\emph{落霞与孤鹜齐飞}'') with aesthetic
    unpacking.
  \item[\(C\)-USEFUL] a deliberately useful generic
    methodological hint
    (``\emph{解题三步：列前提、定指标、再推论}'') with explicit
    operational unpacking.
\end{description}

If the audit's \(0/3\) verdict reflects a high false-negative
rate, we expect \(C\)-USEFUL to behave like the original KEEPs.
If the audit has high specificity, \(C\)-PLACEBO and \(C\)-RANDOM
should be cleanly rejected.

\begin{center}\small
\begin{tabular}{l c c c}
  \toprule
  Control & inner-loop wr (gemini) & L1 wr (haiku) & gate decision \\
  \midrule
  \(C\)-PLACEBO     & \(0.16\) (8/49)  & \(0.11\) (5/45)  & REVERT \\
  \(C\)-RANDOM      & \(0.06\) (3/47)  & \(0.02\) (1/49)  & REVERT \\
  \(C\)-USEFUL      & \(0.15\) (7/48)  & \(0.18\) (9/49)  & REVERT \\
  \midrule
  (KEEP reference)  & \(0.60\)--\(0.64\) & \(0.40\)--\(0.51\) & PASS \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\paragraph{Calibration findings.}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Gate specificity is high.} All three controls,
    including \(C\)-USEFUL, score wr \(\leq 0.18\) under both
    inner and L1 judges. The \(+10\)pp gate would never have
    accepted any of them. False-positive rate of the gate, on
    these three controls, is \(0/3\).
  \item \textbf{The original 12 candidates are \emph{not}
    exchangeable with random text.} The 12 candidates' wr
    (\(0.46\)--\(0.64\)) sits \({\sim}0.40\) above the 3
    controls' wr (\(0.06\)--\(0.18\)). The success/failure
    distillation pipeline was selecting candidates that already
    carry signal over arbitrary text. This refines the
    selection-bias narrative of Exp~45 (\S\ref{sec:per-domain}):
    the empirical-Bayes prior fit on the 12 candidates assumes
    exchangeability, which is approximately wrong --- the 12 are
    pre-filtered. Expected drops on remeasurement under a more
    realistic prior would be smaller than Exp~45's
    \(0.05\)--\(0.12\) range.
  \item \textbf{\(C\)-USEFUL's failure is itself informative.} The
    deliberately useful methodological hint (``list premises,
    define metrics, infer'') performs as poorly as the placebos.
    A plausible reading: v20's frame-and-rewrite step already
    elicits structured premise-listing, so an explicit hint to
    do the same is redundant or even \emph{adds noise}. We do
    not draw a stronger conclusion from a single deliberately-
    useful control.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Combined reading: gate has discrimination, KEEPs are
above-random, but \emph{within-12 selection still possible}.}
The 3 KEEPs (\(0.60\)--\(0.64\)) score above both the 9 inner-loop
REVERTs (\(0.46\)--\(0.58\)) and the 3 controls (\(0.06\)--\(0.18\)).
The gap to controls suggests the 12 candidates are pre-filtered;
the gap to REVERTs is what the \(+10\)pp gate latched onto. Exp~45
shows the within-12 top-3-vs-rest gap is selection-bias-consistent
on the inner-loop judge alone. Exp~44 shows the candidates as a
group are far above random text. These are two distinct claims:
\emph{the 12 candidates contain real methodological content
relative to placebos}, but \emph{which 3 are best is not
distinguishable from selection bias} on a single noisy gate.

Cost: \({\sim}\$5\), \({\sim}30\) min. Log:
\texttt{phase four/autonomous/exp44\_positive\_negative\_controls\_log.json}.

\subsection{Experiment 46 --- Stronger positive controls, including
a base-library duplicate}
\label{sec:exp46}

Exp~44's three controls were a placebo, a literary quote, and a
generic methodological hint --- all three failed the gate, which
established \emph{specificity} but did not test \emph{sensitivity}
(would the gate accept a known-useful addition?). Exp~46
constructs three stronger positive controls and runs them through
the same protocol:

\begin{description}[leftmargin=1.5em,topsep=2pt,itemsep=2pt]
  \item[\(C\)-\textsc{math-numeric}] a math/quant-specific hint
    (``track units; sanity-check order of magnitude before
    final number''), evaluated only on
    \{\texttt{mathematics, science, engineering}\} domains where
    the hint is plausibly useful.
  \item[\(C\)-\textsc{scqa}] the well-documented SCQA framework
    from Barbara Minto's Pyramid Principle (situation /
    complication / question / answer), generic but operational.
  \item[\(C\)-\textsc{library-dup}] a deliberate duplicate of an
    existing base-library wisdom (W016 \emph{兼听则明，偏信则暗}
    --- already part of v20's \(0.86\) win-rate library).
\end{description}

\begin{center}\small
\begin{tabular}{l c c c c}
  \toprule
  Control & gemini & haiku & gpt-mini & 3-fam mean \\
  \midrule
  \(C\)-\textsc{math-numeric} (math/sci/eng pids) & \(0.47\) & \(0.12\) & \(0.15\) & \(0.25\) \\
  \(C\)-\textsc{scqa} (all domains)               & \(0.11\) & \(0.09\) & \(0.15\) & \(0.12\) \\
  \(C\)-\textsc{library-dup} (W016 duplicate)     & \(0.17\) & \(0.13\) & \(0.12\) & \(0.14\) \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\paragraph{All three stronger controls also fail the gate.} No
control reaches \(\mathrm{wr}_{\mathrm{ext}} \geq 0.60\) under any
single judge in the panel; mean wrs are \(0.12\)--\(0.25\). This
includes the \emph{base-library duplicate} --- a wisdom already
known to be active in v20's library. Combined with Exp~44, all
\(6/6\) controls we constructed are rejected. The audit's
specificity is therefore \(100\%\) on this control set; its
\emph{sensitivity} remains untested because none of our
constructed positive controls actually function as positives in
this pipeline.

\paragraph{What this teaches about the gate.} The gate has very
high specificity (it correctly rejects placebo, random,
generic-useful, math-specific, SCQA, and library-duplicate
wisdoms), but the absence of any positive control that the gate
\emph{accepts} strongly suggests a structural property of the
pipeline: \emph{adding any new wisdom record at solve time
generally hurts answers vs.\ the base library on this evaluation
distribution}. The exceptions are the three KEEPs that scored
\(0.60\)--\(0.64\) by passing the gate in the first place. The
combined Exp~45 + Exp~46 reading: those three exceptions are
consistent with regression-to-the-mean-on-12-noisy-gate-scores
(Exp~45) plus the structural anti-additivity bias (Exp~46) ---
\emph{i.e.\ the lucky right-tail of an inherently
add-anything-hurts gate}. We do not claim this proves the
KEEPs lack utility; we claim the conjunction of selection bias
and gate-anti-additivity provides a sufficient null model for
the observed pattern.

\paragraph{Caveat.} \(C\)-\textsc{library-dup} is an imperfect
test: it duplicates a wisdom already in v20's library, so the
ext-condition prompt has W016 mentioned twice. A cleaner test
would remove W016 from the base library and reinstate it as a
candidate; we did not do this because it requires re-running v20
with a modified base library, which falls outside the audit-stack
methodology this paper formalises. The negative result
nevertheless suggests adding the same wisdom \emph{again} cannot
move the gate, which is informative.

\paragraph{Sensitivity is empirically established by Exp~47, not
Exp~44+46.} Constructed positive controls of the form ``insert a
candidate into the prompt'' have a structural problem in this
pipeline: the v20 base solver already retrieves relevant wisdoms
from its 75-entry library at solve time, so injecting one more
wisdom block via the candidate slot is partially redundant,
biasing all such controls toward null. The R8 reviewer's exact
sensitivity question --- ``can this audit stack ever accept
something?'' --- is therefore better answered by Exp~47, where the
inner gate plus L1 cross-family judge \emph{do} accept three
candidates on a fresh 30-pid split, including \emph{WCAND03 (凡事
预则立)} which the original loop had REVERTED. WCAND03 is the
empirical positive control: a candidate the original loop's gate
rejected at \(\mathrm{wr} = 0.56\), that on disjoint fresh data
clears both the inner gate (\(0.68\)) and L1 (\(0.57\)) under
preregistered thresholds. The audit-stack protocol therefore
demonstrates non-zero sensitivity \emph{prospectively} on a fresh
split, even though same-distribution constructed controls fail
for the structural reason above.

Cost: \({\sim}\$8\), \({\sim}30\) min. Log:
\texttt{phase four/autonomous/exp46\_stronger\_positive\_controls\_log.json}.

\paragraph{What this changes about the paper's earlier framing.}

that the preregistration commits to (and that we did not change
upon seeing the result), \(2/3\) of the original KEEPs do
replicate, and one originally-REVERTED candidate (WCAND03)
prospectively passes both gates. \emph{The paper's null story is
specifically about the original cached data's stricter
\(\mathrm{wr} \geq 0.60\) reading, not a universal null.} On the
fresh prospective split with the preregistered thresholds, the
audit stack \emph{does} accept candidates --- demonstrating
non-zero sensitivity that Exp~44+46 controls had failed to
establish.

\paragraph{Two important caveats.}
(i)~The L1 threshold of \(0.55\) is laxer than the inner-loop's
\(0.60\); under the inner-loop's own threshold, only WCAND10 and
WCROSSL01 cleanly pass L1 (\(0.57\) and \(0.71\)) while WCAND03's
\(0.57\) just clears \(0.55\). The replication is therefore
threshold-sensitive in the way the audit stack itself is
threshold-sensitive.
(ii)~30 fresh pids is a smaller \(n\) than the original 50;
Wilson CIs at \(n=30\) for wr near \(0.55\) include both
\(0.50\) and \(0.60\) (e.g.\ wr \(= 0.57, n=30\), CI
\([0.39, 0.74]\)). The replication is consistent with real
signal at this \(n\); a tighter test would require \(n \geq 100\)
on the fresh split.

\paragraph{Implications for the paper's primary claims.}
The preregistered fresh-data replication result \emph{partially overturns}
the strict ``\(0/3\) survive any audit measurement'' framing:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=2pt]
  \item \(W_{077}\) and \(W_{078}\) reproduce on fresh data under
    the preregistered protocol; the original audit failure was
    therefore \emph{specific to the cached \texttt{sample\_extend\_50}
    answer pairs and the inner-loop's \(\mathrm{wr} \geq 0.60\)
    threshold}, not a universal property of these wisdoms.
  \item \(W_{076}\) does not replicate. The original \(0.64\) wr
    on the inner loop is best read as a noisy maximum (consistent
    with Exp~45's selection-bias analysis).
  \item WCAND03 emerges as a candidate that the original
    inner-loop gate \emph{would have missed} (false negative).
    The preregistered fresh-data replication protocol catches it.
  \item The audit stack's sensitivity is now empirically
    demonstrated (the gate accepts something on fresh data),
    addressing the Exp~44+46 sensitivity gap.

\subsection{Experiment 49 --- Bayesian re-analysis: Beta-Binomial
sensitivity per cell + DerSimonian-Laird random-effects pooling
across judges}
\label{sec:exp49}

\paragraph{Motivation.} R8/R9 reviewer feedback flagged that the
paper ``frequently uses threshold-crossing rhetoric (FLIP,
COLLAPSE, REPLICATE) even when intervals overlap substantially''
and that an earlier draft of this section described an
``independent Beta-binomial per cell'' as ``hierarchical Bayes,''
which it was not. We re-do the analysis cleanly in two layers
with no new API calls.

\paragraph{Layer A --- per-cell Beta-Binomial sensitivity (not
hierarchical).} For each (KEEP, judge family, evaluation set)
cell, the observed ext-wins among non-tie pairs is modelled as
\(\text{Binomial}(n_{\text{eff}}, \theta)\) with a weakly
informative \(\text{Beta}(2, 2)\) prior. The posterior is
\(\text{Beta}(2 + \text{ext\_wins}, 2 + n_{\text{eff}} -
\text{ext\_wins})\). Per-cell rows in Table~\ref{tab:exp49-bayes}
make this transparent.

\paragraph{Layer B --- DerSimonian-Laird random-effects
meta-analytic sensitivity (frequentist, not a Bayesian
hierarchical model).} \textbf{Naming clarification (R15
callout):} DerSimonian-Laird (DL) is a frequentist
random-effects meta-analysis estimator, not a Bayesian
hierarchical posterior. We use it here as a sensitivity check
against the per-cell Beta(2,2) posteriors of Layer~A: it pools
across judge cells with explicit between-cell variance \(\tau^2\),
which the per-cell Beta-Binomial does not. The reported
``\(P(\theta > t)\)'' values from DL are Wald-style normal-
approximation tail probabilities under the random-effects
sampling distribution, \emph{not} posterior probabilities under
a Bayesian hierarchy. They are reported as a secondary
descriptive statistic alongside the per-cell posteriors. A
genuine Bayesian hierarchical model over the full
\(50 \times 5 \times 3\) cached pid-by-judge-by-KEEP verdict
array (with pid random effects) is future work.

Concretely: cached verdicts on identical answer pairs across five
judge families share content and side-randomization seed but
differ in judge family. Per cell \(j\) with proportion
\(p_j = (y_j+0.5)/(n_j+1)\) (continuity-corrected), let
\(\theta_j = \log(p_j / (1 - p_j))\) with sampling variance
\(v_j = 1/((n_j+1) p_j (1-p_j))\); the random-effects model is
\(\theta_j \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, v_j + \tau^2)\), with \(\tau^2\)
estimated by Cochran's \(Q\) statistic (DerSimonian \& Laird,
\emph{Controlled Clinical Trials}, 1986). The pooled
population-level effect \(\mu\) has standard error
\(\mathrm{SE}_{\mathrm{RE}} = (\sum_j 1/(v_j + \tau^2))^{-1/2}\),
and we compute
\(P(\theta > t) = 1 - \Phi((\mathrm{logit}(t) - \mu) / \mathrm{SE}_{\mathrm{RE}})\)
on the probability scale via inverse-logit. With \(k=1\)--2
fresh judge cells per KEEP, \(\tau^2\) is essentially not
estimable from the fresh data alone; the fresh DL probabilities
are therefore approximate sensitivity calculations, not
authoritative pooling. Source verdicts as in
Layer A: cached \texttt{sample\_extend\_50} under five families
(gemini-3-flash, claude-haiku-4.5, gpt-5.4-mini in Exp~36;
claude-opus-4.6, gpt-5.4 in Exp~35), plus Exp~47 fresh 30-pid

\subsection{Experiment 51 --- Genuine Bayesian multilevel model
(MCMC) with candidate, judge, and pid random effects}
\label{sec:exp51}

\paragraph{Motivation.} R6--R17 reviewers consistently flagged
that Exp~49's DerSimonian-Laird is (a) frequentist not Bayesian,
(b) does not model within-pid clustering when the same answer
pairs are re-judged by multiple families. We close this with a
genuine Bayesian multilevel logistic model fit by Metropolis-
within-Gibbs on the full verdict array.

\paragraph{Model.} For each verdict \(y_{kjp s}\) (KEEP \(k\),
judge \(j\), pid \(p\), set \(s \in \{\mathrm{cached},
\mathrm{fresh}\}\)),
\[
y_{kjps} \sim \mathrm{Bernoulli}(\theta_{kjps}), \quad
\mathrm{logit}\,\theta_{kjps} = \mu + \alpha_k + \beta_j +
\gamma_p + \delta_{ks}
\]
with random effects
\(\alpha_k \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_\alpha^2)\),
\(\beta_j \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_\beta^2)\),
\(\gamma_p \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_\gamma^2)\); fixed-effect
fresh shift \(\delta_{ks} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1)\). Hyperpriors:
\(\mu \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 4)\), \(\sigma_\alpha, \sigma_\beta \sim
\text{Half-}\mathcal{N}(0, 1)\), \(\sigma_\gamma \sim
\text{Half-}\mathcal{N}(0, 0.5)\). Sampler: Metropolis-within-
Gibbs, 1500 burn + 4000 draws, adaptive proposal SDs. Pure stdlib
Python (no PyMC/Stan dependency) for reproducibility.

\paragraph{Posterior on the exact preregistered decision rule.}
Marginalizing pid clustering by integrating
\(\gamma \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \hat\sigma_\gamma^2)\) for a typical
fresh pid:

    \midrule
    \(W_{076}\) & 0.411 & 0.411 & 0.014          & 0.011 \\
    \(W_{077}\) & 0.593 & 0.592 & 0.448          & 0.385 \\

\paragraph{Variance components.}
\(\hat\sigma_\alpha\) (between-candidate) \(\approx 0.20\),
\(\hat\sigma_\beta\) (between-judge) \(\approx 0.15\),
\(\hat\sigma_\gamma\) (between-pid) \(\approx 0.80\). The pid
random-effect SD is markedly larger than judge or candidate SDs:
within-pid clustering is a much bigger source of dispersion than
judge-family disagreement. \emph{This is the dependency structure
that DL and Beta(2,2) per-cell ignore} --- properly accounting for
it strengthens the W078 posterior (0.76 vs 0.54 naive joint) and
strengthens the W076 collapse posterior (0.014 vs 0.07 single-cell
Beta).

\paragraph{What this changes about the paper's claims.}
Under the hierarchical model, the joint preregistered rule
posteriors \(\{0.014, 0.448, 0.763\}\) for \(W_{076}, W_{077},
W_{078}\) provide the cleanest probabilistic ordering. W078 has
posterior \(P > 0.7\) of clearing both gates, supporting its
``replicate on fresh data'' label. W076 has posterior \(< 0.02\),
supporting its ``collapse'' label. W077 sits at moderate \(\sim
0.45\); its label of ``replicates'' on fresh is not unambiguous
under the strictest probabilistic reading. We adopt the
hierarchical posterior as the primary inferential target and
demote the per-cell Beta and DL pool to secondary statistics.
Cost: \(\$0\) (pure analysis on existing logs); MCMC walltime
\({\sim}\)5\,min single-thread. Log:

\subsection{Experiment 15 — Trigger-conditioned gate (L1
measurement redesign)}
\label{sec:l1}

We separate the three dimensions confounded in pair-wr into
explicit components, guided by MC-WM's principle
``\emph{when a module fails its role, switch its role}.'' For
each of \(12 \times 50 = 600\) (wisdom, pid) pairs, Claude Opus
4.6 labels the pid as \texttt{SHOULD\_FIRE}, \texttt{NEUTRAL},
or \texttt{NO\_FIRE} relative to the wisdom's claimed trigger.
Existing cached pair-verdicts and Exp 14 citation judgments are
then partitioned by this label.

Four derived metrics per candidate:

\begin{description}[leftmargin=1.5em, topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt]
  \item[\(\text{trigger\_fit\_rate}\):] fraction of pids labelled
    as SHOULD\_FIRE or NEUTRAL (sanity: self-description is at
    all coherent).
  \item[\(\text{util\_when\_fires}\):] pair-wr on SHOULD\_FIRE
    subset (actual utility given trigger presence).
  \item[\(\text{cite\_when\_fires}\):] any-cite rate on
    SHOULD\_FIRE subset (Exp 14 citation locatability given
    trigger presence).
  \item[\(\text{harm\_when\_absent}\):] pair-wr on NO\_FIRE
    subset (should approximate 0.50; no-op when not triggered).
\end{description}

wisdom validity to a scalar — each component has its own data
source and cannot be pumped by noise in the others.

\paragraph{Positive signal on the tuning set.}
The trigger-conditioned gate retains \textbf{W077} and
\textbf{W078} — two of the three original KEEPs that had
collapsed under pooled n=100 pair-wr (Exp 8) — and additionally
rescues two REVERTs: WCAND01 (\emph{上工治未病 (\emph{prevent disease (Huangdi Neijing)})}; util\_fire =
\(0.59\) on 22 should-fire problems) and WCAND02
(\emph{别高效解决一个被看错的问题 (\emph{Don't efficiently solve a misread problem (Drucker)})}; util\_fire = \(0.71\) on
14 should-fire problems). W076 fails not on utility but on
\emph{breadth}: Claude labels only 5/50 problems as should-fire,
below the \(n_{\text{fire}} \geq 8\) minimum — the wisdom's
self-description is empirically too narrow for the holdout
distribution.

This is the minimal evidence for a non-vacuous gate: positive
signal where pooled pair-wr sees noise, reliable negatives on
the obviously-weak candidates (WCAND06, WCAND11 with \(n_{\text{fire}}
\leq 3\)), and refused-for-good-reason verdicts on borderline
cases (WCAND03 util = 0.54 barely fails, WCAND04 harm\_absent =
0.67 penalises wrong-domain firing). The validated library
delta under this gate becomes:
\[
  \boxed{+2\ \text{retained (W077, W078)} \;+\; 2\ \text{rescued (WCAND01, WCAND02)} \;-\; 1\ \text{withdrawn (W076)}}
\]
on the held-out 50. \textbf{This is a tuning-set result, not a
validated delta.} The trigger-conditioned gate was designed on
these 12 candidates and its thresholds chosen with knowledge of
their verdicts; the boxed expression above is therefore the
gate's training-set discrimination, not its prospective
performance.

\paragraph{The prospective test (Exp~33) does not support the
generalization claim.} The same gate applied without re-tuning
to the 9 new candidates from the relaxed-cluster pool (Exp~29,
\S\ref{sec:rebuttal2}) produces \(0/9\) PASS; combined with the
\(4/12\) tuning-set rate this is \(4/21 \approx 19\%\). For
reference, a random-inclusion procedure at the threshold
implicit in this gate would deliver \(\sim 30\%\) PASS by
construction; the observed rate is below that reference
(\(95\%\) Wilson CI \([0.08, 0.38]\); one-sided binomial
\(p = 0.14\) against \(p_0 = 0.30\)). We use the random-30\%
figure only as an arbitrary calibration point, not as an
external utility baseline. The \(+4/-1\) box above should
therefore not be cited as a validated library improvement. The generalization
gap is itself evidence for the audit stack's central claim:
an orthogonal-component gate remains a single-family
single-set measurement and inherits the same identifiability
problem formalised in \S\ref{sec:theory}.

\subsection{Experiment 16 — Trigger-indexed library (L2
structural redesign)}
\label{sec:l2}

The current library is a flat list; retrieval is by embedding
cosine over \texttt{unpacked\_for\_llm}. We design a
trigger-indexed alternative: a 10-class canonical taxonomy

library wisdoms is labelled with 1–3 trigger classes (Claude);
each of the 50 held-out problems is labelled with 0–3 active

\subsection{Experiment 17 — Citation-aware solver (L3 solver
redesign)}
\label{sec:l3}

The retrospective citation audit in Experiment 14 depended on a
judge attributing wisdoms to answer passages after the fact.
Prospective citation removes that indirection: the solver's
prompt is modified to require explicit
\texttt{used\_wisdoms: [\{id, applied\_at, reason\}]} output
alongside the answer, referencing its provided retrieval set.
We run this on 20 held-out pids with the base 75 wisdoms plus
\(W_{076}\) always retrieved, and measure three rates:

\begin{center}\small
\begin{tabular}{l r l}
  \toprule
  metric & value & note \\
  \midrule
  Target shown in retrieval & 20/20 (100\%) & W076 forced into top-2 \\
  Any wisdom cited          & 19/20 (95\%)  & solver reliably declares usage \\
  \(W_{076}\) cited given shown & 19/20 (95\%) & prospective target-cite rate \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Prospective target-citation (\(95\%\)) \emph{substantially
exceeds} retrospective any-cite for the same wisdom from
Experiment 14 (\(82\%\)). Requiring citation in output directly
forces specific integration. Quotes recovered include:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.2em, topsep=2pt, itemsep=2pt]
  \item \texttt{business\_0062}: ``without disrupting cash flow,
    complete a smooth transition from `selling goods' to `selling
    brand''' (不中断现金流的前提下，完成从「卖货」到「卖品牌」的
    平稳过渡) — W076's
    ``continuity'' + ``staged transition''.
  \item \texttt{software\_engineering\_0256}: ``treat this
    optimization as a `paradigm migration on an in-service system'
    \ldots carry out phased transformation against failed
    historical premises'' (将此次优化视为「在役系统上的范式迁移」
    \ldots 针对失效的历史前提进行分阶段转型) — W076's
    \texttt{unpacked\_for\_llm}
    phrasing is reproduced nearly verbatim.
  \item \texttt{mathematics\_0246}: ``while preserving the
    symmetry of modular forms as a `production continuity' premise''
    (在保持模形式对称性这一「生产连续性」的前提下) — W076's
    ``preserve core assets'' translated to a math-domain analogue.
\end{itemize}

\noindent Prospective citation is an architectural requirement,
not a judging requirement: once the solver emits citations, no
external judge is needed to answer ``where did the wisdom
apply?''. The cost of requiring citations in output is
negligible; the benefit is that \emph{any} downstream gate can
condition on solver-declared usage rather than retrospective
attribution, breaking the circularity that every experiment up
to Experiment 14 had to live with. The open question — does the
\(95\%\) citation rate translate to a utility gain when paired
with the trigger-conditioned gate (Experiment 15) — is a direct
follow-up.

\subsection{Experiments 18--19 — Can the agent design and implement
a replacement gate? (summary; full in Appendix~\ref{app:agent-gate})}
\label{sec:meta}

Two pilots probe whether the replacement-gate design step itself can
be delegated. \textbf{Exp~18} (design): Claude Opus 4.6, given the
failure evidence for the naive gate plus a seven-source data inventory
and the MC-WM ``switch-the-module-role'' principle, produces an
orthogonal decomposition gate with \(0.28\) structural similarity to
the researcher's Exp~15 design and three independently-motivated
components the researcher did not include. \textbf{Exp~19}
(implementation): given its own spec plus a clean data API, the agent
writes 460 lines of Python in 3 attempts; 2/4 components execute
end-to-end, the others hit data-contract field-name slips. Filling in
the missing bodies strictly from the agent's own spec, the gate
passes 12/12 candidates — identifying the architectural move the
agent did \emph{not} discover: \emph{conditioning} the gate on a
wisdom-specific trigger class. Full protocol, agent outputs, code
traces in Appendix~\ref{app:agent-gate}.


\section{Multi-step research-task pilot (summary;
full in Appendix~\ref{app:multistep})}
\label{sec:multistep}

The audit stack is validated on single-advisory A/B pairs. As a
proof-of-concept that the framework extends to sequential tasks, we
run a multi-round research-task harness on three scenarios where an
oracle holds private evidence and the agent must plan multi-turn
queries. The harness produces clean verdicts via a third-family
judge; the solver uses its library throughout. Two of three
scenarios favour multi-turn + wisdom over one-shot prompting; one is
a tie. This is a pilot (\(N=3\)); a broader benchmark with mixed
scenario types is a natural next study. Full harness, scenarios, and
verdicts in Appendix~\ref{app:multistep}.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{The single most important question: do the KEPT wisdoms
have \emph{any} value?} Cross-family re-judgment says ``not as much
as the inner loop thought.'' But it does not say ``zero.'' Claude's
scores are $0.40, 0.47, 0.51$—one collapse and two near-parities.
A future cycle could: (a)~commit wisdoms only if \emph{both} judges
return $\geq 0.55$, turning the gate into an AND; (b)~commit only
when the \emph{judge-family-average} wr
$\bar{\text{wr}} \geq 0.58$; (c)~keep the same gate but require a
second A/B on a completely new held-out split. Any of these would
have rejected all three of our current KEEPs. The library would
still grow—just from a smaller, more stable candidate set.

\paragraph{What does this mean for the prior literature?} Many
recent reports of self-improving LLM loops—self-rewarding LMs,
self-play fine-tuning, instruction-backtranslation, STaR-style
bootstrap—use a judge (or reward model) from the same family as
the generator. Our experiment does not falsify these directly (they
target the \emph{weights}, not a retrieval library), but it raises a
specific testable question: are the reported gains intra-family
judge-preference alignments or true capability gains? A drop-in
cross-family re-judge, on the \emph{identical} generator outputs
from the paper's most-improved checkpoint, is cheap and decisive.

\paragraph{Why did the failure generator never fire?} The intended
signal was ``judge gave B \(\geq\) 8-point margin, accumulate
\(\geq 3\) such in a cluster.'' In our run judges almost always
separate by \(1\) point; true multi-point gaps are rare
(\(1/50\) in the held-out-vs-baseline judgments). Consequently the
buffer fills slowly. We read this as a property of the underlying
judge, not the buffer design: if a stronger discriminator judge were
used, the failure buffer would fire more often and distinguishably.
This is an empirical claim we can test in future work.

\paragraph{The \(+10\)pp gate is strict by design.} A symmetric
relaxation to \(+5\)pp would have kept \(6/11\) success-distilled
candidates (see Figure~\ref{fig:distribution}); \(+8\)pp would have
kept \(4/11\). We believe the tighter gate is the right setting for
a library intended for long-horizon accumulation: a \(+5\)pp false
positive is a candidate that is almost certainly inside the
\(2\sigma\) noise band of a \(50\)-pair test. At a larger test size
(say \(N = 200\)), the gate could be tightened numerically while
increasing true-positive yield.

\paragraph{Cross-LLM is a narrow lens, not a broad one.} W078's
evidence set is \(\{2\) math problems\(\}\). The distilled orientation
(``bake-off'') is domain-general in principle but empirically emerged
from a narrow source. The held-out A/B verifies generality
(\(\text{wr}_{\text{ext}} = 0.60\) across mixed domains), but this is
exactly the kind of KEEP where a future cycle may override the earlier
decision—which is, in turn, exactly why the registry tracks
\texttt{contribution\_gain} and \texttt{activation\_count} on an
ongoing basis.

\paragraph{Is this a solution or an interesting artifact?} This is
\emph{one cycle}, not steady-state forever-up. We showed the loop
closes once; we did not show it keeps closing for \(K = 10\)
cycles with monotone improvement. Testing the latter requires an
order of magnitude more held-out problems and compute, and is the
natural next study.

\paragraph{What is actually novel in the audit stack, and what is
not.} We are explicit about this so that reviewers need not infer
it. Of the six layers, two carry the paper's methodological claim:

\begin{description}[leftmargin=1.5em,topsep=2pt,itemsep=2pt]
  \item[\textbf{L1 (cross-family re-judgment)}]
    is non-trivial. The current self-improvement literature does
    not routinely re-judge across judge families, and our empirical
    finding is that doing so flips \(3/3\) of an inner loop's KEEP
    decisions. L1 is cheap (the answer text is already cached) and
    specifiable enough to drop into any existing loop.
  \item[\textbf{L6 (faithfulness via non-pair-wr axes)}]
    is the newest layer and the most preliminary. Our two
    operationalisations --- embedding-direction alignment
    (Exp~9+10) and LLM-judged citation strictness (Exp~11+14) ---
    are proxies, not validated faithfulness measures. Embedding
    cosine between \(\Delta a\) and \texttt{unpacked\_for\_llm} is
    a weak surrogate for ``the wisdom causally drove the answer's
    reasoning''; a single-LLM PARTIAL/YES rubric on citation is
    sensitive to prompt wording (Exp~17 boosts citation rate to
    \(95\%\) by adding an explicit \texttt{used\_wisdoms} field,
    without proving the wisdom \emph{caused} better reasoning).
    A future version of this stack should validate L6 on
    synthetic cases with a known inserted wisdom effect, include
    human faithfulness labels, and add a placebo-wisdom and
    length-matched-random-context control to estimate
    false-positive / false-negative rates of the faithfulness
    layer itself.
\end{description}

\noindent Layers L2 (side-reseed), L3 (sample extension), L4
(cross-solver), and L5 (fresh domain and standard-benchmark port)
are \emph{standard statistical hygiene} — no individual layer is
novel. What \emph{is} novel is their bundling into an audit stack
whose layers are each specifiable operationally, each attach to a
single non-specific term in the decomposition of
\S\ref{sec:theory}, and are cheap enough to include as default
reporting. Put bluntly: this paper's primary contribution is
\emph{methodological advocacy with a concrete null result as
evidence that the advocacy is needed}, not a new statistical
method. We believe the field needs the advocacy, and the
accompanying testbed, more than it needs another method.

\paragraph{Objections we have \emph{not} closed.} Several
reasonable reviewer concerns remain open; we list them rather
than leave them to footnotes.

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Gate-design freedom / hindsight.} We tried naive
    pair-wr, three-tier pair-wr, then the trigger-conditioned
    gate. The number of gate designs tried before the final
    ordering was fixed is not zero, and Exp~15's thresholds were
    chosen with knowledge of the 12 verdicts. Exp~33's null on
    the 9 prospective candidates partially addresses this but a
    proper pre-registered gate-design protocol is future work
    (a preregistration template is included in the supplementary;
    see PREREGISTRATION\_v2.md).
  \item \textbf{Audit stack itself is post-hoc.} Layers L1--L6
    were added in order in response to reviewer-simulation
    objections, not pre-registered. \S\ref{sec:experiments}
    documents the order. Threshold values were chosen from
    statistical defaults before each layer's data was observed,
    bounding threshold-hindsight; layer-selection hindsight is
    bounded only to the extent that each layer responded to an
    external objection rather than a desired conclusion. A truly
    pre-registered application of the stack to a freshly-run
    candidate-generation loop (i.e.\ rerunning candidate
    generation, distillation, and pruning on fresh data, then
    auditing the new KEEPs) is the strongest available correction
    and is future work.
  \item \textbf{Solver-family scope (partially closed by Exp~40).}
    The original 12 inner-loop candidates were all judged on
    gemini-solved answers. Exp~40 (\S\ref{sec:exp40}) re-runs the
    full cross-solver L4 audit on the three KEEPs with
    claude-haiku and gpt-5.4-mini as the SOLVER (not just judge),
    on the same 50 holdout pids and with the 3-family cheap
    panel: \(7/18\) (KEEP, judge) cells reach wr \(\geq 0.60\)
    under at least one judge, but \(0/6\) (KEEP, solver)
    combinations clear \(0.60\) under all three judges --- the
    cross-family fragility persists across solver families.
    A second \emph{full inner-loop cycle} on a non-gemini solver
    (proposers, gate, audit) is still open.
  \item \textbf{Uneven probe coverage across the audit layers.}
    Several individual probes test only a subset of the 3 KEEPs:
    Exp~26 (cross-solver, original L4) only \(W_{076}\); Exp~31
    (fresh math) only \(W_{078}\); Exp~33 (\(9\) new candidates
    prospective) gemini-only judging. The full panel (3 KEEPs
    \(\times\) all relevant axes) is not balanced. Exp~35,
    Exp~40, Exp~41, Exp~42 close most of these gaps for the 3
    KEEPs, but a balanced factorial design (all KEEPs across
    all solvers and all judges and all problem subsets at the
    same \(n\)) is future work.
  \item \textbf{One evolution cycle.} We ran the loop once. We
    have no data on whether a multi-cycle run converges, cycles,
    or drifts.
  \item \textbf{No human ground truth on the open-ended Chinese
    pool (partially mitigated by GSM8K + gpt-5.5 rating).}
    Cross-family disagreement is not the same as
    ``the wisdoms lack substantive utility.'' Two
    LLM-substitute checks bound the alternative explanation:
    Exp~41 (\S\ref{sec:exp41}) shows gpt-5.5 (a stronger
    non-panel rater under structured 4-dim Likert) prefers base
    over ext on all three KEEPs; Exp~32 + Exp~42
    (\S\ref{sec:exp42}) show all three KEEPs give a small
    negative point estimate on GSM8K objective accuracy
    (\(1\)--\(2\) problems out of 30, direction uniform but not
    individually McNemar-significant at this \(n\)). An
    actual human study with \(n \geq 30\) raters across at
    least two demographic backgrounds remains future work, but
    the alternative explanation (``audit panel is wrong; original
    judge was right'') is not supported by any of the available
    LLM-substitute proxies.
  \item \textbf{Positive controls remain unresolved
    (Exp~44 + Exp~46).} Exp~44 (\S\ref{sec:exp44}) runs three
    controls (placebo, random literary quote, generic-useful
    hint); Exp~46 (\S\ref{sec:exp46}) adds three stronger
    positive controls (math-specific, SCQA framework,
    base-library duplicate). All \(6/6\) controls fail the gate
    (wr \(\leq 0.25\)). The gate has very high specificity but
    \emph{no positive control we constructed actually functions
    as a positive}, so the audit's sensitivity remains untested.
    Combined with Exp~45's selection-bias finding, the
    parsimonious null model is now: the gate is structurally
    anti-additive (adding any wisdom hurts), and the three
    KEEPs are the lucky right-tail of 12 noisy gate measurements.
    A genuinely positive control --- a synthetic task family
    where a specific known-useful intervention deterministically
    helps, or a human-curated wisdom validated independently
    against task outcomes before insertion --- remains future
    work and is the strongest single missing piece.
  \item \textbf{L6 operationalization is under-specified.}
    Embedding cosine and citation strictness are two proxies
    for faithfulness; they could in principle disagree. On our
    12 candidates they converge (both near-zero for all), but
    this is a single data point of operationalization
    agreement, and neither proxy is itself validated against
    ground-truth faithfulness.
  \item \textbf{Expensive-judge panel is partial.} Exp~34--35
    cover \(2\) expensive judges on the \(3\) inner-loop KEEPs
    (\(150\) judge calls); a full \(2 \times 21\) replication on
    every candidate is deferred.
\end{itemize}

\noindent We regard the first three (gate-design hindsight,
audit-stack post-hoc-ness, solver-family scope) plus the lack of
human ground truth and positive controls as the most
consequential; the others are resource-bounded rather than
conceptually open.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\textbf{Held-out exposure via dynamic same-domain exemplars
(real but symmetric).} The v20 solver pipeline
(\S\ref{sec:gate}, code at
\texttt{phase one/scripts/validation/phase2\_v15\_exemplar\_framework.py})
includes, at solve time, a same-domain exemplar dynamically
selected from the evaluation pool's other pids: for evaluation
on pid \(p\), the prompt is augmented with the most-similar
in-pool pid in the same domain, with that pid's cached
v15-class answer text. This is a real exposure to the
evaluation distribution. \emph{Crucially, it applies symmetrically
to the base condition (no-wisdom) and the ext condition
(with-wisdom): both prompts have the same same-domain exemplar
augmentation.} The relative measurement
\(\mathrm{wr}_{\mathrm{ext}} = \mathrm{ext} / (\mathrm{ext}
+ \mathrm{base})\) is therefore approximately
exposure-neutral, and we believe the comparison remains
informative about whether the wisdom adds anything beyond the
base condition under the same exposure. A separate
cross-domain mining pass exists in
\texttt{validate\_parallel.py:91--278}; it would have stored
PID-bound exemplars in the candidate wisdom records under
\texttt{wisdom\_diverse\_exemplars.json}. We verified
empirically that for our 12 candidates, this file contains zero
WCAND/WCROSS entries: the cross-domain mining was either skipped
(the ``\(\geq 3\) exemplars'' guard branched out) or never run
on this candidate set. The candidates' \texttt{cross\_domain\_examples}
visible in the wisdom records (\texttt{success\_distilled\_candidates.json})
are abstract scenarios generated by the success distiller, with
no PID grounding; the v20 prompt formatter does not consume them.
An earlier draft of this Limitations section described an
exemplar mining leak as serious; that draft was based on a
reading of the code path that was not actually exercised on
our 12 candidates. We retain the symmetric same-domain
exposure as the actual extent of held-out involvement.

A clean re-run of the inner loop with strict pool separation
(no same-domain exemplar from the eval pool) is straightforward
to implement and remains future work; given the symmetry
argument above we do not expect it to materially change the
wr\_ext numbers on this candidate set.

\textbf{Language scope: primarily Chinese open-ended problems.}
The full pipeline (scaffold v20, 75-entry wisdom library, the
1768-problem benchmark pool) is authored in Chinese and designed
for \emph{open-ended, multi-domain} reasoning. Our audit stack's
null verdict on the three KEEPs is established on this
distribution. Exp~39 runs a minimal English-language replication
on 30 MT-Bench prompts with a frozen 6-entry English-only wisdom
library; \(0/6\) English wisdoms clear wr \(\geq 0.60\) under any
single family across two independent runs, and all 3-family means
sit below \(0.50\). We report this as one further language loop,
not as cross-lingual generality. GSM8K (Exp~32) is included as a closed-form English
portability probe, not as a representative test; the small
negative point estimate on \(W_{078}\) GSM8K accuracy there
(29/30 vs 30/30, not significant by McNemar) should be read as
``the wisdom does not appear to transfer out of the Chinese
open-ended scaffold,'' not as confident harm. A full English
replication of the closed loop (English
proposers, English wisdoms, English gate, English audit) remains
future work.

\textbf{Single LLM family for solver.} The solver is
\emph{gemini-3-flash} throughout. A paired (solver, judge) from the
same weights can share biases we cannot measure from inside the loop.
Exp~26 partially probes this (same wisdom re-solved by three solver
families: mean wr\_ext \(= 0.41\), no family \(\geq 0.50\)), but a full
re-run with an Anthropic/OpenAI solver is the natural next step.

\textbf{Judge budget.} The audit stack's three judges are in the cheap
tier (gemini-3-flash, claude-haiku, gpt-5.4-mini). Exp~34 re-runs the
L1 judge on W078 with expensive-tier judges (Claude Opus 4.6, GPT-5.4);
see \S\ref{sec:exp34} for the result. This is one family combination
on one candidate; a fuller judge-budget sweep is deferred.

\textbf{Small held-out set per layer.} \(50\) problems per layer is
enough to distinguish \(+10\)pp from noise but not to resolve finer
gradations. We report binomial CIs in
\S\ref{sec:stats} for core claims; stratified ROC analysis is deferred.

\textbf{Reproducibility via third-party proxy.} API access to the
three families is mediated by the \texttt{ruoli.dev} OpenAI-compatible
proxy, not the official vendor endpoints. Temperatures and seeds are
recorded in each log file; however, exact-token reproduction requires
the proxy's routing state at run time, which is not under our control.
The decision trajectory (pid $\to$ verdict $\to$ KEEP/REVERT) is
deterministic given the cached answer text; re-runs against official
endpoints are a pending replication.

\textbf{Source-credentialed aphorisms.} Our library entries are
rooted in real human authorship (Confucius, Drucker, Munger, etc.).
The success distiller refuses to fabricate sources when asked. Whether
the loop \emph{could} be run with fully self-authored aphorisms is an
open question and probably deserves its own paper.

\textbf{One evolution cycle.} We ran one cycle. A multi-cycle study
with staged held-out splits is the natural continuation; our audit
stack's operational specification does not change with the cycle count.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We set out to build the simplest fully-logged closed-loop agent
that \emph{proposes} wisdom, \emph{validates} it, \emph{prunes}
what stops being used, and \emph{imports} what a stronger
cross-model generator sees but the solver misses. We did build it,
and its inner loop works as specified: three new active wisdoms,
nineteen retired, \(3/12\) candidates KEPT by a strict \(+10\)pp
A/B gate, every decision traceable to its evidence.

We then ran the cheapest available falsification on our own
result: re-judging the identical answer pairs with a
disjoint-family judge. On the cached data, all three KEEPs fall
below the \(+10\)pp gate; the agent's pre-committed self-rebuttal
step had named this exact failure mode first. The cached-data
\emph{drops}, however, are statistically consistent with what
top-3-of-12 selection would produce on noisy \(n{=}50\) measurements
of an empirical-Bayes--fit population mean (Exp~45) — so a
cached-data drop alone does not distinguish ``the signal was
spurious'' from ``the signal was real but the point estimate
shrank toward the mean.''

We partially resolve this ambiguity (Exp~47) by re-evaluating the
original 12 candidates against the inner gate plus L1 cross-family
judge on 30 preregistered fresh pids at thresholds fixed in
advance. \emph{This is not a re-execution of the full loop:
candidate generation, distillation, and pruning are not rerun.}
Two of the three original KEEPs (W077, W078) clear both the inner
gate and the L1 gate at the preregistration's laxer threshold
(0.55) on disjoint data; W076 fails the inner gate already
(wr 0.41) and does not survive. The differentiated finding is
therefore: cached-data strict re-judgment yields a \(\boxed{+0}\)
library delta (Exp~35 expensive judges and Exp~36 cheap re-judgment
do not cross \(+10\)pp on cached pairs); preregistered fresh-data
re-evaluation of the original candidates at the laxer L1 yields a
\(\boxed{+2/3}\) replication, with the W077 fresh L1 evidence
markedly weaker than W078 (\(P(\theta>0.55)\) of \(0.55\) vs
\(0.95\); see Exp~49). One signal (W076) is non-replicating in
both directions and is the cleanest example of a same-family gate
accepting a candidate that has no cross-family or fresh-data
support. Two signals (W077, W078) recover under the preregistered
protocol, with the caveat that fresh \(n=30\) and the W077 fresh
L1 result is moderate rather than strong.

The substantive finding is that a six-layer cross-family audit
stack — combined with selection-bias modelling and a
preregistered fresh-data re-evaluation of the original
candidates — produces a diagnostic pattern consistent with
\emph{distinguishing}
selection-driven cached-data drops from genuine non-replication.
In our single case study, exactly one of three KEEPs is
diagnosed as a true non-replication; the other two recover. The
recommendation we draw, scoped to this single-loop case study, is
that (i) cross-family re-judgment, (ii) selection-bias modelling
on the inner-loop ranking, and (iii) preregistered fresh-data
re-evaluation of the original candidates be reported jointly as
default audit items for
retrieval-level self-improvement claims of this form, and that a
same-family \(+N\)pp on \(n{=}50\) be reported as preliminary
until at least one of these three confirms the direction. Whether
this generalises to other classes of self-improvement claim is an
empirical question we do not settle here.

\section{Reproducibility appendix}
\label{app:repro}

\paragraph{Model identities.} All API calls flow through the
\texttt{ruoli.dev} OpenAI-compatible proxy, with three family routes:
Google (model \texttt{gemini-3-flash}), Anthropic (models
\texttt{claude-haiku-4-5-20251001}, \texttt{claude-opus-4-6}), OpenAI
(models \texttt{gpt-5.4-mini}, \texttt{gpt-5.4}). The proxy is used
uniformly for both solver and judge roles across every experiment;
no experiment mixes proxy and direct vendor access.

\paragraph{Temperature and sampling.} Solver Turn-0 (frame + rewrite)
and Turn-1 (execute): \texttt{temperature} \(= 0.2\text{--}0.3\),
\texttt{max\_tokens} \(= 500\) (frame) / \(1000\) (execute). Judge
calls: \texttt{temperature} \(= 0.0\), \texttt{max\_tokens} \(= 300\).
These values are constants in the experiment scripts and not
hyperparameter-tuned.

\paragraph{Random seeds.} The A/B side-randomization uses
\texttt{random.Random(hash(pid) \% 2**32)} with the problem\_id as
seed key, giving deterministic per-pid allocation across re-runs of
the same experiment. The side-shuffle audit layer (Exp~5) changes
the seed offset to \texttt{hash(pid) + 17} to decouple from the
original run. Problem-subset sampling (Exp~31) uses
\texttt{random.Random(2028)} as the global seed. All seeds are
recorded in the script header and carried into log JSON.

\paragraph{Data sources and cached artifacts.} The 1768-problem pool
lives in \texttt{phase zero/benchmark/problems/\{mathematics,
science, business, software\_engineering\}.json}. Cached answers
per-experiment sit under \texttt{phase two/analysis/cache/answers/};
cross-family judge logs under \texttt{phase four/autonomous/}.
Every audit layer's verdict is recoverable from its log file alone:
\{pid\(\to\)winner\} maps are preserved end-to-end, so the exact
decision trajectory is deterministic given the cached answer text.

\paragraph{What is \emph{not} reproducible.} Exact output tokens
depend on the proxy's routing state at call time; re-running the
same prompt two weeks later is not token-identical even with fixed
temperature and seed. This is an inherent property of hosted LLM
APIs, not specific to our setup. Our reproducibility target is one
level up: given the cached answer text (\texttt{.../answers/*.json}),
every audit-layer verdict in the paper is exactly reproducible.

\paragraph{Supplementary archive.} Code, prompts, cached answers,
and log JSONs are bundled in the supplementary tarball. A single
entry point, \texttt{phase four/run\_audit\_stack.sh}, reproduces
Tables~\ref{tab:ci} and the six-layer audit table given the cached
answers.

\section{Experiments 18--19 — Agent-designed replacement gate (full)}
\label{app:agent-gate}

\subsection{Experiment 18 — Can the agent design the
replacement gate?}
\label{sec:meta}

So far in this paper the replacement gate (Experiment 15) was
designed \emph{by the researcher} — we identified the confound,
chose the decomposition, and wrote the validator. The
closed-loop-agent claim requires one further step: can the agent
independently rediscover this kind of decomposition, given the
same inputs the researcher had?

We test this with a pilot. The agent is Claude Opus 4.6
(cross-family to the solver, so not the same model doing the
validation). It receives three inputs: (a)~the code of the four
failed gates (Experiments 1/2/3/8 gates), with failure evidence
sketched in prose; (b)~an inventory of the seven data sources in
the project with schemas; (c)~the MC-WM design principle
``when a module fails its role, switch its role.'' It is asked,
in three sequential prompts, to (P1) diagnose the common
confound, (P2) enumerate orthogonal data sources, and (P3)
design a replacement gate with components, thresholds, and
independence argument. Experiment 15's design is \emph{withheld}
from its context. A third-family judge (GPT-5.4) then scores
the agent's output against Experiment 15's design.

\paragraph{Phase 1 — Diagnosis.} The agent correctly identifies
``LLM pairwise win-rate (wr)'' as the common confound and
attributes its failure to four stacked sources of variance:
judge identity, judge seed, problem-subset sampling, and
finite-\(n\) sampling. This matches our own diagnosis.

\paragraph{Phase 2 — Orthogonal sources.} The agent produces
six candidate sources, of which three coincide with ones we
used and three are ones we did \emph{not} use:

\begin{center}\small
\begin{tabular}{p{4.5cm} p{3.5cm} p{3cm}}
  \toprule
  Agent-proposed source & Overlap with researcher & Novel? \\
  \midrule
  reframe meta-cognition records &  our Stage C (Exp 9)   & — \\
  wisdom–problem semantic alignment & our Stage B (Exp 9) & — \\
  holdout difficulty stratification & our Stage 3 (Exp 5) & — \\
  \textbf{answer-level textual diff}
    & — & \textbf{new}\\
  \textbf{anti-pattern structural coverage}
    & — & \textbf{new}\\
  \textbf{cross-LLM judge agreement ($\kappa$)}
    & similar to Exp 1 but formalised & \textbf{refined}\\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\paragraph{Phase 3 — Design.} The agent produces
\texttt{orthogonal\_decomposition\_gate\_v1} with 4 components:

\begin{description}[leftmargin=1.4em, topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt]
  \item[\texttt{reframe\_depth}] ``median reframe distance \(\geq 0.25\)''
    (Stage-C analogue)
  \item[\texttt{substantive\_content\_delta}] ``median content delta
    \(\geq 0.20\)'' — novel: explicit answer-text diff threshold
  \item[\texttt{wisdom\_problem\_alignment}] ``median alignment z
    \(\geq 1.5\)'' (closest to our trigger\_fit\_rate)
  \item[\texttt{antipattern\_avoidance}] ``median avoidance improvement
    \(\geq 0.15\)'' — novel: check anti\_patterns listed in Turn-0
    are actually avoided in Turn-1 answer
\end{description}

judge).} The third-family judge's verdict is \textbf{AGENT\_REACHED\_RELATED\_DESIGN}
with structural-similarity \(0.28\). Component-level matches:

The agent's design is \emph{not a copy} of the researcher's.
It recovers the decomposition principle (2/4 components

dimensions (\texttt{reframe\_depth}, \texttt{substantive\_content\_delta},

include, while missing two the researcher did
(\texttt{cite\_when\_fires}, \texttt{harm\_when\_absent}).

\paragraph{What this establishes and what it does not.}
\emph{Establishes}: (i)~the design \emph{process} of
decomposing a confounded scalar into orthogonal components is
transferable to an LLM agent given the same inputs; (ii)~the
agent does not merely mimic—its output contains novel
axes—so it is exercising independent design judgment, not
pattern-matching on the researcher's artefacts; (iii)~the
combined researcher + agent design is a strict superset, not
a zero-sum disagreement.
\emph{Does not establish}: whether the agent's gate would
empirically out-perform the researcher's when run on the same
12 candidates (Phase 4–6 of the full protocol — code
generation, execution, and comparative verdict — were scoped
out as stretch goals). A complete autonomy claim would close
the loop on Phase 4–6; this experiment ends at Phase 3.

\paragraph{Implication for autonomy.} The earlier closed-loop
agent (\S\ref{sec:orchestrator}) could \emph{propose and
validate object-level wisdoms} under a fixed gate. It could not
replace that gate. Experiment 18 shows it can, in principle,
also replace the gate — the same LLM-based reasoning that
produces wisdoms can produce replacement gates when given a
structured prompt chain. Experiment 19 (below) closes Phase 4–6.

\subsection{Experiment 19 — Phase 4–6: the agent writes,
executes, and commits its own gate code}
\label{sec:meta-exec}

Exp 18 stopped at Phase 3 (design spec). Exp 19 asks: if we also
hand the agent an API inventory and a correction loop, can it
actually write, run, and produce verdicts from its own design?

\paragraph{Protocol.}
\emph{Phase 4}: the agent is shown (i) its Phase 3 design spec,
(ii) a \(\approx 50\)-line clean data API
(\texttt{exp21\_data\_api.list\_candidates}, \texttt{candidate\_info},
\texttt{per\_pid\_records}), and (iii) the project path. It must
emit one code block implementing the spec.
\emph{Phase 5}: the script is saved and executed. On failure, the
agent is given its code + traceback + original task and asked to
correct (up to 2 retries).
\emph{Phase 6}: the produced verdicts are normalised and
overlap-compared with Exp 15's trigger-conditioned gate on the
same 12 candidates.

\paragraph{Result.} After three attempts the agent produced a
460-line Python file that executes end-to-end. Two of its four
components extract data correctly (\texttt{substantive\_content\_delta},
\texttt{antipattern\_avoidance}); the other two
(\texttt{reframe\_depth}, \texttt{wisdom\_problem\_alignment})
use field names (\texttt{prompt}, \texttt{reframe}) that do not
exist in the provided API (which uses \texttt{problem} and
\texttt{critical\_reframe})—a documentation-contract slip, not a
reasoning failure. The gate's verdict is therefore overly strict:
with two components mechanically stuck at \texttt{False}, no
candidate passes.

\begin{center}\small
\begin{tabular}{l r r}
  \toprule
  Metric & value & note \\
  \midrule
  Agent-generated code & 460 lines & 3 attempts \\
  Components that executed correctly & 2/4 & by inspection \\
  Components with data-contract bugs & 2/4 & field-name slips \\
  Agent gate PASS on 12 & 0 & over-strict (bugs force FAIL) \\
  Exp 15 gate PASS on 12 & 4 & W077, W078, WCAND01, WCAND02 \\
  Agreement rate (PASS=PASS $\vee$ FAIL=FAIL) & 0.67 & 8/12 both FAIL \\
  Disagreement on PASS & 4/12 & Exp 15 PASS, agent FAIL due to bugs \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\paragraph{Interpretation.} The agent demonstrably writes working
gate code. The two components whose data contract it got right
produce sensible per-candidate signals. Where the agent fails is
in cross-referencing the API's exact field names.

\paragraph{Targeted correction attempt (Exp 19b).} We test
whether a single human-supplied diagnosis (``you used
\texttt{rec['prompt']}; the API exposes \texttt{rec['problem']}'')
unblocks the stuck components. Mechanical substitution of four
field names on the original code makes \texttt{reframe\_depth}
execute without \texttt{KeyError}, but reveals a
\emph{second-layer} bug in the text-distance helper
(\texttt{median\_dist} \(=1.0\) for all candidates, indicating
the embedding function silently returns the max-distance
fallback), and the other component now raises \texttt{NoneType}
subscripting on a new line. A full agent-produced gate requires
iterative correction through several layers of bug, not a single
field-name fix.

\paragraph{Refined take-away.} We distinguish three capability
claims, with increasingly strong evidence:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*, topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt]
  \item \textbf{Agent can design} a replacement gate that
    structurally resembles the researcher's (Exp 18, sim \(0.28\),
    \(+3\) novel dimensions).
  \item \textbf{Agent can implement} the design as runnable
    Python (Exp 19, 460 lines execute without human edits).
  \item \textbf{Agent can produce a fully-correct gate} in a
    single pass — this we do NOT show. Exp 19's code has
    \(\geq 3\) bug layers that silently failed (wrong field
    names → returns empty lists → misleading default
    thresholds). Successive LLM correction rounds fix each
    layer one at a time.
\end{itemize}
The gap between ``agent-in-the-loop with schema help'' and
``agent-autonomously-correct-on-first-try'' is still there.

\paragraph{Exp 19c — completing the gate strictly per the
agent's spec and running it.}
We manually fill in the three missing evaluator bodies strictly
from the agent's Phase-3 spec (the two truncated classes plus
the never-written \texttt{antipattern\_avoidance}), keeping the
spec's thresholds (\texttt{reframe\_depth} median\_dist $\geq
0.25$; \texttt{substantive\_content\_delta} median\_dist $\geq
0.20$ AND $60\%$ above $0.15$; \texttt{antipattern\_avoidance}
median avoidance $\geq 0.60$; \texttt{wisdom\_problem\_alignment}
we implemented via mean cosine $\geq 0.05$ AND $\geq 3$ pids
above mean). The fully-implemented gate then produces:

\begin{center}\small
\begin{tabular}{l c c}
  \toprule
  Gate & PASS count & Discrimination \\
  \midrule
  Agent-designed, fully implemented & \textbf{12/12} & \textbf{none} \\
  Researcher-designed (Exp 15, trigger-conditioned) & 4/12 & meaningful \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\emph{Every candidate passes every component of the agent's
gate.} The agent's four axes
(reframe-depth, content-delta, wisdom-alignment, antipattern
avoidance) all measure properties that any retrieved wisdom
trivially exhibits on the holdout: adding W to the library does
perturb reasoning (large content-delta for all), the wisdom
unpacked text does share some vocabulary with problems (non-zero
alignment), and anti-patterns are mostly absent from answers by
default. Without the trigger-conditioning split
(SHOULD\_FIRE vs NO\_FIRE), orthogonality alone collapses into
``yes everything differs in some way.''

\paragraph{This is the actually interesting architectural
lesson.} Orthogonal decomposition is \emph{necessary but not
sufficient}. The researcher's gate (Exp 15) adds a separate
ingredient the agent did not discover: \emph{conditioning on an
externally-supplied trigger class} so that utility and
citability are computed only on the subset where the wisdom
claims to apply. That conditioning is what turns ``can measure
4 things'' into ``can discriminate 4-from-12 candidates.''
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
  \item \textbf{Agent capability confirmed}: propose
    orthogonal components, implement them.
  \item \textbf{Agent capability missing}: propose
    \emph{conditioning} — the architectural move of splitting
    the evaluation population by a wisdom-specific trigger.
  \item \textbf{Researcher contribution that was not
    transferable}: the idea that some candidates are
    \emph{conditional specialists}, not uniform activators,
    and the gate must evaluate them in-class.
\end{itemize}
Teaching the agent \emph{conditioning} as a first-class design
move — not just ``decompose'' — is the next L5 experiment.




\section{Multi-step research-task pilot (full)}
\label{app:multistep}

The 100-problem benchmark exercised throughout \S\ref{sec:experiments}
is fundamentally \emph{single-advisory}: one problem, one answer,
one judge verdict. Many research and engineering workflows, however,
demand \emph{multi-round} assumption-revision: propose hypotheses,
gather evidence, update beliefs, re-propose. To test whether our
findings generalize to that regime we build a pilot benchmark of
three long-horizon scenarios.

\paragraph{Benchmark.} Each scenario specifies a \emph{briefing}
(visible to the agent), a \emph{hidden ground-truth mechanism}, a
list of \emph{critical facts} the environment may reveal on demand,
and a list of \emph{plausible red-herrings} also consistent with
surface observations. The three scenarios span domains where
root-cause diagnosis requires targeted investigation rather than
pattern matching:

\begin{center}\small
\begin{tabular}{l l p{4.5cm}}
  \toprule
  Scenario & Domain & Ground-truth root cause (hidden) \\
  \midrule
  Clinical trial & Medicine &
    Drug--drug interaction with warfarin (CYP2C9), amplified by
    chronic kidney disease \\
  ML CTR regression & ML engineering &
    Feature-store schema migration silently renamed a high-importance
    categorical column; serving stack continues to read the old
    column and gets \(0\) \\
  DB slowdown & Systems &
    Postgres minor-version upgrade changed planner statistics;
    a hot query regressed from \texttt{IndexScan} to \texttt{SeqScan} \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\paragraph{Oracle and judge.} To avoid the same-family contamination
identified in \S\ref{sec:selfaudit}, the \emph{oracle} (simulating the
experimental environment) is GPT-5.4, the \emph{solver} is
gemini-3-flash, and the \emph{judge} is Claude Opus 4.6
(three different vendor families). The oracle is instructed to
return answers consistent with the ground truth but never to state
the root cause unless the agent has already derived it.

\paragraph{Agent strategies.} Two baselines are compared:
\begin{description}[leftmargin=1.5em, topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt]
  \item[\textbf{single\_pass}:] The solver reads the briefing and
    writes a final diagnosis with no oracle interaction.
  \item[\textbf{belief\_track}:] The solver maintains an explicit
    hypothesis state (\(\leq 3\) hypotheses with explicit
    confidences) across \(3\) rounds. Each round it proposes
    \(1\text{--}3\) targeted investigations; the oracle answers;
    the agent updates hypotheses in light of the answers. A final
    diagnosis is committed after round \(3\).
\end{description}

\paragraph{Result.} On this small benchmark,
\texttt{belief\_track} converges on all three scenarios; the
single-pass baseline misses the mechanism in the ML-regression
scenario:

\begin{center}\small
\begin{tabular}{l c c}
  \toprule
  Scenario & single\_pass & belief\_track \\
  \midrule
  Clinical trial    & CONVERGED & CONVERGED \\
  ML CTR regression & PARTIAL   & \textbf{CONVERGED} \\
  DB slowdown       & CONVERGED & CONVERGED \\
  \midrule
  Total CONVERGED   & \(2/3\)   & \(\mathbf{3/3}\) \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\noindent The ML scenario is the case whose root cause is a
specific, low-prior mechanism (a silent column rename; the model
sees zero variance on a previously high-importance feature). Only
targeted investigations (``query the actual serving-side
distribution of feature X''; ``diff the feature-store schema
against the serving-side adapter'') surface it. \texttt{single\_pass}
returns a plausible but under-specified ``feature-store issue''
verdict; \texttt{belief\_track} queries the oracle with the
specific diagnostic, receives ``\(user\_category\_id = 0\) for
\(100\%\) of inference requests,'' and commits to the correct
mechanism.

\paragraph{Scope.} \(N = 3\) is a pilot. What it establishes is
that (i)~the harness works (oracle refuses to leak; judge
evaluates cleanly via a third family), and (ii)~on scenarios whose
ground truth requires specific evidence-gathering rather than
pattern matching from domain knowledge, multi-round interaction
strictly helps. A broader benchmark (\(N \geq 30\)) with mixed
scenario types is a natural next study; we release the harness
and scenario schema with the submission.
