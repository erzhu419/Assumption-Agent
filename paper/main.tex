\begin{abstract}
Large language models improve markedly when given retrieval-augmented
\emph{methodological scaffolding}: reframes of the problem, anti-patterns
to avoid, evaluation criteria, and exemplars of related prior reasoning.
A natural next step is an LLM agent that \emph{curates its own}
methodological wisdom library—proposing, validating, and pruning
entries without a human in the loop. We build and exercise exactly
such a loop, and report a finding that we believe should reshape how
self-improvement results in this literature are read.

\noindent\textbf{The setup.} A strong scaffolded solver (v20,
\(+28\)pp vs. the v16 case-library champion on 100 problems,
\(+76\)pp vs. a budget-matched baseline on a 50-problem held-out set) is
paired with four orchestration modules: a failure generator, a
success distiller, a cross-model distiller, and a Darwinian pruner.
Each candidate wisdom must pass a \(+10\)pp held-out A/B gate to
enter the library.

\noindent\textbf{The inner-loop result looks strong.} Over a single
cycle the library grows \(75 \to 77 \to 58_{\text{active}} +
19_{\text{deprecated}} \to 59_{\text{active}} + 19_{\text{deprecated}}\).
Three new wisdoms are committed (\(W_{076}, W_{077}, W_{078}\))
from disjoint evidence sources; nine candidates are refused;
nineteen stale entries are deprecated. Only \(3/12\) proposals pass
the gate—evidence that the A/B gate is doing real work.

\noindent\textbf{The outer-loop audit breaks the result.} We then
re-judge the same 150 answer pairs (50 held-out problems \(\times\) 3
KEEP decisions) with a different LLM family (Claude Opus 4.6).
\textbf{\(0/3\) KEEP decisions survive cross-family re-judgment:}
\(W_{076}\) falls from \(0.64 \to 0.40\), \(W_{077}\) from
\(0.60 \to 0.47\), \(W_{078}\) from \(0.60 \to 0.51\). Every single
KEEP flips to REVERT under the cross-family judge. A self-rebuttal
step, run \emph{before} the cross-judge was invoked, had already
predicted this: the agent returned \texttt{further\_test} (never
\texttt{accept}) on all three KEEPs, naming judge-preference
alignment as the strongest confound. The cross-judge confirmed the
rebuttal.

\noindent\textbf{The take-away.} Single-family self-adjudication is
not enough. An agent can be simultaneously (i)~honest about its gate
(9/12 REVERTs, disjoint evidence sources, auditable logs),
(ii)~successful by its own measurement, and (iii)~wrong in a way that
only becomes visible once a separate-family judge re-scores the
identical text. We argue cross-family re-judgment should become a
routine reporting item for any self-improving LLM loop, not a
stretch goal.

\noindent\textbf{We then close the recursion.} A final experiment
(\texttt{auto\_recurse.py}, \S\ref{sec:recursion}) lets the agent
chain audit$\,\to\,$belief-update$\,\to\,$gate-proposal$\,\to\,$re-audit
by itself. It converges in three cycles to a strictly stricter
fixed-point gate
(\texttt{gemini\_wr$\geq$0.60 AND claude\_wr$\geq$0.55}) and, under
that gate, \emph{withdraws every one of its own previous commits}.

\noindent\textbf{We then pull the original plan back in.} A staged
validation funnel (pilot at $n{=}10$, comparative at $n{=}50$,
cross-domain, cross-family) run retrospectively on all $12$
candidates produces $12 \to 4 \to 0 \to 0 \to 0$: zero candidates
survive all stages. The unexpected result is that even \emph{same
judge family, same content, different side-randomization seed}
suffices to flip every single original KEEP decision. The gate is
fragile along at least three independent axes simultaneously:
seed, family, and domain aggregation.

\noindent\textbf{Contribution.} The loop, the four-way audit, the
recursion to fixed point, the staged funnel, and the concrete
multi-dimensional failure mode they reveal. A small but concrete
step toward agents that build the tools they think with
\emph{and} notice when their own tools mislead them.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

A large language model asked to ``just solve it'' will typically
underperform a version of itself given a well-matched \emph{cognitive
prosthesis}: a problem reframe, a list of anti-patterns, the memory of
two superficially different but structurally parallel prior cases, and
an evaluation rubric. The gap between the raw solver and the scaffolded
solver is usually attributed to chain-of-thought~\citep{wei2022chain},
retrieval-augmented generation~\citep{lewis2020rag}, self-reflection
and verification~\citep{madaan2023selfrefine, shinn2023reflexion}, or
agentic decomposition~\citep{yao2023react}. We call the artifact that
these methods all point to a \emph{wisdom}: a small piece of
methodological knowledge of the form ``in situations exhibiting pattern
\(P\), the right move is \(M\); the canonical failure mode is \(F\).''

A working \emph{wisdom library} is a retrieval corpus of such entries,
selected per problem, foregrounded in the prompt, and paired with
cross-domain exemplars that show what the wisdom looks like when
instantiated in a specific domain. In our setting (see
\S\ref{sec:method}), we use \(75\) such wisdoms, each authored in the
classical tradition (aphorisms from \emph{Yi Jing}, Munger, Drucker,
Mao, Confucius, folk proverbs, etc.) and unpacked into LLM-legible form.
Over six iterative framework revisions (v13 through v20, see
Figure~\ref{fig:arch}), such a scaffolded solver rises from \(74\%\) to
\(86\%\) win rate against a budget-matched baseline.

The limits of this approach, however, are exactly the limits of human
library curation. Every wisdom that is missed is a residual—a
systematic failure mode the solver walks into repeatedly. Every wisdom
that is wrong becomes dead weight, consuming retrieval slots. And every
wisdom that is \emph{nearly right but poorly phrased} costs the solver
one full pass of the scaffold.

\textbf{This paper asks a direct question: can the agent \emph{itself}
curate its wisdom library?} Concretely, we ask whether an LLM agent,
running under a reasonable scaffold, can:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*), leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Detect} recurrent orientations in its own reasoning that
    a static library has not yet articulated;
  \item \textbf{Propose} new wisdom entries with source, signal,
    unpacking, and exemplars;
  \item \textbf{Filter} proposals against a held-out A/B gate that is
    strict enough to refuse most of them;
  \item \textbf{Prune} stale wisdoms that have stopped activating;
  \item \textbf{Import} orientations that a stronger cross-model
    generator uses but the solver misses;
\end{enumerate}
all \emph{without} a human in the loop.

We answer affirmatively, with qualifications. Our contributions are:

\paragraph{Contribution 1 — A scaffold that amplifies rather than
overfits.} We show that a combined \emph{frame-directive +
problem-rewriting} architecture (v20) produces a \(+28\)pp win rate
against the previous case-library champion (v16) on the training split,
and a \(+76\)pp advantage against a matched-budget baseline on a fully
held-out 50-problem test set. The held-out advantage is \emph{larger}
than the training-split advantage, not smaller.

\paragraph{Contribution 2 — A four-component closed-loop library
agent.} We specify an orchestrator comprising a failure-driven
candidate generator, a success-driven reframing distiller, a
cross-model distiller, and a Darwinian pruner, with a shared held-out
A/B gate. Unlike open-ended self-improvement
schemes~\citep{zelikman2024star, chen2024selfplay}, every
library-modification action is logged with evidence, provenance, and a
gain estimate, producing an auditable evolution trajectory.

\paragraph{Contribution 3 — An honest evolution trajectory.} Over one
evolution cycle, the library moves through states
\(75 \to 77 \to 58_{\text{active}} + 19_{\text{deprecated}} \to
59_{\text{active}} + 19_{\text{deprecated}}\), producing three new
active wisdoms (W076 \emph{Change with the times}, W077 \emph{No
investigation, no right to speak}, W078 \emph{Is it a mule or a
horse—take it out for a test}) from disjoint evidence sources.
\emph{Only 3 of 12 candidates pass}: the gate is doing real work.

\paragraph{Contribution 4 — Cross-family re-judgment \emph{breaks}
single-family self-adjudication.} This is the result we want this
paper to be read for. After a clean inner-loop cycle that produces
three KEEPs under a \(+10\)pp held-out gate judged by
\emph{gemini-3-flash}, we re-judge the exact same \(3 \times 50\)
answer pairs using \emph{Claude Opus 4.6} (Anthropic). Under the
cross-family judge, \emph{every single KEEP flips}: \(W_{076}\) goes
from wr\(= 0.64\) to \(0.40\); \(W_{077}\) from \(0.60\) to \(0.47\);
\(W_{078}\) from \(0.60\) to \(0.51\). This is not a subtle
numerical drift; it is a complete failure of the inner-loop gate to
produce decisions that are stable under a disjoint judge family.
The symmetry of the finding (\(3/3\) flips, not \(1/3\) or
\(2/3\)) is the evidence: this is systematic, not statistical.

\paragraph{Contribution 5 — The agent's self-rebuttal step had
already predicted this.} Before we invoked the cross-family judge,
a self-rebuttal step asked GPT-5.4 to produce the three strongest
counter-hypotheses for each KEEP and a final verdict. On all three
KEEPs the verdict was \texttt{further\_test} (never
\texttt{accept}), and the \#1-ranked confound for all three was
\emph{judge-preference alignment}: ``the judge's taste for
`investigate-first', `compare-explicitly', `staged migration' is a
well-attested bias; a wisdom that \emph{activates such phrasings}
can win without improving substance.'' Cross-judge Experiment~1 is
the direct falsifier of this prediction, and it confirmed it.

\paragraph{Contribution 6 — A meta-wisdom about the gate itself.}
A separate self-audit step, shown only the log of past KEEP/REVERT
decisions, diagnoses the gate as too strict in the \([0.54, 0.62]\)
gray zone (where a single pair-flip moves wr by \(0.02\)) and
proposes a falsifiable three-tier replacement:
\texttt{wr} \(\geq 0.64 \Rightarrow\) auto KEEP, \texttt{wr} \(\leq 0.52
\Rightarrow\) auto REVERT, \([0.53, 0.63] \Rightarrow\) extend to
\(n = 100\) or run a second independent A/B. The agent supplies its
own falsifier for this meta-wisdom. Combined with Contribution 5,
this is evidence that the loop has genuine introspective bandwidth
\emph{separate from} its tendency to self-congratulate on the
object-level A/B.

\paragraph{Contribution 7 — A study of failure modes.} We report
where the closed loop breaks at the object level:
(i)~failure-driven candidates never fire in our run because the
solver's residuals are too idiosyncratic at \(N = 50\);
(ii)~cross-model distillation produces only one candidate with
borderline novelty (\(0.75\)); (iii)~the Darwinian pruner deprecates
\(25\%\) of the library on a single scan, which may reflect
over-aggressive default thresholds. These are actionable, not
failures-as-marketing.

\textbf{Scope and non-claims.} We do not claim the agent invents new
\emph{concepts}: every committed wisdom is still rooted in a
human-authored aphorism with a verifiable source. We claim the agent
\emph{discovers which aphorisms its own solver is missing}, a weaker
but more tractable target. We also do not claim monotonic forever-up
performance; see \S\ref{sec:discussion} for a tempered reading.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Retrieval-augmented and in-context methods.} Chain-of-thought
prompting~\citep{wei2022chain} established that surfacing explicit
reasoning helps; self-consistency~\citep{wang2023selfcons} stabilized
it; reflection/verification~\citep{madaan2023selfrefine,
shinn2023reflexion} closed a one-step inner loop. RAG over document
stores~\citep{lewis2020rag, borgeaud2022retro} and agentic
tool-use~\citep{yao2023react, schick2024toolformer} extended the input
context. Our scaffold (v20) is a retrieval-augmented method over a
\emph{methodological} corpus rather than a factual one.

\paragraph{Self-improvement of language models.}
STaR~\citep{zelikman2022star, zelikman2024star}, self-rewarding
LMs~\citep{yuan2024selfrewarding}, self-play fine-tuning and
instruction-backtranslation~\citep{chen2024selfplay, li2024backtrans}
all close an outer loop at the \emph{weights} level. We close an outer
loop at the \emph{retrieval corpus} level, which is orders of magnitude
cheaper and allows the changes to be auditable.

\paragraph{Case-based reasoning and skill libraries.} Case-based
reasoning~\citep{aamodt1994cbr} and skill-library
agents~\citep{wang2023voyager, zhu2023ghost, sumers2024agentkb} both
maintain a growing store of reusable units. Voyager~\citep{wang2023voyager}
grows a skill library from procedural success in Minecraft; our
setting grows a \emph{methodological} library from A/B-judged open
reasoning. AgentKB~\citep{sumers2024agentkb} focuses on task-specific
routines, whereas we target \emph{domain-general orientations}
(\(\leq 35\)-character aphorisms with cross-domain exemplars).

\paragraph{Curriculum and program synthesis of thoughts.} Automated
discovery of reasoning structures via Self-Discover
\citep{zhou2024selfdiscover} generates per-problem module stacks; Tree
of Thoughts~\citep{yao2023tot} branches the search. Our library
\emph{outlives} any single problem and is acted on by the solver at
retrieval time, not at generation time. We view this as a complementary
axis: one can always add orientation-level retrieval on top of a
better-synthesized per-problem structure.

\paragraph{Darwinian / evolutionary approaches.} Evolutionary prompt
optimization~\citep{promptbreeder, fernando2023promptbreeder} uses a
selection signal on prompts themselves. We share the Darwinian framing
but select at the \emph{unit-of-retrieval} granularity (one wisdom),
which allows finer-grained credit assignment and interpretable audit.

\paragraph{Cross-model distillation.} Using a stronger model as a
teacher is classical~\citep{hinton2015distillation} and modern at the
instruction-tuning level~\citep{taori2023alpaca, peng2024evolinstruct}.
Our \emph{cross-LLM distiller} (\S\ref{sec:cross-llm}) distills not
answers but \emph{orientations}: what the stronger model's
answer-structure foregrounds that the weaker model's scaffolding does
not.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Preliminaries: the scaffolded solver (v20)}
\label{sec:solver}

We use \emph{gemini-3-flash} as the underlying
generator\footnote{Exposed via the ruoli.dev proxy; our pipeline does
not depend on any property specific to this model.} and an independent
LLM judge (same family) to produce A/B verdicts. A \textbf{wisdom}
\(w\) is a record
\[
  w = (\text{id}, \text{aphorism}, \text{source}, \text{signal},
      \text{unpacked\_for\_llm}, \text{cross\_domain\_examples}).
\]
At inference time a retriever selects the top \(K=2\) wisdoms for
problem \(p\). The solver then runs a three-turn procedure:

\begin{description}[leftmargin=1.5em, topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt]
  \item[\textbf{Turn 0 (frame + rewrite).}] A single JSON call produces
  \((\text{frame}, \text{critical\_reframe}, \text{anti\_patterns},
  \text{evaluation\_criteria}, \text{rewritten\_problem},
  \text{what\_changed})\). The ``rewritten problem'' is the
  subject-of-attention the solver will then see.
  \item[\textbf{Turn 1 (execute).}] The solver is given a PRIMARY FRAME
  block (not a ``context bag''), the rewritten problem, retrieved
  wisdoms with per-wisdom same-domain + cross-domain exemplars, and an
  attention-prior block derived from the problem domain/difficulty
  cluster.
  \item[\textbf{Turn 2 (audit).}] A brief self-reflection pass asks
  whether the draft violates the frame's anti-patterns or fails its
  evaluation criteria, and patches accordingly.
\end{description}

\paragraph{Why this matters for the library loop.} Turn 0 produces a
log of \texttt{what\_changed} fields—the solver's own articulation,
per problem, of ``what is this problem really about.'' These are the
signals the \emph{success distiller} (\S\ref{sec:success}) mines.

\subsection{The library as mutable state}
\label{sec:registry}

The library is a versioned registry whose entries track
\texttt{status} \(\in \{\text{active, deprecated, removed}\}\),
\texttt{activation\_count}, \texttt{last\_activated},
\texttt{contribution\_gain}, \texttt{source}, and a \texttt{keep\_reason}
string. Every mutation bumps a semantic version
(\texttt{v20.0} \(\to\) \texttt{v20.1} \(\to \ldots\)). The registry
is the ground truth an auditor would check to verify that an alleged
library change was actually made against a specific A/B outcome.

\subsection{Closed-loop orchestrator}
\label{sec:orchestrator}

Figure~\ref{fig:loop} shows the four-component loop. Each batch of
problems flows through the solver; the signals captured are: judge
verdicts (for failure mining), Turn-0 reframes (for success mining),
retrieval selections (for pruning), and cross-variant gaps (for
cross-model mining).

  \caption{Library evolution over one closed-loop cycle. The library
    moves from \(75\) original wisdoms to \(77\) (adding W076 and W077
    from the success distiller), is pruned to \(58\) active +
    \(19\) deprecated, and finally grows to \(59\) active with the

\paragraph{Candidate generation.} Three generators run asynchronously
against a shared buffer of recent signals:

\subsubsection{Success distiller}
\label{sec:success}

Given \(N\) recent Turn-0 outputs, we embed the concatenation
\texttt{what\_changed || critical\_reframe} with a multilingual
sentence encoder~\citep{reimers2019sbert} and apply agglomerative
clustering with cosine-distance threshold \(0.4\) and minimum cluster
size \(6\). For each surviving cluster, a GPT-class prompter reads
\(\leq 10\) cluster members plus the current library summary and
either emits \texttt{\{"proposal": null, "reason": ...\}} or a complete
wisdom record with rationale and cluster-coverage PIDs. We measure
novelty by maximum cosine similarity of the proposed
\texttt{unpacked\_for\_llm} embedding against all active entries; we
reject when \(\text{sim} > 0.78\). The rationale: wisdom candidates
are \emph{latent orientations in the solver's own reasoning}, surfaced
by repetition, not by failure.

\subsubsection{Failure generator}
\label{sec:failure}

A symmetric generator listens to judge verdicts and accumulates
problems where the solver lost or tied into a failure buffer. When the
buffer size passes a threshold, a prompter asks: ``do these
\(\geq 3\) problems share a systematic residual pattern a new wisdom
could address?'' This generator is conservative; in our run it never
fired a proposal (buffer grew \(4 \to 9 \to 11 \to 13\); threshold
\(15\)). We treat this as informative and discuss it in
\S\ref{sec:discussion}.

\subsubsection{Cross-LLM distiller}
\label{sec:cross-llm}

For each problem where the solver's answer lost to a comparison
variant's answer by a judge margin \(\geq 1\), a stronger generator
(GPT-5.4, accessed through the same proxy) independently re-solves the
problem. A fresh judge compares our original answer against the
stronger generator's answer. Problems where the stronger generator
\emph{also} wins by \(\geq 1\) are kept. A single distillation prompt
is then shown all surviving
\(\text{(problem, our answer, stronger answer, both judge reasonings)}\)
tuples, and asked to name a single recurring orientation that the
stronger generator's structure foregrounds but the scaffolding does
not. The output is subject to the same novelty gate as the success
distiller.

\subsubsection{Darwinian pruner}
\label{sec:prune}

The pruner scans every available retrieval-selection file and counts
how often each wisdom appeared in the top-\(K\) for some problem.
Active wisdoms with zero activations across the scan are marked
\texttt{deprecated}; already-deprecated wisdoms with zero activations
on a subsequent scan are \texttt{removed}. A \emph{newborn protection}
rule exempts wisdoms created after the newest scan file's
modification time.

\subsection{The A/B validation gate}
\label{sec:gate}

Every proposed candidate is run through the same gate:
\begin{enumerate}[topsep=2pt,itemsep=2pt,leftmargin=*]
  \item Mine \(3\) cross-domain exemplars from the held-out \(50\) using
    a separate GPT-class prompter (deterministic selection from
    \(\leq 30\) candidates).
  \item Build \(\text{lib}_{\text{ext}} = \text{lib}_{\text{base}}
    \cup \{w_{\text{new}}\}\).
  \item Run v20 with both libraries on all \(50\) held-out problems,
    in parallel.
  \item Judge \texttt{ext} vs. \texttt{base} with side-of-presentation
    randomized by a per-PID seed.
  \item KEEP iff \(\text{wr}_{\text{ext}} \geq 0.50 + 0.10\); otherwise
    REVERT.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent The \(+10\)pp threshold is intentionally strict—it refuses
candidates that are right-on-average but undistinguishable from
sampling noise (\(50\) pairs; a one-sample flip changes wr by \(0.02\)).
Six parallel v20 subprocesses and eight parallel judge threads bring
the total wall time for \(11\) candidates + \(1\) base from
\({\sim}6\) hours to \({\sim}1\) hour \emph{without compromising the
evaluation}.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\paragraph{Benchmark.} \(100\) problems for training (\texttt{sample\_100}),
\(50\) disjoint problems for held-out evaluation
(\texttt{sample\_holdout\_50}). Domains: mathematics, science,
engineering, software\_engineering, business, daily\_life. Difficulty:
easy/medium/hard. All problems are open-ended
(\(\geq 200\)-word solutions), judged pairwise by the same independent
LLM judge family.

\paragraph{Baselines.}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*, topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt]
  \item \texttt{baseline\_long}: solver with full token budget, no
    scaffolding.
  \item \texttt{phase2\_v13\_reflect}: case-library + Turn-2 reflection
    (first workable scaffold).
  \item \texttt{phase2\_v16}: case-library + audit (prior champion;
    \(0.86\) vs. \texttt{baseline\_long}).
  \item \texttt{phase2\_v19b}: problem-rewriting only (no frame block).
  \item \texttt{phase2\_v20} (ours): combined
    frame-directive + problem-rewriting.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Measurement.} All experiments report pairwise win rates.
\(N=100\) on \texttt{sample\_100} and \(N=50\) on
\texttt{sample\_holdout\_50}. Standard error
\(\sigma \approx \sqrt{p(1-p)/N}\); for \(p=0.6, N=50\) this is
\(\approx 0.07\), which informs our \(+10\)pp threshold choice.

\subsection{Architecture ratchet: v13 \(\to\) v20}
\label{sec:ratchet}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figs/fig1_architecture_progression.png}
  \caption{\textbf{(a)} Win rate of each scaffolded variant against
    \texttt{baseline\_long} on the training split. v16 reaches \(0.86\)
    with case library + audit. \textbf{(b)} Post-v16 experiments
    against v16 directly. v17/v18/v19d regress;
    v19a (frame-directive), v19b (problem-rewriting), and v19c
    (per-object + per-paradigm) separately give modest gains. v20,
    their superset, achieves \(0.64\) vs. v16—a \(+28\)pp break of
    the prior ceiling.}
  \label{fig:arch}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:arch} shows the architecture-level ratchet. Two
observations bear on the central claim of this paper:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{The scaffolding \emph{space} is not saturated}:
    between v16 and v20 we discover a \(+28\)pp improvement by
    combining two independent Turn-0 interventions (frame-directive and
    problem-rewriting). This alone justifies the scaffold-level
    research agenda.
  \item \textbf{The scaffolding \emph{surface} is non-monotonic}: v17,
    v18, v19d all fail to beat v16. Of the seven post-v16 variants,
    three regress below parity. This is exactly the setting in which an
    \emph{autonomous gate} (\S\ref{sec:gate}) is valuable: half the
    plausible modifications are net-negative.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Held-out generalization (amplifying, not degrading)}
\label{sec:holdout}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.75\linewidth]{figs/fig2_v20_generalization.png}
  \caption{v16 evaluated on the \(100\) training problems (light blue)
    and on \(50\) held-out problems (green). Against
    \texttt{baseline\_long}: \(0.86 \to 0.88\). Against the
    next-strongest scaffold (v13\_reflect): \(0.62 \to 0.82\).}
  \label{fig:holdout}
\end{figure}

A standard worry: the scaffold overfits to the training-split LLM
judge. Figure~\ref{fig:holdout} rebuts this. On held-out problems the
advantage over \texttt{baseline\_long} \emph{grows} by \(2\)pp; the
advantage over v13\_reflect \emph{grows by \(20\)pp}. We read this as
evidence that the scaffold's contribution is a property of the input,
not of the specific 100-problem judge fingerprint.

\subsection{Autonomous library evolution}
\label{sec:evolution}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figs/fig4_candidate_distribution.png}
  \caption{Success-distilled candidates A/B'd against
    \texttt{library}$_{v20.1}$ on the held-out 50. The KEEP threshold
    (green dashed, \(+10\)pp) is strict: \(2/11\) pass. Bars are colored
    green if KEEP and red if REVERT. Names are the winning aphorisms.}
  \label{fig:distribution}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:distribution} shows the wr\(_{\text{ext}}\) distribution
across \(11\) deduplicated success-distilled candidates. Two pass
(\(\mathrm{W}_{076}\) \emph{凡益之道，与时偕行} [``Change with the times'']
at \(0.64\); \(\mathrm{W}_{077}\) \emph{没有调查，就没有发言权} [``No
investigation, no right to speak''] at \(0.60\)). Nine fall between
\(0.46\) and \(0.58\)—close to parity, honestly noisy, correctly
refused.

\paragraph{Cross-LLM result (W078).} We run the cross-LLM distiller on
\(6\) v20-vs-v16 hard losses. The stronger generator (GPT-5.4) re-solves
all six; the A/B judge confirms a widened gap in only \(2\), both
mathematical. A single distillation pass names the recurring
orientation \emph{是骡子是马，拉出来遛遛} (``Is it a mule or a
horse—take it out for a test''): a \emph{bake-off orientation} that
builds a candidate set and compares against explicit metrics rather
than committing early. Novelty similarity is \(0.75\) (below the
\(0.78\) gate). The candidate is A/B'd against the
post-prune library and passes at \(\text{wr}_{\text{ext}} = 0.60\),
exactly on the \(+10\)pp threshold. Committed as W078 with
\texttt{source=cross\_llm}.

exempted by the newborn-protection rule. No wisdom reached the
``removed'' state on this first scan.

\subsection{Ablation: does each component carry weight?}
\label{sec:ablation}

We did not re-run the full cycle with each component disabled (the
scaffold alone costs \({\sim}20\) min/variant on \(50\) problems even
with \(6\times\) parallelism). Instead we inspect the \emph{sources of
the KEEP set} and the \emph{overlap of evidence}:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
  \item W076, W077 come from clusters of sizes \(9\) and \(6\)
    respectively; their evidence is \(\{pid\}\) from
    \(\{\text{business, engineering, software\_engineering}\}\).
  \item W078 comes from mathematical hard-losses; its evidence is
    \(\{\mathrm{mathematics\_0243, mathematics\_0081}\}\).
  \item The pruner's 19 deprecations include \(14\) wisdoms that
    \emph{no retrieval file has ever selected into top-2}—i.e., they
    were never active under any variant's selection logic, not just
    v20's. This is strong evidence the pruner is removing genuine dead
    weight.
\end{itemize}

\noindent Evidence sets are disjoint by construction (success from
Turn-0 logs; cross-LLM from hard-loss judgments). This is the minimum
test for complementarity across sources, and it passes.

\subsection{Recursive self-audit: three experiments}
\label{sec:selfaudit}

The loop as described so far \emph{proposes hypotheses and
self-adjudicates} via a held-out A/B gate. But two concerns remain:
(i)~solver and gate judge share the same LLM family, which could mask
judge-preference confounds rather than measure methodological lift,
and (ii)~the gate itself is never inspected for systematic bias. We
run three experiments that give the agent one additional level of
reflection.

\paragraph{Experiment 1 — Cross-judge stability (the central
result of this paper).} The three KEEP decisions are re-judged on
the \emph{same 150 answer pairs} by \emph{Claude Opus 4.6}, accessed
through a proxy that serves multiple vendors so the plumbing is
identical. Results:
\begin{center}\small
\begin{tabular}{l r r c l}
  \toprule
  & \multicolumn{2}{c}{Win rate of ext} & & \\
  \cmidrule(lr){2-3}
  ID & gemini-3-flash & Claude Opus 4.6 & $\Delta$ & Agreement \\
  \midrule
  W076 & 0.64 ($32 : 18$) & \textbf{0.40} ($18 : 27 :  5_{\text{tie}}$)
    & $-0.24$ & \textbf{FLIP} \\
  W077 & 0.60 ($30 : 20$) & \textbf{0.47} ($19 : 21 : 10_{\text{tie}}$)
    & $-0.13$ & \textbf{FLIP} \\
  W078 & 0.60 ($30 : 20$) & \textbf{0.51} ($23 : 22 :  5_{\text{tie}}$)
    & $-0.09$ & \textbf{FLIP} \\
  \midrule
  \multicolumn{4}{r}{Cross-judge STABLE fraction:} &
  \textbf{0 / 3} \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\noindent \emph{The exact same 50 ext-answer strings and 50 base-answer
strings are being compared in both columns}—only the judging
family changes. Every KEEP flips. The magnitude ranges from
\emph{collapse} ($W_{076}$: $0.64 \to 0.40$) to \emph{narrow flip}
($W_{078}$: $0.60 \to 0.51$), but the \emph{direction} is
consistent: a judge from outside the family downgrades every
KEEP.

Interpretation: the inner-loop A/B gate is measuring
\emph{gemini-3-flash's preference for answers that activate
certain rhetorical structures} (``staged migration,'' ``diagnose
first,'' ``bake-off before choosing''), which the three KEEPs
specifically encourage. A new library entry that \emph{activates
those phrasings} wins the intra-family A/B without improving
methodological substance. This is exactly the confound
Experiment~2's self-rebuttal named first, unprompted.

The consequence for self-improving-LLM papers is stark: a
$+N$pp improvement reported with a same-family judge is, until
further notice, a statement about judge-preference alignment, not
methodological uplift. We believe cross-family re-judgment of the
\emph{identical answer pairs} is the cheapest meaningful
falsification test one can bolt onto any such loop.

\paragraph{Experiment 2 — Self-rebuttal.} For each KEEP, GPT-5.4 is
given the wisdom record, the 50-pair A/B evidence, and told to
produce the three strongest counter-hypotheses and a final verdict
(\texttt{accept} / \texttt{further\_test} / \texttt{reject}). The
agent returns \texttt{further\_test} on all three (no
\texttt{accept}s), citing three high-severity confound classes that
recur verbatim across cases:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, topsep=2pt, itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Judge-preference (3/3 cases).} The judge's taste for
    ``investigate first,'' ``compare explicitly,'' ``staged
    migration'' is a well-attested bias; a wisdom that \emph{activates
    such phrasings} can win without improving substance.
  \item \textbf{Held-out distribution match (3/3 cases).} If the 50
    held-out problems happen to over-represent domains where the
    wisdom's trigger condition holds, the measured \(+10\)pp is a
    sample-coverage effect, not a general uplift.
  \item \textbf{Library-interaction (3/3 cases).} The ext library
    contains all base wisdoms plus one; behavioural changes may come
    from retrieval-order shifts, not the new entry per se.
\end{enumerate}
These are exactly the confounds a skeptical human reviewer would
raise. The system predicts them about itself. We see this as a
strength for paper-writing and a concrete roadmap for a follow-up
cycle (stratify test set by triggering conditions; ablate one-wisdom-at-a-time
retrieval; cross-judge).

\paragraph{Experiment 3 — Meta-wisdom about the gate.} The agent is
shown all twelve validation rows (\(9\) REVERT, \(3\) KEEP) and asked
to diagnose systematic bias in the gate itself. It identifies, from
the wr distribution alone, four patterns:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, topsep=2pt, itemsep=0pt]
  \item Nine REVERTs cluster in \([0.46, 0.58]\); six of them are
    within \(1\) pair-flip of the threshold.
  \item \texttt{wr} \(= 0.58\) (\(29\) : \(21\)) and \texttt{wr} \(= 0.60\)
    (\(30 : 20\)) differ by \emph{one pair} but span the life-death
    boundary.
  \item All three KEEPs are at the right tail
    (\(0.60, 0.60, 0.64\)).
  \item The full distribution is right-skewed: \(10/12\) candidates
    have \texttt{wr} \(\geq 0.51\), yet the gate kills everything
    \(< 0.60\).
\end{enumerate}
From these observations the agent proposes a meta-wisdom
\emph{``临界一票，不定生死''} (``One critical vote must not decide
life and death'') with a concrete gate modification:
\begin{quote}\small
\texttt{wr >= 0.64} \(\Rightarrow\) auto KEEP; \texttt{wr <= 0.52}
\(\Rightarrow\) auto REVERT; \textbf{\(0.53 \leq\) wr} \(\leq 0.63
\Rightarrow\) extend to \(n = 100\) or run a second independent A/B;
in all cases, report confidence intervals rather than a single
point estimate.
\end{quote}
It also produces its own falsifier:
\begin{quote}\small
``If gray-zone candidates remain \(< 0.55\) after extension to \(n
\geq 100\), and current KEEPs hold \(\geq 0.60\) on re-test, then the
threshold is not too strict—the candidates are genuinely weak, and I
will abandon this meta-wisdom.''
\end{quote}

\noindent This is, to our knowledge, the first time a library-evolution
loop has produced an auditable, testable hypothesis
\emph{about its own decision procedure}, stated at exactly the level
of abstraction needed to modify the procedure. We do not automatically
apply the meta-wisdom—its correct use is to run the falsifier in
the next cycle. What we show is that the loop has the introspective
bandwidth to name its own failure mode without external prompting
beyond the log of its own past choices.

\paragraph{How these experiments move the self-adjudication claim.}
Before these experiments, the paper could at best claim
\emph{single-judge self-adjudication inside one LLM family}.
Experiments 1--3 together let us say, concretely:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*, topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt]
  \item \textbf{Cross-family judge agreement} is measurable
    (Experiment 1) and should become a routine reporting item;
  \item \textbf{Self-predicted failure modes} are specific and
    useful—the system's rebuttal verdicts \emph{agree with the
    criticisms a human reviewer would raise}, not a rubber-stamp
    (Experiment 2);
  \item \textbf{Meta-level self-hypothesis} is generatable: given a
    log of past gate behaviour, the agent can propose a
    \emph{falsifiable} modification to the gate itself (Experiment 3).
\end{itemize}
But the three audit steps above are still \emph{human-triggered
snapshots}. The next subsection closes the recursion.

\subsection{Experiment 4 — Recursion demonstrated: the agent runs
its own audit-update-reaudit loop to fixed point}
\label{sec:recursion}

Experiments 1--3 each produce useful signal in isolation. To claim
\emph{recursive self-validation} we need the system to
\emph{itself} chain them: audit, update belief, propose a new
gate, apply the new gate, re-audit under it, and decide whether
belief has converged. We implement this as a single script,
\texttt{auto\_recurse.py}, parameterised by
\texttt{max\_cycles}. Each cycle is:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt]
  \item Apply the current gate to the \(12\) validation rows; take
    the resulting \texttt{KEEPs\_t}.
  \item For every \(cid \in \texttt{KEEPs\_t}\) whose cross-family
    score is missing, invoke Claude Opus 4.6 to score the \(50\)
    answer pairs.
  \item Update per-wisdom confidence by a smoothed average of the
    two families' \(\text{wr}\) scores.
  \item Ask GPT-5.4 (with the full two-judge evidence table and
    current gate as context) whether the gate should be tightened;
    accept a new gate iff it is strictly more conservative.
  \item Re-apply the (possibly new) gate; if the gate is unchanged
    from the previous cycle, declare fixed point.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent The run (seeded with Experiment 1's Claude scores for the
three original KEEPs) converges in \textbf{three cycles}:

\begin{center}\small
\begin{tabular}{c l l r r}
  \toprule
  Cycle & Gate in effect & Proposed next gate & \(|\)KEEPs\(|\)
    & Action \\
  \midrule
  1 & \texttt{gemini\_wr>=0.60} &
      \texttt{...AND claude\_wr>=0.50} & \(3\) & tighten \\
  2 & \texttt{gemini>=0.60 AND claude>=0.50} &
      \texttt{...AND claude\_wr>=0.55} & \(1\) & tighten \\
  3 & \texttt{gemini>=0.60 AND claude>=0.55} &
      (unchanged) & \(0\) & \textbf{fixed point} \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\noindent The agent's reasoning at each step (paraphrased from the
GPT-5.4 output with numerics preserved):

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*, topsep=2pt, itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Cycle 1 rationale:} ``\(3/12\) pass the single-judge
    gate, but Claude's re-judgment shows only \(1\) reaches
    \(0.50\)—single-family gate mislabels; require agreement.''
  \item \textbf{Cycle 2 rationale:} ``The surviving candidate
    (WCROSSL01, \emph{是骡子是马}) only reaches Claude
    \(\text{wr} = 0.51\); safety margin too thin; tighten to
    \(0.55\).''
  \item \textbf{Cycle 3 rationale:} ``No candidate passes; further
    tightening would be stricter but adds no safety given zero
    current survivors; hold.''
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Meaning.} This is, as far as we know, the first paper
that demonstrates a closed-loop LLM agent proposing a gate,
testing it with an out-of-family judge, updating its belief,
proposing a \emph{stricter} gate, and terminating at an empirical
fixed point—all without human intervention between cycles. The
fixed-point gate eliminates every candidate the original gate had
approved. Put differently: \emph{the recursion, given the tools it
itself asked for, withdraws its original commits}.

\paragraph{Does this commit the agent to ``no library growth ever
again''?} No. The fixed point is a statement about the current
\(12\) candidates. A future cycle running on a \emph{new} batch of
problems can produce new candidates; those would go through the
same (now-tightened) gate; if one of them passes, the library
grows. The claim here is narrower and more defensible: \emph{under
its own self-proposed, cross-family-aware gate, the agent reverses
every one of its current commits}. This is what recursive
self-validation looks like when it works.

The original Phase-4 plan (see project documentation) specified a
\emph{three-stage} validation pipeline: (1) a cheap pilot on
\(n \approx 10\) problems, (2) the full comparative A/B at
\(n = 50\), and (3) a cross-domain generalization check. The
\(+10\)pp gate used in the main inner loop collapses all three
into a single step, which trades rigor for speed. We now run the
original three-stage gate on every one of our \(12\) candidates,
prepend it with a \emph{side-stability} check (the same gemini
judge, re-rolled), and append Experiment~1's cross-family check as
Stage~4:

\paragraph{The side-stability finding.} The most unexpected result
in this table is the \texttt{F} at Stage 1 for \emph{all three
original KEEPs}. The \emph{same} gemini-3-flash judge, running on
the \emph{same} solver outputs, returns
wr\(_{\text{ext}}\)(W076) \(= 0.41\) on the stage-1 pilot (and
\(0.41\) on the full 50-pair re-run), compared with \(0.64\) in
the original validation run. The only difference between the two
runs is \textbf{the Python process random seed that assigns
each pair's left/right presentation order}. In other words, the
original decision depended on a specific side-randomization
seed: with a different seed, \emph{the same judge family returns
the opposite verdict}. The ext--vs--base content is identical in
both runs; only the ordering of presentation to the judge
differs. This is a widely discussed LLM-judge phenomenon
(\cite{zheng2023judging, wang2024positionbias}), but is rarely
surfaced at the level of individual library-mutation decisions.

\paragraph{Putting cross-family and side-stability together.} The
original KEEP for W076 is fragile in \emph{at least three}
independent dimensions:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=0pt]
  \item \textbf{Side-order sensitivity.} Same judge, same content,
    different presentation: \(0.64 \to 0.41\).
  \item \textbf{Cross-family sensitivity.} Different judge family,
    same content, same presentation: \(0.64 \to 0.40\)
    (Experiment 1).
  \item \textbf{Cross-domain fragility.} Per-domain wr ranges from
    \(0.20\) to \(0.75\); the gate ignores the former and profits
    from the latter.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent Any one of these would have been enough to refuse the
commit. All three fire on the same candidate. The
same-family \(+10\)pp gate is not just one-judge-biased; it is
\emph{seed-biased}, \emph{family-biased}, and
\emph{domain-aggregation-biased}, independently.

\paragraph{The full claim.} Combining the four post-hoc
experiments: the original inner-loop gate's \(3\) KEEPs survive
\(0/5\) independent stability checks (side-stability, cross-family,
cross-domain, pilot, recursion-to-fixed-point). We do not claim the
underlying wisdoms have zero value; we claim the specific
\emph{procedure} that committed them was systematically insufficient
by multiple measurable standards.

\section{Beyond single-advisory benchmarks: a multi-step
research-task pilot}
\label{sec:multistep}

The 100-problem benchmark exercised throughout \S\ref{sec:experiments}
is fundamentally \emph{single-advisory}: one problem, one answer,
one judge verdict. Many research and engineering workflows, however,
demand \emph{multi-round} assumption-revision: propose hypotheses,
gather evidence, update beliefs, re-propose. To test whether our
findings generalize to that regime we build a pilot benchmark of
three long-horizon scenarios.

\paragraph{Benchmark.} Each scenario specifies a \emph{briefing}
(visible to the agent), a \emph{hidden ground-truth mechanism}, a
list of \emph{critical facts} the environment may reveal on demand,
and a list of \emph{plausible red-herrings} also consistent with
surface observations. The three scenarios span domains where
root-cause diagnosis requires targeted investigation rather than
pattern matching:

\begin{center}\small
\begin{tabular}{l l p{4.5cm}}
  \toprule
  Scenario & Domain & Ground-truth root cause (hidden) \\
  \midrule
  Clinical trial & Medicine &
    Drug--drug interaction with warfarin (CYP2C9), amplified by
    chronic kidney disease \\
  ML CTR regression & ML engineering &
    Feature-store schema migration silently renamed a high-importance
    categorical column; serving stack continues to read the old
    column and gets \(0\) \\
  DB slowdown & Systems &
    Postgres minor-version upgrade changed planner statistics;
    a hot query regressed from \texttt{IndexScan} to \texttt{SeqScan} \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\paragraph{Oracle and judge.} To avoid the same-family contamination
identified in \S\ref{sec:selfaudit}, the \emph{oracle} (simulating the
experimental environment) is GPT-5.4, the \emph{solver} is
gemini-3-flash, and the \emph{judge} is Claude Opus 4.6
(three different vendor families). The oracle is instructed to
return answers consistent with the ground truth but never to state
the root cause unless the agent has already derived it.

\paragraph{Agent strategies.} Two baselines are compared:
\begin{description}[leftmargin=1.5em, topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt]
  \item[\textbf{single\_pass}:] The solver reads the briefing and
    writes a final diagnosis with no oracle interaction.
  \item[\textbf{belief\_track}:] The solver maintains an explicit
    hypothesis state (\(\leq 3\) hypotheses with explicit
    confidences) across \(3\) rounds. Each round it proposes
    \(1\text{--}3\) targeted investigations; the oracle answers;
    the agent updates hypotheses in light of the answers. A final
    diagnosis is committed after round \(3\).
\end{description}

\paragraph{Result.} On this small benchmark,
\texttt{belief\_track} converges on all three scenarios; the
single-pass baseline misses the mechanism in the ML-regression
scenario:

\begin{center}\small
\begin{tabular}{l c c}
  \toprule
  Scenario & single\_pass & belief\_track \\
  \midrule
  Clinical trial    & CONVERGED & CONVERGED \\
  ML CTR regression & PARTIAL   & \textbf{CONVERGED} \\
  DB slowdown       & CONVERGED & CONVERGED \\
  \midrule
  Total CONVERGED   & \(2/3\)   & \(\mathbf{3/3}\) \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\noindent The ML scenario is the case whose root cause is a
specific, low-prior mechanism (a silent column rename; the model
sees zero variance on a previously high-importance feature). Only
targeted investigations (``query the actual serving-side
distribution of feature X''; ``diff the feature-store schema
against the serving-side adapter'') surface it. \texttt{single\_pass}
returns a plausible but under-specified ``feature-store issue''
verdict; \texttt{belief\_track} queries the oracle with the
specific diagnostic, receives ``\(user\_category\_id = 0\) for
\(100\%\) of inference requests,'' and commits to the correct
mechanism.

\paragraph{Scope.} \(N = 3\) is a pilot. What it establishes is
that (i)~the harness works (oracle refuses to leak; judge
evaluates cleanly via a third family), and (ii)~on scenarios whose
ground truth requires specific evidence-gathering rather than
pattern matching from domain knowledge, multi-round interaction
strictly helps. A broader benchmark (\(N \geq 30\)) with mixed
scenario types is a natural next study; we release the harness
and scenario schema with the submission.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{The single most important question: do the KEPT wisdoms
have \emph{any} value?} Cross-family re-judgment says ``not as much
as the inner loop thought.'' But it does not say ``zero.'' Claude's
scores are $0.40, 0.47, 0.51$—one collapse and two near-parities.
A future cycle could: (a)~commit wisdoms only if \emph{both} judges
return $\geq 0.55$, turning the gate into an AND; (b)~commit only
when the \emph{judge-family-average} wr
$\bar{\text{wr}} \geq 0.58$; (c)~keep the same gate but require a
second A/B on a completely new held-out split. Any of these would
have rejected all three of our current KEEPs. The library would
still grow—just from a smaller, more stable candidate set.

\paragraph{What does this mean for the prior literature?} Many
recent reports of self-improving LLM loops—self-rewarding LMs,
self-play fine-tuning, instruction-backtranslation, STaR-style
bootstrap—use a judge (or reward model) from the same family as
the generator. Our experiment does not falsify these directly (they
target the \emph{weights}, not a retrieval library), but it raises a
specific testable question: are the reported gains intra-family
judge-preference alignments or true capability gains? A drop-in
cross-family re-judge, on the \emph{identical} generator outputs
from the paper's most-improved checkpoint, is cheap and decisive.

\paragraph{Why did the failure generator never fire?} The intended
signal was ``judge gave B \(\geq\) 8-point margin, accumulate
\(\geq 3\) such in a cluster.'' In our run judges almost always
separate by \(1\) point; true multi-point gaps are rare
(\(1/50\) in the held-out-vs-baseline judgments). Consequently the
buffer fills slowly. We read this as a property of the underlying
judge, not the buffer design: if a stronger discriminator judge were
used, the failure buffer would fire more often and distinguishably.
This is an empirical claim we can test in future work.

\paragraph{The \(+10\)pp gate is strict by design.} A symmetric
relaxation to \(+5\)pp would have kept \(6/11\) success-distilled
candidates (see Figure~\ref{fig:distribution}); \(+8\)pp would have
kept \(4/11\). We believe the tighter gate is the right setting for
a library intended for long-horizon accumulation: a \(+5\)pp false
positive is a candidate that is almost certainly inside the
\(2\sigma\) noise band of a \(50\)-pair test. At a larger test size
(say \(N = 200\)), the gate could be tightened numerically while
increasing true-positive yield.

\paragraph{Cross-LLM is a narrow lens, not a broad one.} W078's
evidence set is \(\{2\) math problems\(\}\). The distilled orientation
(``bake-off'') is domain-general in principle but empirically emerged
from a narrow source. The held-out A/B verifies generality
(\(\text{wr}_{\text{ext}} = 0.60\) across mixed domains), but this is
exactly the kind of KEEP where a future cycle may override the earlier
decision—which is, in turn, exactly why the registry tracks
\texttt{contribution\_gain} and \texttt{activation\_count} on an
ongoing basis.

\paragraph{Is this a solution or an interesting artifact?} Our honest
reading: this is \emph{one cycle}, not steady-state forever-up. We
showed the loop closes; we did not show it keeps closing for \(K = 10\)
cycles with monotone improvement. Testing the latter requires an
order of magnitude more held-out problems and compute, and is the
natural next study.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\textbf{Single LLM family.} Both solver and judge come from the same
\emph{gemini-3-flash} family through a proxy. A paired
(solver, judge) from the same weights can share biases we cannot
measure from inside the loop. The cross-LLM distiller's use of GPT-5.4
partially probes this; a thorough study would require an
Anthropic/OpenAI solver paired with a Google judge.

\textbf{Small held-out set.} \(50\) problems is enough to distinguish
\(+10\)pp from noise but is not enough to resolve finer gradations or
to study the candidate-gate ROC. We cap the test set at \(50\) to
preserve compute budget for the loop; larger held-out sets are the
first scaling axis.

\textbf{Source-credentialed aphorisms.} Our library entries are
rooted in real human authorship (Confucius, Drucker, Munger, etc.).
The success distiller refuses to fabricate sources when asked. Whether
the loop \emph{could} be run with fully self-authored aphorisms is an
open question and probably deserves its own paper.

\textbf{One evolution cycle.} As noted above, we ran one cycle. A
multi-cycle study with staged held-out splits is the natural
continuation.

\section{Appendix: Experiment 1 per-problem detail}
\label{app:crossjudge}

Full \texttt{\{pid \(\to\) verdict\}} maps for all three KEEP cases
are stored in
\texttt{phase four/autonomous/exp1\_cross\_judge\_log.json} and
regenerated by
\texttt{phase four/exp1\_cross\_judge.py}. The regenerator uses the
\(+\)/- pair-side randomization rule inherited from
\texttt{validate\_parallel.py} so that per-PID side allocation is
identical across both judging families; the only input difference
is the judge family. Problem-level audit: we observe no single
pid that flipped in only one direction—the judges agree on the
direction of individual pairs about as often as they disagree,
but \emph{Claude's tie rate is materially higher} (14\% vs.\ 0\%
for \emph{gemini-3-flash}) and its ext-advantage score is
systematically lower. This is consistent with the hypothesis that
the gemini judge has a sharper preference signal for specific
rhetorical patterns.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We set out to build the simplest honest closed-loop agent that
\emph{proposes} wisdom, \emph{validates} it, \emph{prunes} what
stops being used, and \emph{imports} what a stronger cross-model
generator sees but the solver misses. We did build it, and its
inner loop works: three new active wisdoms, nineteen retired,
\(3/12\) candidates KEPT by a strict \(+10\)pp A/B gate, all
auditable.

Then we ran the cheapest falsification test on our own result: we
re-judged the identical answer pairs with a disjoint-family judge.
\emph{All three KEEPs flipped}, and the agent's own pre-committed
self-rebuttal step had named this exact failure mode first.

What remains true: the loop is honest about its state
(\(9/12\) rejections, disjoint evidence sources, every decision
logged with evidence). What we now know is additionally true: an
honest loop with a same-family gate produces KEEPs that do not
transport to a different-family re-judgment. The discipline going
forward is not to close this loop harder; it is to include
cross-family re-judgment as a first-class reporting item and to
treat a same-family $+N$pp as preliminary until cross-family
re-judgment confirms the direction. Our agent could have told us
this; now we have told the reader.

\section{Compute and cost}
\label{app:compute}

Each v20 subprocess on \(50\) problems: \({\sim}20\) minutes wall time,
\({\sim}\) input \(180\)k tokens, output \(40\)k tokens. Twelve
parallel v20 runs in the validation gate: \({\sim}45\) minutes wall
with \(6\) concurrent workers. Eleven parallel A/B judges (\(50\)
pairs each): \({\sim}12\) minutes with \(8\) concurrent judge threads.
Total for one full success-distill \(\to\) A/B cycle including
exemplar mining: \({\sim}1.5\) hours wall time, within the budget of a
single researcher's afternoon.
